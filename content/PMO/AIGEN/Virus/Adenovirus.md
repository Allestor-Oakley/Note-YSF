
## **Adenoviridae – Human Adenoviruses (HAdVs)**

---

### **1. Introduction**

**Human adenoviruses (HAdVs)** are **non-enveloped, double-stranded DNA viruses** known to cause a wide spectrum of clinical syndromes. Discovered in the 1950s in adenoids, these viruses are significant **pathogens of the respiratory, gastrointestinal, urinary, and ocular tracts**.

Over **120 adenovirus types** have been identified, classified into **7 species (A–G)**, with at least **50+ types infecting humans**. They are capable of both **lytic** and **latent infections**, especially in lymphoid tissues.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Adenoviridae
    
- **Genus**: Mastadenovirus (human adenoviruses)
    
- **Genome**: Linear **double-stranded DNA (~36–38 kb)**
    
- **Structure**:
    
    - **Non-enveloped**, icosahedral symmetry
        
    - ~90–100 nm in size
        
    - Capsid has **240 hexon proteins**, **12 penton bases**, and **fiber proteins**
        

#### **Key Proteins & Functions**

- **Fiber protein**: Mediates cell attachment via **CAR** (coxsackie-adenovirus receptor)
    
- **Penton base**: Facilitates internalization via **integrins (αvβ3, αvβ5)**
    
- **E1A**: Early gene product; transactivates host transcription
    
- **E1B**: Blocks apoptosis (interacts with p53)
    
- **E3 proteins**: Immune evasion (e.g., inhibition of MHC I expression)
    
- **E4**: Involved in DNA replication, RNA processing
    
- **Late genes (L1–L5)**: Encode structural proteins for virion assembly
    

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Entry & Replication**

1. **Attachment**:
    
    - Via fiber protein binding to CAR or CD46 (species-dependent)
        
2. **Internalization**:
    
    - Clathrin-mediated endocytosis via penton-integrin interaction
        
3. **Uncoating & Nuclear Transport**:
    
    - Capsid transported via microtubules to nuclear pore
        
    - DNA released into nucleus
        
4. **Replication Phases**:
    
    - **Early phase (E1–E4)**: Prepares cell for replication, evades immunity
        
    - **DNA replication**
        
    - **Late phase (L1–L5)**: Produces structural proteins
        
5. **Assembly & Release**:
    
    - Virus assembled in nucleus → cell lysis → release of virions
        

#### **B. Latency**

- Some types (e.g., HAdV-5, 12) persist in **adenoids and tonsils**
    
- Reactivation possible in immunosuppressed hosts
    

#### **C. Immune Evasion**

- Inhibits **apoptosis**, **interferon responses**, and **MHC presentation**
    
- Downregulation of NK cell activating ligands
    

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **By Syndrome and Associated Serotypes**

|Syndrome|Common Serotypes|Notes|
|---|---|---|
|**Pharyngitis / Pharyngoconjunctival fever**|3, 4, 7|Common in children, "swimming pool conjunctivitis"|
|**Acute respiratory disease (ARD)**|4, 7, 14, 21|Military recruits, closed populations|
|**Pneumonia**|3, 7, 14, 21|Can be severe in infants, elderly, or immunocompromised|
|**Conjunctivitis / Epidemic keratoconjunctivitis (EKC)**|8, 19, 37|Highly contagious, affects both children and adults|
|**Gastroenteritis**|40, 41 (species F)|Watery diarrhea in children <2 years|
|**Hemorrhagic cystitis**|11, 21|More common in boys and stem cell transplant recipients|
|**Hepatitis**|5, 2 (rare)|In immunocompromised|
|**Meningoencephalitis**|Rare|In immunocompromised, sometimes fatal|
|**Disseminated disease**|Especially in transplant patients|Multiple organs involved|

---

### **5. Complications**

- **Severe pneumonia** (especially in military recruits or immunosuppressed)
    
- **Encephalitis**, **hepatitis**, **nephritis**
    
- **Chronic keratoconjunctivitis** → visual impairment
    
- **Graft rejection** or **organ failure** in transplant recipients
    
- **Death** in disseminated infections if untreated
    

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Laboratory Diagnosis**

- **PCR** (gold standard): Blood, respiratory, conjunctival swab, stool
    
- **Viral culture**: HEp-2 or A549 cells (takes days to weeks)
    
- **Direct antigen detection**: Immunofluorescence or ELISA
    
- **Histopathology**:
    
    - **Intranuclear basophilic inclusions** in infected epithelial cells
        
- **Serology**: Retrospective utility only
    

#### **B. Differential Diagnosis**

|Syndrome|Differentials|
|---|---|
|Pharyngitis|Streptococcus, EBV|
|Conjunctivitis|HSV, enterovirus, chlamydia|
|Pneumonia|RSV, influenza, COVID-19, bacterial pneumonia|
|Gastroenteritis (children)|Rotavirus, norovirus, astrovirus|
|Hemorrhagic cystitis|BK virus, bacterial UTI|
|Disseminated disease|CMV, EBV, fungal or bacterial sepsis|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. General Management**

- Mostly **supportive**:
    
    - Fluids, oxygen, antipyretics
        
    - Eye lubrication or antibiotic drops (for bacterial superinfection)
        

#### **B. Antiviral Therapy** (for severe or immunocompromised)

- **Cidofovir**:
    
    - Nucleotide analog, inhibits viral DNA polymerase
        
    - Nephrotoxic → needs probenecid and hydration
        
- **Brincidofovir (CMX001)**:
    
    - Oral lipid conjugate of cidofovir
        
    - Less toxic, used in clinical trials and emergency settings
        
- **Ribavirin**: Sometimes used (variable efficacy)
    

#### **C. Immunotherapy**

- **IVIG**: May be used as adjunct in severe or disseminated cases
    

---

### **8. Prevention**

#### **A. Hygiene**

- Handwashing, disinfecting surfaces
    
- Avoiding shared towels or eye equipment
    

#### **B. Vaccines**

- **Live, oral vaccine (HAdV-4 and HAdV-7)**:
    
    - Used in **U.S. military recruits only**
        
    - Highly effective in preventing ARD
        

#### **C. Infection Control**

- Isolation of infected individuals
    
- Infection control measures in hospitals, especially transplant wards
