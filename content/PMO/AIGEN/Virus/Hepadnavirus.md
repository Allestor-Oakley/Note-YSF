
### **1. Introduction**

Hepadnaviridae is a family of **partially double-stranded DNA viruses** that infect the liver. The most clinically relevant member is the **Hepatitis B virus (HBV)**, a **major global pathogen** causing **acute and chronic hepatitis**, **cirrhosis**, and **hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC)**. Over **290 million people** are chronically infected worldwide.

HBV exhibits a unique replication strategy involving **reverse transcription** of an RNA intermediate, akin to retroviruses. It is **highly infectious**, transmitted via blood, sexual contact, and perinatally.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Hepadnaviridae
    
- **Genus**: Orthohepadnavirus
    
- **Species**: Hepatitis B virus (human pathogen)
    

**Structure**:

- **Enveloped**, icosahedral capsid (~42 nm "Dane particle")
    
- **Partially double-stranded circular DNA genome (~3.2 kb)**
    
- Three forms seen in serum:
    
    - **Dane particle**: infectious virion
        
    - **Spheres and filaments**: non-infectious HBsAg particles
        

**Key Genes and Proteins**:

- **S (surface gene)** → HBsAg: Major viral envelope protein
    
- **C (core gene)** → HBcAg (core), HBeAg (secreted)
    
- **P (polymerase)** → DNA polymerase with reverse transcriptase, RNase H, and DNA polymerase activities
    
- **X (HBx protein)**: Transactivator protein involved in viral replication and oncogenesis

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Transmission**

- **Perinatal**: Common in endemic areas (vertical transmission)
    
- **Percutaneous**: Needle sharing, transfusion
    
- **Sexual contact**
    
- **Horizontal (childhood contact)**: In highly endemic settings
    

#### **B. Entry and Replication**

1. Entry via **NTCP (sodium taurocholate co-transporting polypeptide)** receptor on hepatocytes
    
2. Release of **relaxed circular DNA (rcDNA)** into nucleus
    
3. Conversion into **covalently closed circular DNA (cccDNA)** — acts as transcriptional template
    
4. Transcription of mRNAs and pregenomic RNA (pgRNA)
    
5. **pgRNA reverse-transcribed** into DNA by viral polymerase
    
6. Assembly in the cytoplasm and secretion or recycling to nucleus
    

#### **C. Immune Response**

- HBV itself is **not cytopathic** — liver injury is **immune-mediated**
    
- **CTLs (CD8+)** destroy infected hepatocytes
    
- **Strong response** → viral clearance
    
- **Weak response** → chronic infection
    
- **HBeAg** modulates immune tolerance in vertical transmission

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **A. Acute HBV Infection**

- Incubation: 1–4 months
    
- Symptoms (in 30–50% adults):
    
    - Fatigue, anorexia, nausea
        
    - **Jaundice**, dark urine, pale stools
        
    - Hepatomegaly, RUQ tenderness
        
- Elevated ALT/AST
    
- Typically **self-limited in adults**
    

#### **B. Chronic HBV Infection**

- **>6 months of HBsAg positivity**
    
- Most perinatal infections progress to chronicity
    
- **Four clinical phases**:
    
    1. **Immune-tolerant**: High viral load, normal ALT, minimal inflammation
        
    2. **Immune-active**: Elevated ALT, active liver inflammation
        
    3. **Inactive carrier**: Low HBV DNA, normal ALT, minimal inflammation
        
    4. **HBeAg-negative chronic hepatitis**: Viral mutation (pre-core), active disease
        

#### **C. Extrahepatic Manifestations**

- **Polyarteritis nodosa (PAN)** – immune complex-mediated vasculitis
    
- **Glomerulonephritis** – especially membranous nephropathy
    
- **Essential mixed cryoglobulinemia**
    

---

### **5. Complications**

- **Fulminant hepatitis** (<1%) – massive hepatic necrosis
    
- **Cirrhosis**
    
- **Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC)** – can occur even without cirrhosis
    
- **Reactivation**:
    
    - Immunosuppression, chemotherapy, anti-TNF therapy
        

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Serologic Markers**

|Marker|Meaning|
|---|---|
|**HBsAg**|Active infection (acute or chronic)|
|**Anti-HBs**|Recovery or vaccination|
|**HBcAb IgM**|Acute infection|
|**HBcAb IgG**|Past exposure (present in chronic too)|
|**HBeAg**|High infectivity, active replication|
|**Anti-HBe**|Lower infectivity, resolving or inactive disease|
|**HBV DNA**|Direct measure of viral replication (PCR)|

**Acute Infection**: HBsAg+, HBcAb IgM+, HBeAg+, high ALT  
**Chronic Infection**: HBsAg > 6 months, HBcAb IgG+, with/without HBeAg  
**Window Period**: HBsAg−, Anti-HBs−, HBcAb IgM+ only

#### **B. Differential Diagnosis**

|Condition|Key Differences|
|---|---|
|**Hepatitis A/E**|Feco-oral route, no chronicity, positive IgM anti-HAV/HEV|
|**Hepatitis C**|No vaccine; anti-HCV and HCV RNA used|
|**Autoimmune hepatitis**|ANA, anti-SMA, hypergammaglobulinemia|
|**Drug-induced liver injury**|Medication history, histological features|

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. Acute HBV Infection**

- Usually **supportive only**
    
- Antivirals not typically required unless fulminant hepatitis develops
    

#### **B. Chronic HBV Infection**

- Goal: Suppress replication, prevent cirrhosis/HCC, seroconversion
    
- **Indications for treatment**:
    
    - Elevated HBV DNA and ALT
        
    - Liver biopsy showing inflammation/fibrosis
        
    - HBeAg status and presence of cirrhosis
        

**First-line Antivirals**:

- **Tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (TDF)** or **Tenofovir alafenamide (TAF)** – potent, high barrier to resistance
    
- **Entecavir** – also potent, low resistance
    
- **Pegylated interferon-α** – finite therapy but significant side effects
    

**Monitoring**:

- HBV DNA, ALT, HBeAg/HBsAg seroconversion
    
- Monitor for resistance, especially with older agents (e.g., lamivudine)
    

#### **C. Hepatocellular Carcinoma Surveillance**

- **Ultrasound ± AFP** every 6 months in high-risk patients (e.g., cirrhotics, Asian males >40)
    

#### **D. Prevention**

- **Recombinant HBV vaccine** (contains HBsAg)
    
    - Given at birth, followed by 1–2 more doses
        
    - 95% effective
        
- **Hepatitis B immune globulin (HBIG)**:
    
    - Post-exposure prophylaxis (e.g., needlestick)
        
    - Given to newborns of HBsAg+ mothers along with vaccine
