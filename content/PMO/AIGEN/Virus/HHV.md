
## **HHV-1 – Herpes Simplex Virus Type 1 (HSV-1)**

---

### **1. Introduction**

Herpes Simplex Virus type 1 (HSV-1) is a double-stranded DNA virus belonging to the Alphaherpesvirinae subfamily of the Herpesviridae family. It is highly prevalent worldwide and primarily causes orolabial infections, though it can also result in genital herpes. Following primary infection, HSV-1 establishes lifelong latency in sensory ganglia, with potential for periodic reactivation.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Herpesviridae
    
- **Subfamily**: Alphaherpesvirinae
    
- **Genus**: Simplexvirus
    
- **Structure**:
    
    - Enveloped icosahedral capsid (~100–110 nm)
        
    - Tegument layer containing viral proteins
        
    - Linear double-stranded DNA genome (~152 kb)
        
- **Genome**: Codes for ~80 proteins
    
- **Tropism**: Epithelial cells, neurons (especially trigeminal and sacral ganglia)
    

**Key Proteins and Genes**:

- **Glycoproteins (gB, gC, gD, gH, gL)**: Facilitate entry and cell-to-cell spread
    
- **ICP0, ICP4**: Immediate early (IE) transcriptional regulators
    
- **LATs (Latency-Associated Transcripts)**: Maintain latency
    
- **VP16**: Tegument protein that activates IE gene expression
    

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Entry and Primary Infection**

- **Attachment**: HSV-1 uses viral glycoproteins gB, gC, and gD to bind to host cell receptors (e.g., heparan sulfate and nectin-1).
    
- **Fusion and Entry**: Mediated by gB/gH/gL complex and gD interacting with entry receptors, leading to membrane fusion and capsid release into the cytoplasm.
    
- **Transport to Nucleus**: Capsid uses dynein-mediated transport along microtubules to reach the nuclear pore.
    

#### **B. Replication Cycle**

1. **Immediate Early Phase**:
    
    - **VP16** (tegument protein) enters the nucleus with the capsid and initiates transcription of IE genes (ICP0, ICP4, ICP22, ICP27).
        
    - **ICP4**: Essential for the transcription of early and late genes.
        
    - **ICP0**: Degrades host defense proteins, including PML bodies.
        
2. **Early Phase**:
    
    - Viral DNA replication proteins synthesized: UL9 (origin-binding protein), UL5/UL52 (helicase-primase complex), UL30 (DNA polymerase), UL42 (polymerase accessory factor).
        
3. **Late Phase**:
    
    - Structural proteins (capsid, glycoproteins) synthesized.
        
    - Capsids assembled in the nucleus; viral DNA is packaged.
        
4. **Egress**:
    
    - Capsids acquire tegument and envelope in the cytoplasm or Golgi.
        
    - Virus exits via exocytosis.
        

#### **C. Latency**

- **Location**: Trigeminal ganglia (or sacral for genital HSV-1)
    
- **Maintenance**: LATs are expressed; they inhibit apoptosis and suppress expression of lytic genes.
    
- **Proteins Involved**:
    
    - LATs (inhibit ICP0 and ICP4)
        
    - Host epigenetic silencing (histone modifications)
        

#### **D. Reactivation**

- Triggered by stress, immunosuppression, UV light
    
- Reactivation leads to transport of virus to epithelial surface, causing recurrent lesions.
    

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

- **Primary Infection**:
    
    - **Gingivostomatitis** (most common in children): fever, painful vesicular lesions on gums, lips, oral mucosa
        
    - Pharyngitis, fever, lymphadenopathy
        
- **Reactivation**:
    
    - **Herpes labialis** ("cold sores")
        
    - May also cause **herpetic whitlow**, **herpes gladiatorum**, or **ocular herpes**
        
- **Others**:
    
    - **Herpes simplex encephalitis**: fever, altered mental status, focal neurologic deficits
        
    - **Keratoconjunctivitis**: unilateral eye pain, photophobia, dendritic ulcers on fluorescein staining
        

---

### **5. Complications**

- **Encephalitis**: Most common cause of sporadic viral encephalitis in the U.S.
    
- **Neonatal HSV**: If transmitted during delivery
    
- **Erythema multiforme**: HSV is a known trigger
    
- **Disseminated infection in immunocompromised patients**
    
- **Blindness** (from recurrent keratitis)
    

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Diagnosis**

- **Tzanck smear**: Multinucleated giant cells (not specific)
    
- **PCR**: Gold standard for detecting HSV DNA
    
- **Viral culture**: Useful in primary infection
    
- **Direct fluorescent antibody**: For antigen detection
    
- **Serology**: Detects antibodies (IgG for past, IgM for recent infection)
    

#### **B. Differential Diagnosis**

|Condition|Distinguishing Feature|
|---|---|
|Aphthous ulcers|No vesicles, non-keratinized mucosa only|
|Coxsackievirus (HFMD)|Involves palms/soles; fever, children|
|Varicella (VZV)|Generalized rash with successive crops|
|Stevens-Johnson Syndrome|Systemic symptoms, mucosal sloughing|
|Bacterial pharyngitis|No vesicles, usually no oral ulcers|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. Antiviral Therapy**

- **First-line**: Acyclovir (IV for encephalitis, oral for mild disease)
    
    - Mechanism: Guanosine analog; phosphorylated by **HSV thymidine kinase**, inhibits viral DNA polymerase
        
- **Alternatives**: Valacyclovir, Famciclovir (better oral bioavailability)
    
- **Resistant strains**: Foscarnet (direct DNA polymerase inhibitor)
    

#### **B. Supportive Care**

- Fluids, analgesia
    
- Topical anesthetics (e.g., viscous lidocaine for oral ulcers)
    

#### **C. Prophylaxis**

- Suppressive therapy for frequent recurrences (e.g., daily acyclovir)
    
- Avoidance of known triggers (UV light, stress)
    

#### **D. Special Populations**

- **Pregnancy**: If active lesions at delivery, C-section recommended
    
- **Immunocompromised**: Higher doses and longer duration of antivirals

## **HHV-2 – Herpes Simplex Virus Type 2 (HSV-2)**

---

### **1. Introduction**

Herpes Simplex Virus Type 2 (HSV-2) is another member of the Alphaherpesvirinae subfamily and shares many structural and functional characteristics with HSV-1. It primarily causes **genital herpes**, although oral infections are possible. Like HSV-1, HSV-2 establishes latency in sensory neurons and can reactivate, leading to recurrent disease. It is a major concern in sexual health and perinatal medicine.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Herpesviridae
    
- **Subfamily**: Alphaherpesvirinae
    
- **Genus**: Simplexvirus
    
- **Structure**:
    
    - Enveloped icosahedral capsid (~100–110 nm)
        
    - Tegument containing regulatory proteins
        
    - Double-stranded linear DNA genome (~152 kb)
        

**Key Proteins**:

- **Glycoproteins**: gB, gC, gD, gE, gG—critical for attachment and immune evasion
    
- **ICP0, ICP4, ICP27**: Immediate early proteins, key transcriptional regulators
    
- **VP16**: Activates immediate early gene expression
    
- **LATs (Latency-Associated Transcripts)**: Crucial for latency in sacral ganglia
    

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Entry & Primary Infection**

- **Attachment**: Via gC and gB to heparan sulfate; gD binds to nectin-1 and HVEM.
    
- **Fusion**: Mediated by gB/gH/gL and gD; fusion occurs at plasma membrane.
    
- **Capsid Transport**: Dynein motors deliver the capsid to the nuclear pore.
    

#### **B. Replication Cascade**

1. **Immediate Early (IE) Gene Expression**: Initiated by **VP16**, leads to production of **ICP0, ICP4, ICP27**, which suppress host immunity and allow transcription of early genes.
    
2. **Early Phase**: Produces proteins for DNA replication: UL5/UL52 (helicase-primase), UL30 (DNA polymerase), UL42 (polymerase cofactor).
    
3. **Late Phase**: Capsid proteins and envelope glycoproteins synthesized.
    
4. **Assembly & Egress**: Capsid is assembled in the nucleus, exits through cytoplasmic membranes after acquiring tegument and envelope.
    

#### **C. Latency**

- **Location**: **Sacral ganglia** (S2–S5)
    
- **Mechanism**: Suppression of lytic genes by chromatin modification; LATs maintain viral genome silencing and block apoptosis of host neurons.
    

#### **D. Reactivation**

- Stimuli: Fever, stress, UV light, immunosuppression
    
- Reactivation causes anterograde transport of virus to mucosa, resulting in recurrent genital lesions.
    

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **A. Primary Genital Herpes**

- Multiple painful vesicles and ulcers on genitalia, perineum, and perianal region
    
- Dysuria, inguinal lymphadenopathy
    
- Flu-like symptoms (fever, myalgia)
    

#### **B. Recurrent Genital Herpes**

- Fewer lesions, less severe symptoms
    
- Shorter duration
    
- May be asymptomatic shedding
    

#### **C. Other Presentations**

- **Herpetic proctitis** (especially in MSM)
    
- **Neonatal herpes** (via perinatal transmission)
    
- **Aseptic meningitis** (Mollaret's meningitis)
    
- **Herpes whitlow, pharyngitis** (less common)
    

---

### **5. Complications**

- **Neonatal herpes**: Transmitted during vaginal delivery; can be localized (skin, eyes, mouth), CNS-only, or disseminated (multi-organ).
    
- **HIV facilitation**: HSV-2 increases risk of HIV acquisition and transmission.
    
- **Recurrent meningitis**: HSV-2 is the most common cause of recurrent benign lymphocytic meningitis.
    
- **Psychosocial impact**: Significant due to chronicity, stigma, and sexual transmission.
    

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Diagnosis**

- **PCR**: Most sensitive and specific; detects HSV DNA
    
- **Viral culture**: Gold standard for viable virus in active lesions
    
- **Tzanck smear**: Shows multinucleated giant cells (nonspecific)
    
- **Serology**: IgG can differentiate HSV-1 vs HSV-2 (useful if asymptomatic)
    

#### **B. Differential Diagnosis**

|Condition|Distinguishing Features|
|---|---|
|Syphilitic chancre|Painless ulcer, firm border, single lesion|
|Chancroid (Haemophilus ducreyi)|Painful ulcer, ragged edge, tender adenopathy|
|Genital warts (HPV)|Painless, verrucous, no ulceration|
|Behçet's syndrome|Recurrent oral/genital ulcers, systemic symptoms|
|Aphthous ulcers|Non-sexual history, mucosal involvement|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. Acute Treatment**

- **First-line**: Acyclovir (oral, or IV if severe or immunocompromised)
    
    - Inhibits HSV DNA polymerase after phosphorylation by HSV thymidine kinase
        
- **Alternatives**: Valacyclovir, Famciclovir
    

#### **B. Suppressive Therapy**

- **Indicated if**:
    
    - > 6 recurrences/year
        
    - Severe psychosocial burden
        
    - Discordant couples (to reduce transmission)
        
- **Regimens**:
    
    - Acyclovir 400 mg BID
        
    - Valacyclovir 500–1000 mg daily
        

#### **C. Special Considerations**

- **Pregnancy**:
    
    - Acyclovir safe in pregnancy (especially 3rd trimester)
        
    - If active lesions at labor, C-section recommended
        
- **Neonatal HSV**:
    
    - Empiric IV acyclovir
        
    - Follow-up with CNS imaging, CSF PCR
        

#### **D. Prevention**

- Barrier protection (condoms reduce but do not eliminate risk)
    
- Avoid intercourse during symptomatic periods
    
- Serologic testing in high-risk populations

## **HHV-3 – Varicella-Zoster Virus (VZV)**

---

### **1. Introduction**

Varicella-Zoster Virus (VZV), classified as Human Herpesvirus 3 (HHV-3), is a member of the Alphaherpesvirinae subfamily. It causes two distinct clinical entities: **varicella (chickenpox)** as the primary infection and **herpes zoster (shingles)** upon reactivation. VZV is highly contagious and establishes latency in cranial nerve and dorsal root ganglia.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Herpesviridae
    
- **Subfamily**: Alphaherpesvirinae
    
- **Genus**: Varicellovirus
    
- **Structure**:
    
    - Enveloped icosahedral capsid (~100–120 nm)
        
    - Double-stranded linear DNA genome (~125 kb)
        
    - Tegument layer with regulatory proteins
        

**Key Proteins and Genes**:

- **Glycoproteins**: gE, gI, gB, gH—crucial for cell entry and immune evasion
    
- **IE62 and IE63**: Immediate early proteins that regulate transcription
    
- **ORF29, ORF28**: Helicase-primase complex
    
- **ORF62**: Transactivator, major regulator of viral gene expression
    
- **ORF63**: Involved in latency maintenance
    
- **gE/gI complex**: Important for viral spread in skin and neurons
    

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Primary Infection (Varicella)**

- **Entry**: Inhalation of respiratory droplets or contact with vesicular fluid.
    
- **Replication**: Initial replication in mucosal epithelium of upper respiratory tract → lymphatic system → viremia (primary, then secondary) → widespread dissemination to skin.
    
- **Skin Lesions**: Appear as vesicles on an erythematous base ("dew drop on a rose petal").
    

#### **B. Latency**

- **Site**: Sensory ganglia (e.g., trigeminal, dorsal root ganglia)
    
- **Mechanism**:
    
    - VZV DNA persists as an episome.
        
    - **ORF63** and host histone modification help suppress lytic genes.
        
    - Virus remains dormant in neurons indefinitely.
        

#### **C. Reactivation (Herpes Zoster)**

- **Triggers**: Aging, immunosuppression, stress
    
- **Process**:
    
    - Reactivation causes anterograde transport of virus to the dermatome innervated by the affected ganglion.
        
    - Inflammatory and immune response leads to vesicular rash and neuropathic pain (postherpetic neuralgia).
        

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **A. Primary Infection – Varicella (Chickenpox)**

- **Prodrome**: Fever, malaise, headache
    
- **Rash**:
    
    - Pruritic, centripetal distribution (starts on trunk, spreads to extremities)
        
    - Lesions in different stages (macules → papules → vesicles → crusts)
        

#### **B. Reactivation – Herpes Zoster (Shingles)**

- **Prodrome**: Burning, tingling, hyperesthesia in dermatome
    
- **Rash**: Unilateral, dermatomal vesicular eruption
    
- **Systemic symptoms**: Fever, malaise
    
- **Contagious**: Can cause varicella in non-immune individuals via contact
    

#### **C. Special Presentations**

- **Zoster ophthalmicus**: CN V1 involvement; can cause corneal ulceration, vision loss
    
- **Zoster oticus (Ramsay Hunt syndrome)**: CN VII involvement; facial paralysis, ear vesicles, hearing loss
    

---

### **5. Complications**

#### **A. Varicella**

- Secondary bacterial infection (S. aureus, GAS)
    
- Pneumonia (esp. in adults)
    
- Encephalitis, cerebellitis
    
- Reye syndrome (if aspirin used in children)
    

#### **B. Herpes Zoster**

- **Postherpetic neuralgia (PHN)**: Persistent neuropathic pain >90 days after rash
    
- Vision loss (ophthalmic zoster)
    
- Motor neuropathies
    
- Disseminated zoster in immunocompromised
    
- Stroke (zoster-associated vasculopathy)
    

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Diagnosis**

- **Clinical diagnosis**: Characteristic rash and distribution
    
- **PCR**: Most sensitive method for detecting VZV DNA
    
- **Direct fluorescent antibody (DFA)**: Identifies viral antigens
    
- **Tzanck smear**: Shows multinucleated giant cells (nonspecific)
    

#### **B. Differential Diagnosis**

|Condition|Distinguishing Feature|
|---|---|
|HSV-1/2|Mucocutaneous or genital distribution, recurrent|
|Coxsackievirus (HFMD)|Hand, foot, and mouth involvement|
|Impetigo|Honey-colored crusts, no vesicles|
|Contact dermatitis|Symmetric rash, no systemic symptoms|
|Dermatitis herpetiformis|Intense pruritus, elbows/knees, associated with celiac|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. Varicella**

- **Healthy children**: Supportive (antipyretics, antihistamines)
    
- **Antiviral indications**:
    
    - > 12 years old
        
    - Chronic pulmonary or skin disease
        
    - Immunocompromised
        
    - Pregnant women
        
- **Drug of choice**: Acyclovir (oral or IV)
    
- **Avoid**: Aspirin (risk of Reye syndrome)
    

#### **B. Herpes Zoster**

- **Antiviral therapy** (best if started within 72 hours):
    
    - Acyclovir, Valacyclovir, or Famciclovir
        
    - Reduces severity and duration of symptoms
        
- **Pain control**:
    
    - NSAIDs, opioids
        
    - For PHN: gabapentin, pregabalin, TCAs, topical lidocaine or capsaicin
        
- **Steroids**: May reduce acute pain, not proven to prevent PHN
    

#### **C. Postexposure Prophylaxis**

- **VZIG (Varicella-Zoster Immune Globulin)**:
    
    - For high-risk exposed individuals (pregnant, immunocompromised, newborns)
        
- **Acyclovir prophylaxis**: Consider in select high-risk groups
    

#### **D. Vaccination**

- **Varicella vaccine** (live attenuated):
    
    - 2 doses in childhood (ages 1 and 4–6)
        
- **Zoster vaccines**:
    
    - **Zoster Recombinant Vaccine (Shingrix)**: Preferred; non-live; recommended for adults ≥50 years
        
    - **Zostavax**: Live attenuated; less preferred, not for immunocompromised

## **HHV-4 – Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV)**

---

### **1. Introduction**

Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV), or Human Herpesvirus 4 (HHV-4), is a **gammaherpesvirus** primarily known for causing **infectious mononucleosis (IM)** and is implicated in several **B-cell malignancies** and epithelial cancers. It infects over 90% of adults worldwide and establishes **lifelong latency** within B lymphocytes.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Herpesviridae
    
- **Subfamily**: Gammaherpesvirinae
    
- **Genus**: Lymphocryptovirus
    
- **Structure**:
    
    - Enveloped icosahedral nucleocapsid (~120–200 nm)
        
    - Double-stranded linear DNA (~172 kb)
        
    - Tegument proteins for immune modulation
        

**Key Proteins and Genes**:

- **EBV Nuclear Antigens (EBNAs 1–6)**: Critical for latency and transformation
    
- **Latent Membrane Proteins (LMP-1, LMP-2A/B)**: Oncogenic signaling molecules
    
- **BZLF1 and BRLF1 (Z and R proteins)**: Immediate early genes for reactivation
    
- **gp350/220**: Major envelope glycoprotein for B-cell entry
    
- **EBERs**: Small RNAs used diagnostically
    

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Primary Infection**

- **Transmission**: Saliva ("kissing disease")
    
- **Target Cells**:
    
    - **Oropharyngeal epithelial cells**: Site of initial replication
        
    - **B lymphocytes**: Infection via CD21 receptor and MHC II as co-receptor
        
- **Entry**: gp350 binds CD21; fusion via gH/gL complex
    

#### **B. Viral Lifecycle**

1. **Lytic Cycle**:
    
    - Viral replication in epithelial cells and activated B cells
        
    - Promoted by BZLF1/BRLF1
        
    - Leads to shedding in saliva
        
2. **Latent Infection**:
    
    - Viral genome remains as episome in memory B cells
        
    - Expression of latency-associated genes: **EBNAs, LMPs, EBERs**
        
    - **Latency types**:
        
        - **Type I**: EBNA-1 only (Burkitt lymphoma)
            
        - **Type II**: EBNA-1, LMP1/2 (Hodgkin lymphoma)
            
        - **Type III**: EBNA-1–6, LMPs (Post-transplant lymphoproliferative disease)
            
3. **Transformation & Oncogenesis**:
    
    - **LMP-1** mimics CD40 signaling → NF-κB activation
        
    - **EBNA-2** activates oncogenes (e.g., c-MYC, cyclin D)
        
    - Promotes B-cell proliferation and survival
        

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **A. Infectious Mononucleosis**

- **Triad**: Fever, pharyngitis, lymphadenopathy (especially posterior cervical)
    
- Fatigue, malaise, splenomegaly
    
- Palatal petechiae, rash (especially if given ampicillin)
    
- Atypical lymphocytosis (Downey cells)
    

#### **B. EBV-Associated Diseases**

- **Burkitt Lymphoma** (endemic/African form): c-MYC translocation (t(8;14))
    
- **Hodgkin Lymphoma** (especially mixed cellularity and lymphocyte-depleted subtypes)
    
- **Nasopharyngeal carcinoma** (esp. in Southeast Asia)
    
- **Gastric carcinoma**
    
- **Oral hairy leukoplakia** (in immunocompromised patients)
    
- **Post-transplant lymphoproliferative disorder (PTLD)**
    

---

### **5. Complications**

- **Splenic rupture**: Medical emergency; avoid contact sports
    
- **Airway obstruction**: Due to tonsillar hypertrophy
    
- **Autoimmune hemolytic anemia**: Cold agglutinin disease (anti-I antibodies)
    
- **Thrombocytopenia**
    
- **Guillain-Barré syndrome, encephalitis, meningitis**
    
- **Chronic active EBV infection**: Rare, severe, systemic illness
    

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Diagnosis**

- **Monospot test** (heterophile antibody test): Positive within 1–2 weeks in IM
    
- **CBC**: Lymphocytosis with >10% atypical lymphocytes
    
- **EBV-specific serology**:
    
    - **VCA-IgM**: Indicates acute infection
        
    - **VCA-IgG**, **EBNA IgG**: Past infection
        
- **PCR**: Quantifies viral load in lymphomas, PTLD
    
- **EBER in situ hybridization**: Confirms EBV in tissue biopsies
    

#### **B. Differential Diagnosis**

|Condition|Key Features|
|---|---|
|**CMV mononucleosis**|Negative Monospot, less pharyngitis/lymphadenopathy|
|**Strep pharyngitis**|No atypical lymphocytes, rapid onset, exudates|
|**Toxoplasmosis**|Exposure to cats, less sore throat|
|**HIV acute infection**|Mononucleosis-like syndrome, high-risk behavior|
|**Lymphoma**|B-symptoms, mass lesions, cytopenias|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. Infectious Mononucleosis**

- **Supportive care**: Rest, fluids, analgesics (avoid NSAIDs in thrombocytopenia)
    
- **Avoid amoxicillin/ampicillin**: Triggers rash in ~90% of IM cases
    
- **Steroids**: Only for airway obstruction, hemolytic anemia, severe thrombocytopenia
    
- **Avoid sports**: For 3–4 weeks due to splenic rupture risk
    

#### **B. EBV-Related Malignancies**

- **Burkitt lymphoma**:
    
    - Intensive chemotherapy (e.g., cyclophosphamide, methotrexate)
        
    - Rituximab (anti-CD20)
        
- **Nasopharyngeal carcinoma**:
    
    - Radiotherapy ± chemotherapy
        
- **PTLD**:
    
    - Reduce immunosuppression
        
    - Rituximab ± chemotherapy
        
    - EBV-directed cytotoxic T-cell therapy (experimental)
        

#### **C. Prevention and Monitoring**

- No licensed EBV vaccine
    
- Universal precautions in transplant settings
    
- Monitor EBV DNA in immunocompromised patients to prevent PTLD

## **HHV-5 – Cytomegalovirus (CMV)**

---

### **1. Introduction**

Human Cytomegalovirus (CMV), also known as Human Herpesvirus 5 (HHV-5), is a **betaherpesvirus** with the ability to establish **lifelong latency** in **monocytes and myeloid progenitors**. CMV infection is typically asymptomatic in immunocompetent individuals but poses significant risks to **fetuses (congenital CMV)** and **immunocompromised patients**, such as transplant recipients and those with AIDS.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Herpesviridae
    
- **Subfamily**: Betaherpesvirinae
    
- **Genus**: Cytomegalovirus
    
- **Structure**:
    
    - Enveloped, icosahedral capsid (~150–200 nm)
        
    - Double-stranded linear DNA (~230 kb, the largest among HHVs)
        
    - Contains many immunomodulatory genes
        

**Key Proteins and Genes**:

- **UL97**: Viral kinase, activates ganciclovir (target of antivirals)
    
- **UL54**: DNA polymerase
    
- **pp65 (UL83)**: Tegument protein, used in diagnostic antigenemia assays
    
- **IE1 and IE2 (Immediate Early genes)**: Transcriptional regulators for lytic replication
    
- **gB (UL55)**: Major envelope glycoprotein, mediates cell entry
    
- **US2–US11**: Inhibit MHC I expression to evade cytotoxic T cells
    

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Primary Infection**

- **Transmission**:
    
    - Saliva, urine, breast milk, sexual contact
        
    - Vertical (transplacental)
        
    - Blood transfusion, organ transplant
        
- **Tropism**: Epithelial cells, endothelial cells, fibroblasts, smooth muscle, monocytes/macrophages
    
- **Viral Entry**: gB and gH/gL complexes mediate attachment and membrane fusion
    

#### **B. Latency**

- **Reservoir**: CD34+ hematopoietic progenitor cells and monocytes
    
- **Latency Maintenance**: Expression of latency-associated transcripts, suppression of lytic IE gene expression (e.g., through histone modifications)
    
- Reactivation triggered by inflammation, immunosuppression
    

#### **C. Immunoevasion**

- Downregulation of **MHC I and II** via **US2–US11**
    
- Interference with **NK cell activation**
    
- Secretion of **viral IL-10 homologs**
    
- Establishment of an immunosuppressive microenvironment
    

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **A. Immunocompetent Hosts**

- Asymptomatic (most common)
    
- **CMV mononucleosis**:
    
    - Fever, malaise, lymphocytosis
        
    - Negative Monospot test
        
    - Mild hepatitis
        

#### **B. Congenital CMV Infection**

- Most common congenital viral infection
    
- Risk highest with **primary maternal infection**
    
- **Signs**:
    
    - Sensorineural hearing loss (most common sequela)
        
    - Periventricular calcifications (vs. diffuse in toxoplasmosis)
        
    - Microcephaly, seizures
        
    - Hepatosplenomegaly, petechiae ("blueberry muffin baby")
        
    - IUGR, jaundice
        

#### **C. Immunocompromised Hosts**

- **CMV retinitis**: Common in AIDS (CD4 <50); “pizza pie” hemorrhagic retinitis
    
- **CMV pneumonitis**: Especially post-transplant
    
- **Gastrointestinal disease**: Esophagitis, colitis
    
- **Hepatitis, encephalitis**
    
- **Graft rejection and failure** in solid organ transplantation
    

---

### **5. Complications**

- **Sensorineural hearing loss** (in congenital infection)
    
- **Chorioretinitis and blindness** (AIDS patients)
    
- **Organ rejection** and increased mortality post-transplant
    
- **Colonic perforation**, severe GI hemorrhage
    
- **Guillain-Barré syndrome**, myocarditis, thrombocytopenia (rare)
    

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Diagnosis**

- **PCR**: Preferred for quantifying viral load (especially in blood, CSF)
    
- **CMV antigenemia assay**: Detects pp65 in leukocytes (useful in transplant monitoring)
    
- **Serology**:
    
    - **IgM**: Recent infection
        
    - **IgG**: Past exposure
        
- **Histology**:
    
    - Classic “**owl’s eye**” intranuclear inclusions in infected cells
        
- **Tissue culture** (slow, rarely used now)
    

#### **B. Differential Diagnosis**

|Condition|Distinguishing Feature|
|---|---|
|**EBV mononucleosis**|Positive Monospot, prominent pharyngitis|
|**Toxoplasmosis**|Diffuse calcifications, chorioretinitis in congenital form|
|**HSV esophagitis**|Shallow ulcers; CMV has linear/deep ulcers|
|**HIV retinopathy**|Cotton wool spots, not hemorrhagic or necrotic|
|**IBD**|Can mimic CMV colitis, but lacks viral inclusions|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. First-Line Antiviral Therapy**

- **Ganciclovir** (IV): Activated by viral UL97 kinase; inhibits viral DNA polymerase (UL54)
    
- **Valganciclovir** (oral prodrug): Preferred for long-term maintenance
    

#### **B. Second-Line Agents**

- **Foscarnet**: Directly inhibits viral DNA polymerase; used when UL97 mutations cause ganciclovir resistance
    
- **Cidofovir**: DNA polymerase inhibitor; nephrotoxic—requires probenecid and hydration
    

#### **C. Congenital CMV**

- Oral **valganciclovir** for 6 months in symptomatic infants to improve hearing and neurodevelopment
    

#### **D. CMV Prophylaxis**

- In transplant patients:
    
    - **Universal prophylaxis** (valganciclovir) or
        
    - **Pre-emptive therapy** based on viral load monitoring
        

#### **E. Monitoring**

- Regular PCR or antigenemia testing in high-risk patients (e.g., post-transplant)
    
- No approved CMV vaccine yet; several candidates under investigation

## **HHV-6 – Human Herpesvirus 6 (Types A and B)**

---

### **1. Introduction**

Human Herpesvirus 6 (HHV-6) includes two closely related variants: **HHV-6A** and **HHV-6B**. Both are **betaherpesviruses**, but they differ in epidemiology and disease associations:

- **HHV-6B** is the **most clinically relevant**, associated with **roseola infantum (exanthem subitum)**.
    
- **HHV-6A** is less understood but is more neurotropic and possibly linked to **multiple sclerosis** and other neuroinflammatory conditions.
    

Nearly all children are infected by age 2, with HHV-6B responsible for the majority of **febrile seizures** in infants.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Herpesviridae
    
- **Subfamily**: Betaherpesvirinae
    
- **Genus**: Roseolovirus
    
- **Structure**:
    
    - Enveloped, icosahedral capsid (~150–200 nm)
        
    - Linear double-stranded DNA genome (~160–170 kb)
        

**Key Proteins and Genes**:

- **U14**: Latency-associated protein
    
- **U90 (IE-A protein)**: Immediate early protein; regulator of replication
    
- **U83**: Chemokine mimic; promotes viral spread and immune modulation
    
- **gQ1/gQ2-gH/gL complex**: Required for receptor binding and fusion
    
- **DR7**: Immunomodulatory, anti-apoptotic protein
    

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Transmission**

- **Mode**: Saliva, respiratory droplets, vertical transmission (possible)
    
- **Primary Infection Site**: Oropharynx, then systemic spread
    
- **Target Cells**: CD4+ T cells (primary), also CD8+ T cells, NK cells, glial cells, astrocytes
    

#### **B. Entry and Replication**

- Virus binds **CD46** (for HHV-6A) or **CD134 (OX40)** (for HHV-6B)
    
- Fusion mediated by gQ/gH/gL complex
    
- Viral replication occurs in nucleus of T cells and epithelial cells
    

#### **C. Latency and Reactivation**

- **Latent Reservoir**: T cells, monocytes/macrophages, CNS glial cells
    
- Integrated into host **telomeric regions** of chromosomes (unique among HHVs)
    
- Reactivation can occur under immunosuppression (e.g., transplant, HIV)
    

#### **D. Immune Evasion**

- Downregulation of MHC I and II
    
- Interference with IL-2 signaling (impairs T cell proliferation)
    
- Expression of **virokines** (e.g., U83) that mimic host chemokines
    

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **A. In Children (HHV-6B)**

- **Roseola infantum (exanthem subitum)**:
    
    - High fever (3–5 days) followed by abrupt defervescence and appearance of **maculopapular rash**
        
    - Rash starts on trunk → spreads to limbs and face
        
    - Mild lymphadenopathy, irritability
        
- **Febrile seizures** (common in roseola)
    

#### **B. In Immunocompromised Hosts**

- **Encephalitis**: Limbic system involvement (especially in HSCT recipients)
    
- **Bone marrow suppression**: Pancytopenia
    
- **Pneumonitis, hepatitis**
    
- **Delayed engraftment** in HSCT
    

#### **C. HHV-6A**

- **Neuroinflammation**: Experimental associations with **multiple sclerosis**, **temporal lobe epilepsy**, **glioma**
    
- **In vitro** neurotropism more pronounced than HHV-6B
    

---

### **5. Complications**

- **Febrile seizures**: Most common cause under 2 years
    
- **Encephalitis**: Especially in immunosuppressed; high morbidity
    
- **Graft-versus-host disease (GVHD)** potentiation
    
- **Chronic fatigue-like syndromes** (controversial)
    
- **Chromosomally integrated HHV-6 (ciHHV-6)**: ~1% of population; inherited in autosomal dominant manner
    

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Diagnosis**

- **PCR**: Gold standard for detecting viral DNA (CSF, blood, tissue)
    
- **Quantitative PCR**: Helps distinguish active infection from latent or chromosomally integrated virus
    
- **Serology**: Not helpful for reactivation (near-universal seropositivity by age 2)
    
- **Immunohistochemistry**: In tissue biopsy (e.g., brain in encephalitis)
    

#### **B. Differential Diagnosis**

|Condition|Distinguishing Features|
|---|---|
|**Measles**|Rash starts at head, Koplik spots|
|**Rubella**|Postauricular lymphadenopathy, milder symptoms|
|**Enterovirus infection**|Summer seasonality, GI/respiratory symptoms|
|**Meningitis/encephalitis**|Need to distinguish in febrile seizures with altered consciousness|
|**Chromosomally integrated HHV-6**|Very high viral load (>10⁶ copies/mL); family members often positive|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. Supportive Care**

- For roseola: antipyretics, hydration
    
- No specific antiviral therapy indicated in immunocompetent children
    

#### **B. Antiviral Therapy (Immunocompromised/Severe Cases)**

- **First-line**:
    
    - **Ganciclovir** (IV): Inhibits viral DNA polymerase
        
    - **Foscarnet**: Second-line or for ganciclovir resistance
        
- **Other options**:
    
    - **Cidofovir**: For resistant cases, nephrotoxic
        
- **No established prophylaxis** in transplant patients, but some centers monitor HHV-6 DNA
    

#### **C. Monitoring**

- Regular PCR in **hematopoietic stem cell transplant (HSCT)** recipients
    
- Close neurological monitoring in high-risk patients

## **HHV-7 – Human Herpesvirus 7**

---

### **1. Introduction**

Human Herpesvirus 7 (HHV-7) is a **betaherpesvirus** closely related to HHV-6, first isolated in 1990. Like HHV-6, it is lymphotropic and establishes latency in CD4+ T cells. HHV-7 typically infects individuals in early childhood, and although often asymptomatic, it can cause **roseola-like illness**, **febrile seizures**, and potentially contribute to **neurological diseases** in rare cases.

HHV-7's clinical importance is less well-defined than other herpesviruses, and most infections occur **after** HHV-6 primary infection.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Herpesviridae
    
- **Subfamily**: Betaherpesvirinae
    
- **Genus**: Roseolovirus
    
- **Structure**:
    
    - Enveloped, icosahedral capsid (~150–200 nm)
        
    - Linear dsDNA genome (~145 kb)
        
    - Shares significant genomic homology with HHV-6
        

**Key Proteins and Genes**:

- **U14**: Latency-associated protein
    
- **U21**: Downregulates MHC I
    
- **gB (glycoprotein B)**: Mediates viral entry
    
- **IE proteins (e.g., U42, U95)**: Regulate early replication
    
- **Chemokine mimic genes**: Modulate immune responses
    

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Transmission**

- **Mode**: Saliva (primary route), possibly vertical
    
- **Initial Infection Site**: Likely oropharynx
    
- **Peak Age**: 2–5 years
    

#### **B. Cellular Tropism**

- **CD4+ T lymphocytes**: Primary reservoir
    
- Also infects salivary gland epithelial cells, glial cells (less commonly)
    

#### **C. Replication and Latency**

- **Cell entry**: Mediated via **CD4 receptor** and possibly **CXCR1/2** co-receptors
    
- Viral DNA replicates in the nucleus
    
- Establishes latency in T cells with low-level gene expression
    

#### **D. Reactivation**

- Can reactivate during immunosuppression or stress
    
- Reactivation often occurs simultaneously with HHV-6 or CMV
    

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **A. In Immunocompetent Children**

- Often asymptomatic or presents with:
    
    - **Roseola-like illness** (indistinguishable from HHV-6)
        
    - **Fever without rash**
        
    - **Febrile seizures**
        
- Rash may occur **after fever resolves**, starting on the trunk
    

#### **B. In Immunocompromised Patients**

- **Encephalitis**, **meningitis**, **myelitis** (rare but reported)
    
- **Pneumonitis**, **fever of unknown origin**
    
- Coinfection/reactivation with CMV or HHV-6 can complicate transplant cases
    

#### **C. Suspected Chronic Conditions** (under investigation)

- Chronic fatigue syndrome
    
- Lichen planus
    
- Multiple sclerosis (speculative link)
    

---

### **5. Complications**

- **Febrile seizures**
    
- **CNS complications** (encephalitis, especially in transplant patients)
    
- Possible involvement in chronic neurological and inflammatory diseases (rare, not well established)
    

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Diagnosis**

- **PCR**: Most sensitive method; detects viral DNA in blood, CSF, or tissue
    
- **Viral culture**: Rarely used, difficult to grow
    
- **Serology**: Not helpful due to high background seroprevalence
    
- **CSF PCR**: Important for suspected HHV-7-associated encephalitis
    

#### **B. Differential Diagnosis**

|Condition|Distinguishing Features|
|---|---|
|**HHV-6 infection**|More common cause of roseola; indistinguishable clinically|
|**Measles or rubella**|More diffuse rash, Koplik spots (measles), lymphadenopathy|
|**Enteroviral infections**|Summer/fall seasonality, associated with GI symptoms|
|**Seizure due to other causes**|History, EEG, no associated viral symptoms|
|**HSV/CMV encephalitis**|Requires PCR and neuroimaging to differentiate|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. Supportive Care**

- In most cases: antipyretics and monitoring
    
- Self-limited in healthy children
    

#### **B. Antiviral Therapy (For Severe/Reactivated Disease)**

- **Ganciclovir**, **foscarnet**, or **cidofovir** may have in vitro activity
    
- No antiviral approved specifically for HHV-7
    
- Reserved for use in immunocompromised hosts with proven HHV-7 disease
    

#### **C. Monitoring**

- HHV-7 viral load rarely used clinically due to its unclear significance
    
- Monitoring may be considered in co-infected transplant recipients

## **HHV-8 – Human Herpesvirus 8 (Kaposi’s Sarcoma-Associated Herpesvirus, KSHV)**

---

### **1. Introduction**

Human Herpesvirus 8 (HHV-8), also called **Kaposi’s Sarcoma-Associated Herpesvirus (KSHV)**, is a **gammaherpesvirus** discovered in 1994. It is the etiologic agent of **Kaposi’s sarcoma (KS)**, **primary effusion lymphoma (PEL)**, and **multicentric Castleman disease (MCD)**. HHV-8 is unique among human herpesviruses for its strong **oncogenic potential**, encoding multiple proteins that mimic human oncogenes, cytokines, and signaling molecules.

HHV-8 is endemic in parts of **Africa**, **Mediterranean countries**, and among **men who have sex with men (MSM)**.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Herpesviridae
    
- **Subfamily**: Gammaherpesvirinae
    
- **Genus**: Rhadinovirus
    
- **Structure**:
    
    - Enveloped, icosahedral capsid
        
    - Linear dsDNA genome (~165 kb)
        

**Key Proteins and Genes**:

- **LANA-1 (Latency-Associated Nuclear Antigen)**: Maintains episomal latency, inhibits p53 and Rb
    
- **vGPCR (viral G protein-coupled receptor)**: Activates angiogenic and inflammatory pathways
    
- **vIL-6 (viral interleukin-6)**: Promotes B cell proliferation
    
- **vBcl-2**: Inhibits apoptosis
    
- **vFLIP**: Inhibits Fas-mediated apoptosis, activates NF-κB
    
- **ORF50 (RTA)**: Initiates lytic replication
    
- **K1**: Activates PI3K/Akt and NF-κB pathways, promotes cell survival
    

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Transmission**

- **Modes**:
    
    - Saliva (primary)
        
    - Sexual contact (especially MSM)
        
    - Blood transfusion
        
    - Organ transplantation
        
    - Vertical transmission (rare)
        
- High prevalence in sub-Saharan Africa and Mediterranean regions
    

#### **B. Tropism**

- **B lymphocytes**, **endothelial cells**, **epithelial cells**, and **monocytes**
    
- Establishes **latent infection** primarily in B cells
    

#### **C. Latency and Reactivation**

- Latency maintained via **LANA-1**, which tethers viral DNA to host chromosomes
    
- Reactivation triggered by inflammation, immunosuppression, or coinfection (e.g., HIV)
    
- Lytic cycle involves broad expression of viral genes, leading to cytokine production, angiogenesis, and immune evasion
    

#### **D. Oncogenesis**

- HHV-8 encodes numerous **oncogenic homologs**:
    
    - **vIL-6**: Promotes proliferation, angiogenesis, inflammation
        
    - **vBcl-2**, **vFLIP**: Inhibit apoptosis
        
    - **vCCLs** (viral chemokines): Promote cell migration and immune evasion
        
    - **vGPCR**: Induces VEGF, promotes angiogenesis and tumor formation
        
- Tumor cells are often of **spindle cell phenotype**, derived from endothelial cells
    

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **A. Kaposi’s Sarcoma (KS)**

- **Cutaneous lesions**: Purple, red, or brown nodules or plaques, often on lower limbs, oral mucosa
    
- **Visceral involvement**: GI tract, lungs, liver
    
- **Four epidemiologic forms**:
    
    - **Classic KS**: Elderly men of Mediterranean origin
        
    - **Endemic KS**: Sub-Saharan Africa; often aggressive
        
    - **Epidemic KS**: HIV/AIDS-associated (AIDS-defining illness)
        
    - **Iatrogenic KS**: In organ transplant recipients
        

#### **B. Primary Effusion Lymphoma (PEL)**

- **Rare, aggressive B cell lymphoma**
    
- Typically presents as malignant effusion in pleural, pericardial, or peritoneal cavity
    
- Often co-infected with **EBV**
    
- No identifiable tumor mass
    

#### **C. Multicentric Castleman Disease (MCD)**

- **Systemic lymphoproliferative disorder** affecting multiple lymph node regions
    
- Symptoms: Fever, night sweats, weight loss, lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly
    
- Elevated **IL-6** levels; can be fatal
    

---

### **5. Complications**

- **Immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome (IRIS)** in HIV patients starting ART
    
- Progression of **Kaposi’s sarcoma** to visceral organs
    
- **Cytokine storm** in MCD
    
- High mortality in **PEL** without treatment
    

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Diagnosis**

- **Biopsy** of lesions with histopathology:
    
    - Spindle cells, slit-like vascular spaces, hemosiderin
        
    - Immunostaining for **LANA-1** confirms HHV-8
        
- **PCR**: Detects HHV-8 DNA in tissue, blood, effusions
    
- **Serology**: Used for epidemiologic studies (not reliable for clinical use)
    
- **Cytology** and flow cytometry for effusions (PEL)
    

#### **B. Differential Diagnosis**

|Condition|Distinguishing Features|
|---|---|
|**Bacillary angiomatosis**|Caused by Bartonella; neutrophilic infiltrate, Warthin-Starry stain|
|**Angiosarcoma**|Highly malignant, more cellular atypia, lacks HHV-8|
|**Lymphomas (e.g., DLBCL)**|Mass-forming, often EBV-related but HHV-8-negative|
|**Other herpesviruses**|HHV-6/7 do not form tumors|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. Kaposi’s Sarcoma**

- **Mild Cutaneous KS**:
    
    - **HAART** alone (in HIV patients) may cause regression
        
- **Moderate-to-Severe KS**:
    
    - **Liposomal doxorubicin** or **paclitaxel**
        
    - **Radiation therapy** for local lesions
        
    - **Immune modulation** (withdrawal of immunosuppression in transplant recipients)
        

#### **B. Primary Effusion Lymphoma (PEL)**

- **Chemotherapy**: CHOP or EPOCH regimens; poor prognosis
    
- Investigational therapies: **targeting NF-κB, mTOR**, or **viral proteins**
    

#### **C. Multicentric Castleman Disease**

- **Rituximab** (anti-CD20) for B cell depletion
    
- **Tocilizumab** or **siltuximab** (anti-IL-6 receptor)
    
- Antivirals are not consistently effective
    

#### **D. Antivirals**

- **Ganciclovir, foscarnet**: Inhibit lytic replication but do not eliminate latent infection
    
- May reduce HHV-8 viral load in MCD or prevent KS progression
