
## **Poxviridae – Variola Virus (Smallpox Virus)**

---

### **1. Introduction**

**Variola virus** is the etiologic agent of **smallpox**, a highly contagious and deadly disease eradicated globally in 1980 through an aggressive vaccination campaign. It belongs to the **Orthopoxvirus** genus of the **Poxviridae** family.

Unlike most DNA viruses, poxviruses **replicate in the cytoplasm**, carrying all the necessary transcriptional machinery. Smallpox had a **30% mortality rate**, especially in the **Variola major** form.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Poxviridae
    
- **Genus**: Orthopoxvirus
    
- **Species**: Variola virus
    

#### **Structure**:

- **Enveloped**, brick-shaped virion (~200–300 nm)
    
- **Linear, double-stranded DNA (~186 kb)**
    
- Complex symmetry (not icosahedral or helical)
    
- Replication occurs in **cytoplasmic "viral factories"**
    

#### **Key Proteins & Enzymes**:

- **Viral DNA-dependent RNA polymerase** (carries its own)
    
- **Transcription factors**, **capping**, and **polyadenylation enzymes**
    
- **Immune evasion proteins**:
    
    - **B18R**: soluble IFN receptor homolog
        
    - **CrmB**: TNF receptor mimic
        
    - **SPI-2**: Serpin that inhibits caspase-1
        

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Transmission and Entry**

- Spread via **respiratory droplets**, **fomites**, and **direct contact**
    
- Entry through **upper respiratory tract** or **oropharynx**
    
- Virus infects **mucosal epithelial cells** and **macrophages**
    

#### **B. Primary Viremia**

- Disseminates to **lymph nodes** and **reticuloendothelial system**
    
- **Replication in monocytes/macrophages**
    
- Leads to **secondary viremia** and seeding of the skin
    

#### **C. Skin Involvement**

- Infects **dermal endothelial cells** and **keratinocytes**
    
- Triggers **dermal inflammation** → vesicle/pustule formation
    

#### **D. Immune Evasion**

- Variola blocks:
    
    - **IFN-α/β signaling**
        
    - **Complement activation**
        
    - **Cytokine signaling (e.g., IL-1, TNF)**
        
- Delays immune clearance → facilitates systemic spread
    

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **A. Incubation Period**: 7–17 days (mean ~12 days)

#### **B. Prodromal Phase (2–4 days)**:

- High fever, malaise, prostration
    
- Severe headache, backache
    
- Vomiting, abdominal pain
    
- No rash yet → **patient already infectious**
    

#### **C. Exanthem Phase**

- **Enanthem**: Lesions in oropharynx appear first
    
- **Skin rash** (centrifugal distribution): Face → extremities → trunk
    
- Lesion progression (synchronous):  
    **Macule → Papule → Vesicle → Pustule → Crust**
    
- **Deep, firm, umbilicated pustules**
    

#### **D. Types of Smallpox**

1. **Variola major**:
    
    - Classic form, ~30% fatality
        
    - Severe systemic symptoms
        
2. **Variola minor (Alastrim)**:
    
    - Milder, <1% fatality
        
3. **Hemorrhagic smallpox**:
    
    - Fulminant, rapidly fatal
        
    - Petechiae, bleeding, DIC
        
4. **Malignant (flat) smallpox**:
    
    - Confluent rash with poor pustule formation
        
    - Often fatal
        

---

### **5. Complications**

- Secondary bacterial infections (cellulitis, sepsis)
    
- **Corneal ulceration**, **blindness**
    
- **Arthritis**, **encephalitis**
    
- **Pneumonia**, **osteomyelitis**
    
- **Severe scarring** (disfiguring)
    
- Death from **toxemia**, **shock**, or **hemorrhage**
    

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Laboratory Diagnosis**

- **PCR**: Most sensitive/specific (vesicle fluid or scabs)
    
- **Electron microscopy**: Detects brick-shaped virions
    
- **Cell culture**: Limited use due to biosafety concerns
    
- **Serology**: Historical (not diagnostic in early disease)
    

#### **B. Differential Diagnosis**

|Disease|Distinguishing Features|
|---|---|
|**Chickenpox (VZV)**|Lesions in crops (asynchronous), centripetal|
|**Monkeypox**|Similar rash but often with lymphadenopathy|
|**Hand-foot-mouth**|Coxsackievirus, oral + hand/foot lesions|
|**Rickettsialpox**|Eschar, regional distribution|
|**Disseminated HSV**|Immunocompromised, painful vesicles|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. Supportive Care**

- Antipyretics
    
- Fluid/electrolyte management
    
- Treat secondary infections
    

#### **B. Antiviral Therapy**

- **Tecovirimat (TPOXX)**:
    
    - FDA-approved for smallpox (in vitro and animal data)
        
    - Inhibits VP37 envelope protein, blocks viral release
        
- **Cidofovir / Brincidofovir**:
    
    - Broad anti-DNA virus activity (IV or oral)
        
    - Reserved for severe or resistant cases
        

---

### **8. Prevention**

#### **A. Vaccination**

- **Live vaccinia virus** (not variola)
    
- Highly effective (~95%)
    
- **Administered via scarification (bifurcated needle)**
    

#### **Types of Vaccines**:

- **ACAM2000**: Live, replicating vaccinia virus (U.S. military)
    
- **Modified Vaccinia Ankara (MVA)**: Non-replicating, safer (Jynneos)
    

#### **B. Post-Exposure Prophylaxis**

- Vaccination within **3–5 days of exposure** may prevent or mitigate illness
    

#### **C. Eradication**

- Declared eradicated in **1980 (WHO)**
    
- **Virus stockpiles** remain in CDC (USA) and VECTOR (Russia)
    
- Concerns remain about **bioterrorism**

## **Poxviridae – Monkeypox Virus (Mpox)**

---

### **1. Introduction**

**Monkeypox virus (MPXV)** is an emerging zoonotic Orthopoxvirus that causes **Mpox**, a disease clinically similar to smallpox but generally less severe. First identified in 1958 in monkeys and in humans in 1970 (Democratic Republic of the Congo), it has become a **global public health concern** due to outbreaks in non-endemic countries, especially since 2022.

It is now considered the most significant Orthopoxvirus threat in the post-smallpox era.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Poxviridae
    
- **Genus**: Orthopoxvirus
    
- **Species**: Monkeypox virus
    

#### **Structure**:

- **Enveloped**, brick-shaped virion (~200–250 nm)
    
- **Linear double-stranded DNA (~197 kb)**
    
- Replicates in the **cytoplasm**
    

#### **Clades**:

- **Clade I (Congo Basin)**: More virulent, ~10% mortality
    
- **Clade II (West African)**:
    
    - Subclades: IIa (historical), IIb (2022 outbreak)
        
    - Lower mortality (<1%)
        

#### **Key Proteins**:

- **F13L (envelope protein)**: Target of tecovirimat
    
- **B16R**: IFN-γ decoy receptor
    
- **CrmB**: TNF receptor mimic
    
- **B21R**: Virulence factor, immunomodulator
    

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Transmission**

- **Zoonotic**: contact with infected animals (rodents, primates)
    
- **Human-to-human**:
    
    - Respiratory droplets (close contact)
        
    - **Skin-to-skin** and **mucosal contact**
        
    - **Sexual transmission** (notably MSM networks in 2022)
        
    - Fomites (bedding, surfaces)
        

#### **B. Entry and Dissemination**

- Entry via broken skin, mucosa, or respiratory tract
    
- Virus replicates at **site of entry** and in **regional lymph nodes**
    
- **Primary viremia** → systemic dissemination → **secondary viremia**
    
- Spreads to skin and mucosa → vesiculopustular lesions
    

#### **C. Immune Evasion**

- Encodes proteins that:
    
    - Inhibit **interferon signaling**
        
    - Suppress **pro-inflammatory cytokines**
        
    - Block **complement activation**
        
- Delays immune clearance → allows systemic spread
    

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **Incubation**: 6–13 days (range: 5–21 days)

#### **Prodrome**:

- Fever
    
- Chills
    
- Headache
    
- **Lymphadenopathy** (key distinguishing feature vs smallpox)
    
- Muscle aches
    
- Fatigue
    

#### **Rash Phase (1–4 days after prodrome)**:

- Begins on face, then trunk, extremities, and mucosa
    
- **Progression** (synchronous):  
    **Macules → Papules → Vesicles → Pustules → Scabs**
    
- Lesions often painful, then itchy as they crust
    
- In 2022 outbreak:
    
    - Lesions concentrated on **genital and perianal areas**
        
    - May be fewer, asynchronous lesions
        

---

### **5. Complications**

- **Secondary bacterial infections**
    
- **Pneumonitis**
    
- **Keratitis and corneal ulceration**
    
- **Sepsis**
    
- **Encephalitis**
    
- In immunocompromised: **prolonged or disseminated disease**
    
- In pregnancy: **fetal loss**, congenital infection
    

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Laboratory Diagnosis**

- **PCR (gold standard)**:
    
    - Lesion fluid or crust
        
- **Electron microscopy**: Shows typical brick-shaped virions
    
- **Viral culture**: Requires BSL-3 lab
    
- **Serology**: Limited (cross-reactivity with other Orthopoxviruses)
    

#### **B. Differential Diagnosis**

|Condition|Distinguishing Features|
|---|---|
|**Smallpox**|More uniform lesions, higher fatality, no lymphadenopathy|
|**Varicella (Chickenpox)**|Lesions at different stages, centripetal pattern|
|**Herpes simplex virus**|Painful clustered vesicles, oral/genital; localized|
|**Syphilis (secondary)**|Maculopapular rash, palms/soles; positive serology|
|**Hand-foot-mouth disease**|Lesions on palms, soles, mouth; usually in children|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **Supportive Care**:

- Fluids, nutrition
    
- Antipyretics, analgesics
    
- Wound care and secondary infection prevention
    

#### **Antiviral Therapy**:

- **Tecovirimat (TPOXX)**:
    
    - Inhibits VP37 protein (F13L gene product)
        
    - Oral and IV forms available
        
    - First-line for severe or high-risk cases
        
- **Brincidofovir** and **Cidofovir**:
    
    - DNA polymerase inhibitors
        
    - Considered in resistant or severe cases
        

#### **Vaccinia Immune Globulin (VIG)**:

- Considered for severe immunocompromised cases
    

---

### **8. Prevention**

#### **A. Vaccination**

- **Pre-exposure** and **post-exposure prophylaxis** for high-risk groups
    
- **Two vaccines**:
    
    - **JYNNEOS** (MVA-BN):
        
        - Non-replicating, subcutaneous
            
        - 2 doses, 4 weeks apart
            
        - Safe in immunocompromised
            
    - **ACAM2000**:
        
        - Live replicating vaccinia virus
            
        - Single dose, scarification
            
        - Higher risk of adverse events
            

#### **B. Public Health Measures**

- Isolation of cases until lesions crusted and healed
    
- Contact tracing
    
- Education on reducing skin-to-skin and sexual transmission

## **Poxviridae – Molluscum Contagiosum Virus (MCV)**

---

### **1. Introduction**

**Molluscum contagiosum virus (MCV)** is a **human-specific** Poxvirus causing **molluscum contagiosum**, a benign, self-limiting skin infection. Unlike other Orthopoxviruses, MCV belongs to the **Molluscipoxvirus** genus and does **not cause systemic illness**.

It is especially common in **children**, **immunocompromised individuals** (e.g., HIV/AIDS), and can also be **sexually transmitted** in adults.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Poxviridae
    
- **Genus**: Molluscipoxvirus
    
- **Species**: Molluscum contagiosum virus (MCV)
    

#### **Structure**:

- **Enveloped**, ovoid/brick-shaped (~200–300 nm)
    
- **Large, linear, double-stranded DNA (~190 kb)**
    
- Encodes its own **DNA replication and transcription machinery**
    
- Replicates entirely in the **cytoplasm**
    

#### **Types/Genotypes**:

- **MCV-1**: Most prevalent (~90% of cases)
    
- **MCV-2**: More common in adults, often sexually transmitted
    

#### **Immune Evasion Proteins**:

- **MC132**: Inhibits NF-κB signaling
    
- **MC159**: Mimics FLIP protein → inhibits apoptosis
    
- **MC80**: MHC class I mimic → evades cytotoxic T-cell response
    
- **MC005L**: Inhibits interferon response
    

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Transmission**

- **Direct skin-to-skin contact** (children, sexual contact in adults)
    
- **Autoinoculation** (scratching spreads virus)
    
- **Fomites** (towels, swimming pools, gym equipment)
    

#### **B. Viral Entry and Replication**

- Enters via **microabrasions** or hair follicles
    
- Infects **epidermal keratinocytes**
    
- Replicates in cytoplasmic viral factories
    
- Induces cell **proliferation** and **enlargement**, forming **molluscum bodies** (intracytoplasmic inclusion bodies)
    

#### **C. Host Response**

- Localized immune response
    
- Little or no systemic involvement
    
- Chronic infection in **immunosuppressed** due to poor T-cell mediated clearance
    

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **A. Incubation Period**: 2–8 weeks (can be up to 6 months)

#### **B. Skin Lesions**:

- Small (2–5 mm), **flesh-colored, dome-shaped papules**
    
- Central **umbilication**
    
- May appear anywhere except palms and soles
    
- In children: face, trunk, limbs
    
- In adults: **genital, pubic, inner thighs** (STI form)
    
- **Pearly** appearance
    
- Often grouped or scattered
    

#### **C. Asymptomatic** or mild **itching** or inflammation

- Scratching may cause spread or secondary infection
    
- Inflammatory response before resolution ("BOTE sign" – beginning of the end)
    

---

### **5. Complications**

- **Secondary bacterial infection** (impetiginization)
    
- **Scarring** (especially if lesions excoriated or treated aggressively)
    
- **Eczema/Molluscum dermatitis** (inflammatory response around lesions)
    
- **Extensive, recalcitrant lesions** in immunosuppressed patients (e.g., HIV)
    

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Diagnosis**

- Usually **clinical** based on typical appearance
    
- **Dermatoscopy**: Central pore/umbilication with white, amorphous core
    
- **Histopathology**:
    
    - **Molluscum (Henderson-Patterson) bodies**: Large eosinophilic intracytoplasmic inclusions in keratinocytes
        
- **PCR or electron microscopy** (rarely needed)
    

#### **B. Differential Diagnosis**

|Condition|Distinguishing Features|
|---|---|
|**Warts (HPV)**|Rough, keratotic, no central dimple|
|**Folliculitis**|Pustular, inflamed, follicular origin|
|**Varicella/HSV**|Vesicular, painful, systemic symptoms possible|
|**Basal cell carcinoma**|Pearly papule, telangiectasia, older patients|
|**Cryptococcosis (AIDS)**|Umbilicated lesions, systemic signs, immunocompromised|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. Watchful Waiting**

- Often resolves spontaneously within **6–12 months**
    
- Indicated for children and asymptomatic individuals
    

#### **B. Physical Destruction**

- **Curettage**: Highly effective; may scar
    
- **Cryotherapy**: Liquid nitrogen application
    
- **Cantharidin** (blistering agent): Painless, used in children
    
- **Laser therapy**: For resistant or cosmetically sensitive cases
    

#### **C. Topical Therapies**

- **Imiquimod**: Immune response modifier (limited efficacy)
    
- **Tretinoin/Adapalene**: Retinoids to induce peeling
    
- **Podophyllotoxin**: Antimitotic, genital area
    

#### **D. Systemic Therapies**

- Reserved for **extensive or immunosuppressed cases**
    
- **Cimetidine**, **cidofovir**, or **interferon-alpha** (rare)
    

---

### **8. Prevention**

- Avoid skin-to-skin contact with infected individuals
    
- Avoid sharing towels, razors, gym equipment
    
- Condom use reduces but doesn’t eliminate risk of sexual transmission
    
- Proper hygiene in communal areas (e.g., swimming pools)
