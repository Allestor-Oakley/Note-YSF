
# Picornaviridae Family Viruses

## Poliovirus

### Introduction

Poliovirus is the prototypic picornavirus, first identified as the causative agent of poliomyelitis by Landsteiner and Popper in 1908. It comprises three serotypes (PV1–3) that historically caused epidemic paralytic disease. Humans are the only natural host for poliovirus. Widespread vaccination (Salk IPV in 1955 and Sabin OPV in 1960) has brought poliovirus to the brink of eradication. In current taxonomy, the three serotypes are classified within _Human enterovirus_ C (formerly _Enterovirus_ genus) of Picornaviridae.

### Characteristics / Profile

Poliovirus is a small (≈30 nm), nonenveloped virus with an icosahedral capsid containing a single-stranded, positive-sense RNA genome ~7.5 kb in length. The genome encodes one long polyprotein that is proteolytically cleaved into structural proteins (capsid proteins VP1–4) and nonstructural proteins (including the 3D^pol RNA-dependent RNA polymerase). The capsid is a quasi-T=3 icosahedron composed of 60 copies of VP1–VP4. Poliovirus particles are **acid-stable** and environmentally hardy, facilitating fecal–oral spread. Transmission occurs primarily by ingestion of virus-contaminated food or water or via oropharyngeal secretions. Initial viral replication occurs in the oropharynx and intestinal mucosa (tonsils, Peyer’s patches). The major cellular receptor for poliovirus is **CD155** (the poliovirus receptor, PVR), a member of the Ig superfamily. Binding to CD155 triggers endocytosis and uncoating. (Notably, CD155 is required for entry at neuromuscular junctions, but poliovirus can also traverse the blood–brain barrier by receptor-independent endocytosis.) After uncoating, the viral RNA is released into the cytoplasm and directly translated by host ribosomes via an internal ribosome entry site (IRES). The viral polyprotein is cleaved by viral proteases (e.g. 2A^pro, 3C^pro) into functional units, and replication occurs on membrane-associated complexes.

### Pathophysiology

Following entry and genome release, poliovirus replicates in gut-associated lymphoid tissue and then seeds a transient viremia. In most infections, viremia is brief and cleared by innate and humoral responses, often without symptoms. In a subset of infections (≈5%), virus amplification leads to a secondary, higher-titer viremia. The virus may then access the central nervous system (CNS) by two routes: passage across the blood–brain barrier or via retrograde axonal transport from peripheral motor endplates. CD155 is not involved in crossing the blood–brain barrier; rather, poliovirus enters endosomes in a manner analogous to transferrin uptake. However, at neuromuscular junctions, poliovirus binds CD155 and is endocytosed into motor neuron axons. The virus-containing endosomes are transported retrogradely along axons (via the dynein motor complex using Tctex-1 light chain) to neuronal cell bodies. In susceptible neurons (especially anterior horn cells of the spinal cord and brainstem), poliovirus undergoes lytic replication, causing cell death. This leads to the flaccid paralysis of motor neurons characteristic of paralytic polio. (Strain-specific virulence largely reflects replication efficiency in the CNS.) Importantly, poliovirus does **not** itself typically trigger massive inflammatory damage – neuronal loss is due to direct viral cytopathic effect. Host immune responses (neutralizing antibodies) prevent infection and facilitate viral clearance.

### Clinical Manifestations

The incubation period of poliovirus is about 7–14 days. Over 90–95% of poliovirus infections are **asymptomatic**, limited to a transient viremia. Approximately 4–8% manifest as a non-specific febrile illness (“abortive poliomyelitis”) with symptoms like fever, malaise, sore throat, and gastrointestinal upset, but with full recovery. Around 1–2% of infections progress to **non-paralytic (aseptic) meningitis**, characterized by headache, neck stiffness, and signs of meningeal irritation, following the initial illness. Less than 1% of infections result in **paralytic poliomyelitis**. Paralytic disease typically appears 1–2 weeks after the prodrome. It is marked by an asymmetric, flaccid paralysis with loss of muscle tone and reflexes in one or more limbs, without sensory loss. Commonly, the legs are more affected than the arms. Bulbar polio (involvement of brainstem motor neurons) can lead to cranial nerve palsies, respiratory muscle paralysis, and respiratory failure, which can be fatal. In cases of paralysis, pain and muscle spasms often precede weakness. Infants are relatively protected by maternal antibodies, whereas older children and adults are more likely to become symptomatic upon exposure.

### Complications

The most serious outcome is **permanent paralysis**, which can lead to lifelong disability. Bulbar involvement can cause respiratory arrest (historically leading to “iron lung” use) and death. Among survivors of paralytic polio, some experience **post-polio syndrome** decades later, with new muscle weakness and fatigue in previously affected or even unaffected muscles. Other complications include osteomyelitis in immobilized limbs, joint contractures, and chronic pain. By contrast, abortive and non-paralytic cases have virtually no long-term sequelae. Occasionally, polio can cause viral meningitis or encephalitis without paralysis, but this is rare.

### Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis

Poliovirus infection is confirmed by isolating the virus or viral RNA from clinical specimens. Diagnosis is typically made by PCR or culture of stool (most sensitive) or throat swab, and in paralytic cases also of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). Serologic tests (anti-polio IgM) can support diagnosis. On CSF analysis in paralytic or meningitic cases, lymphocytic pleocytosis and elevated protein are typical (as with other enteroviral meningitides). Differential diagnoses include Guillain–Barré syndrome (acute flaccid paralysis with albuminocytologic dissociation), other enteroviruses (e.g. Echovirus, Coxsackie A), West Nile virus, and other causes of acute flaccid paralysis. Careful attention to epidemiologic risk (travel, vaccination history) aids diagnosis.

### Treatment and Management

There is no specific antiviral therapy for poliovirus. Management of paralytic polio is entirely supportive: respiratory support for ventilatory failure, physical therapy for rehabilitation, and prevention of complications (e.g. contractures). Non-paralytic cases require only symptomatic care (fluids, antipyretics). Immunoglobulin may be given in exposed individuals (especially unvaccinated) as passive prophylaxis. Crucially, poliovirus is controlled by vaccination. The inactivated Salk vaccine (IPV) and live-attenuated Sabin vaccine (OPV) have been used for decades, with the Salk vaccine conferring >80–90% protection. Global eradication efforts rely on high vaccination coverage and surveillance; wild poliovirus now remains endemic only in a few countries.

## Coxsackievirus (Group A and B)

### Introduction

Coxsackieviruses are a heterogeneous group of enteroviruses first isolated in the late 1940s near Coxsackie, New York. They belong to Picornaviridae, genus _Enterovirus_, and are divided into Group A (CVA) and Group B (CVB) based on early mouse pathogenesis studies. Over 23 serotypes of CVA (serotypes 1–24, excluding 23) and 6 serotypes of CVB (1–6) are recognized. Coxsackieviruses share many biochemical and antigenic features with poliovirus and echovirus.

### Characteristics / Profile

Like all picornaviruses, Coxsackieviruses are small (~30 nm), non-enveloped icosahedral viruses with a positive-sense RNA genome (≈7.4 kb) encoding a single polyprotein. Viral replication in cell culture was achieved early in their discovery, enabling serotyping. Transmission is primarily fecal–oral, with some potential for respiratory spread and fomite transmission. The viruses initially replicate in the oropharynx and intestinal tract (tonsils, Peyer’s patches), then enter the bloodstream (viremia) for systemic dissemination. Differential tissue tropism distinguishes group A vs. B: Group A coxsackieviruses preferentially infect skin and mucous membranes, whereas Group B viruses target internal organs. Receptors include the coxsackievirus–adenovirus receptor (CAR) for many group B strains and some group A, and decay-accelerating factor (DAF/CD55) for certain group A strains. Coxsackie infection, like other enteroviruses, is not typically associated with a vigorous innate immune response early, allowing dissemination before symptoms develop.

### Pathophysiology

Following inoculation, Coxsackieviruses replicate in primary sites as above and cause a transient viremia. Group A viruses tend to invade epithelial tissues of the skin, mucosa, and conjunctiva. For example, CVA serotypes that cause herpangina replicate in oropharyngeal epithelium, leading to local lesions and pain. Group B viruses often invade internal tissues: for instance, CVB infects myocardium, pericardium, pleura, and pancreatic islet cells, reflecting CAR expression. The resulting tissue damage is directly cytolytic in infected cells (e.g. myocytes, pleural cells), often accompanied by a lymphocytic inflammatory infiltrate. Innate and adaptive immune responses (type I interferons, virus-neutralizing antibodies) contribute to limiting infection but also to tissue inflammation. Coxsackieviruses can infect the central nervous system (CNS) to cause meningitis, more often with group B but also some group A strains. In neonates, heavy viremia can lead to multiorgan involvement and a sepsis-like illness. Chronic sequelae have been proposed: for example, CVB infection of pancreatic islets and heart has been linked to autoimmunity (e.g. type 1 diabetes, dilated cardiomyopathy), though mechanisms remain under study.

### Clinical Manifestations

Coxsackievirus infections have a wide clinical spectrum, often with overlapping syndromes. Common features (fever, malaise) are non-specific. Group A infections typically produce mucocutaneous diseases:

- **Herpangina** – fever and pharyngeal pain with small vesicles/ulcers on the soft palate or tonsillar pillars (often CVA1–6,8,10,22).
    
- **Hand–Foot–Mouth Disease (HFMD)** – fever with vesicular rash on the hands, feet, and oral mucosa (classically CVA16 and EV-A71, but many CVA can cause HFMD).
    
- **Acute Hemorrhagic Conjunctivitis** – sudden bilateral conjunctival injection and hemorrhages (e.g. CVA24 variant, also enterovirus 70).  
    Group B infections have predilection for internal organs:
    
- **Bornholm (Pleurodynia)** – severe chest or abdominal pain (pleuritic) and fever, often due to CVB5 (also called "Devil’s grip").
    
- **Myocarditis/Pericarditis** – acute chest pain, arrhythmias or heart failure due to CVB (especially CVB1,3) infecting cardiac muscle.
    
- **Pancreatitis/Hepatitis** – mild elevations of amylase or liver enzymes with CVB infection (notably CVB4).  
    Both groups can cause **aseptic meningitis** – headache, stiff neck, vomiting; this is often subclinical or mild, but outbreaks occur (especially in children). Less commonly, Group A (e.g. CVA7,9) can cause encephalitis, and Group B (especially CVB) can cause severe neonatal hepatitis or sepsis-like illness. Rash and nonspecific febrile illness (eg, "summer flu") can accompany either group. Coxsackie B infections in children may also induce transient arthritis or orchitis.
    

### Complications

Most Coxsackievirus infections are self-limited. However, serious complications can occur. CVB myocarditis can progress to dilated cardiomyopathy, requiring transplantation or causing sudden death. CVB-pericarditis can cause cardiac tamponade (rarely). CVA-induced HFMD or herpangina can lead to dehydration or secondary bacterial infection but usually resolves. Chronic complications potentially linked to Coxsackie (though rare) include type 1 diabetes mellitus (autoimmune destruction of islets, notably after CVB4) and chronic dilated cardiomyopathy post-myocarditis. Neonates infected with CVB may suffer disseminated infection with shock and multiorgan failure.

### Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis

Diagnosis of Coxsackievirus infection can be confirmed by virus isolation (culture or PCR) from throat swabs, vesicle fluid (if rash present), stool, or CSF (in meningitis). No routine serologic tests exist clinically. Enteroviral PCR panels are commonly used. Routine laboratory and imaging tests are non-specific; cardiac enzymes and ECG/echo help diagnose myocarditis. Differential diagnosis includes other enteroviruses (e.g. echovirus, enterovirus D71), Herpesviruses (hand–mouth lesions versus herpetic gingivostomatitis), Kawasaki disease (rash and fever), and bacterial causes of conjunctivitis or pneumonia.

### Treatment and Management

There are no specific antiviral drugs for Coxsackieviruses. Management is supportive: hydration and analgesics for herpangina/HFMD; rest and pain control (e.g. NSAIDs) for pleurodynia; myocarditis requires standard heart failure therapy and occasionally immunosuppression or IVIG in fulminant cases. Coxsackievirus infections are prevented primarily by good hygiene (hand-washing) and isolation during outbreaks. No vaccines are available for Coxsackievirus. Because many Coxsackie cases are mild, treatment focuses on symptom relief and monitoring for complications (e.g. cardiac monitoring for chest pain).

## Echovirus

### Introduction

Echoviruses (named for “enteric cytopathic human orphan” viruses) are a large subgroup of enteroviruses first isolated in the 1950s from apparently healthy children. Previously “orphans” with no known disease, they are now recognized as common pathogens. There are 29 recognized echovirus serotypes, making them the largest group within the Enterovirus genus.

### Characteristics / Profile

Echoviruses are non-enveloped, icosahedral picornaviruses with a positive-sense RNA genome. Like other enteroviruses, they have a polyprotein genome processed into VP1–4 and nonstructural proteins. Transmission is via the fecal–oral route, often following viral replication in the gut or upper airway, leading to viremia. Echoviruses show diverse receptor usage (varying by serotype), but generally infect intestinal and neural tissues. Outbreaks often occur in summer/fall.

### Pathophysiology

After ingestion, echoviruses replicate in the oropharynx and intestinal mucosa, then enter the bloodstream (viremia). They may subsequently infect target organs. Neurologic involvement is common: many echoviruses readily cross into the CNS, causing aseptic meningitis or, less often, encephalitis, due to lytic infection of meningeal or neural cells. Some echoviruses infect the heart or skin. In neonates, high viremia can lead to hepatitis, coagulopathy, and shock. Immune response (interferons, antibodies) eventually clears the virus, but the host inflammatory response (especially in the CNS) contributes to symptoms.

### Clinical Manifestations

Echovirus infections vary by serotype and host age. Common presentations include:

- **Aseptic Meningitis:** the most frequent presentation. Fever, headache, nuchal rigidity; CSF shows lymphocytic pleocytosis. Echovirus is a leading cause of viral meningitis in children.
    
- **Rash and Fever (Exanthem):** A maculopapular or urticarial rash may accompany fever, especially in children (e.g. ECHO 18, ECHO 9).
    
- **Hand–Foot–Mouth or Herpangina:** Overlap with Coxsackie A; some echoviruses can cause these syndromes.
    
- **Neonatal Sepsis-like Illness:** In infants <1 month, echovirus can cause disseminated infection with hepatitis, coagulopathy, myocarditis, and meningoencephalitis; this is severe and potentially fatal.
    
- **Otorhinolaryngologic and respiratory illnesses:** Mild pharyngitis or pneumonia sometimes occur.
    

Most echovirus infections are self-limited. Fever typically lasts 3–7 days, followed by recovery.

### Complications

Severe complications of echovirus infection include encephalitis (leading to seizures or neurologic deficits), myopericarditis, hepatitis with hepatic failure (rare), and neonatal multi-organ failure. Coxsackievirus shares some of these tropisms, so complications can overlap (e.g. myocarditis). Hearing loss or visual impairment have been reported after echovirus encephalitis. Mortality is low in older children and adults, but higher in severe neonatal disease.

### Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis

Echovirus infection is diagnosed by isolating virus (culture or RT-PCR) from CSF, stool, throat swab, or blood. Enterovirus PCR on CSF is commonly used for viral meningitis. There is no rapid point-of-care test. In neonates, PCR of blood is critical. Elevated CSF lymphocytes and normal glucose support viral meningitis. Differential includes other causes of aseptic meningitis (enterovirus A/C, mumps, HSV-2, VZV) and bacterial meningitis (though bacterial cultures are negative in aseptic cases). For rash-fever illnesses, differentiate from measles, rubella, and drug reactions.

### Treatment and Management

No specific antiviral therapy exists for echovirus. Care is supportive: hydration and antipyretics, pain relief for headache, and anticonvulsants if needed for seizures. Severe cases (e.g. neonatal hepatitis or myocarditis) may be treated with IVIG or experimental antivirals (pleconaril was studied for enteroviruses but is not licensed). Prevention relies on hygiene to limit fecal–oral spread. Neonatal cases require intensive care support.

## Enterovirus D68 (EV-D68)

### Introduction

Enterovirus D68 is a re-emerging non-polio enterovirus first isolated in 1962 from children with pneumonia. It is grouped in species Enterovirus D of Picornaviridae. After sporadic detections, EV-D68 gained prominence during large biennial respiratory disease outbreaks since 2014, especially in temperate regions. Notably, the 2014 outbreak in the USA coincided with numerous cases of acute flaccid myelitis (AFM), a polio-like paralytic syndrome in children. EV-D68 has thus become a major pediatric public health concern worldwide.

### Characteristics / Profile

EV-D68 is a small (~30 nm), non-enveloped icosahedral virus with a positive-sense RNA genome (~7.2 kb). It is genetically and biologically acid-labile (unlike most other enteroviruses), making it more akin to rhinoviruses; as a result, EV-D68 replicates in the respiratory tract rather than the gut. The genome encodes the usual picornaviral polyprotein. EV-D68 strains cluster into multiple clades, some of which have diverged antigenically from historical strains.

Receptor usage by EV-D68 is complex and still under study. Early isolates (e.g. the prototype “Fermon” strain) bind to sialic acid (α2,6- and α2,3-linked) on respiratory epithelial cells. Contemporary outbreak strains appear to also utilize other attachment factors: sulfated glycosaminoglycans (e.g. heparan sulfate) or unidentified co-receptors. Intercellular adhesion molecule-5 (ICAM-5), expressed on neurons, has been proposed as a receptor facilitating CNS entry, though its exact role is unclear. Overall, EV-D68 likely uses a combination of sialic acid and other receptors (possibly ICAM-5 or glycosaminoglycans) to infect host cells.

### Pathophysiology

EV-D68 is transmitted primarily by respiratory droplets and possibly contact with respiratory secretions; fecal–oral transmission is not a major route. The virus infects the upper (and sometimes lower) respiratory epithelium. After binding to receptors, the virion undergoes receptor-mediated endocytosis, uncoating, and release of its +RNA genome into the cytoplasm. The viral RNA is translated and replicated by the usual picornaviral machinery. The infection induces a pro-inflammatory response in the airway, with cytokine and chemokine release.

In severe cases, EV-D68 can reach the central nervous system. Animal and laboratory models suggest that EV-D68 is **neurotropic**, able to infect motor neurons and astrocytes even in the absence of a known neuronal receptor. It is hypothesized that EV-D68 may enter the CNS either via retrograde axonal transport (similar to poliovirus) or via a yet-unidentified neuroinvasion route. Once in the spinal cord, EV-D68 replicates in anterior horn cells, causing localized inflammation and neuron destruction. This leads to the acute flaccid paralysis of AFM in children. Immune responses (T cells, interferons) contribute to viral clearance but may also mediate bystander damage; neutralizing antibodies correlate with protection.

### Clinical Manifestations

EV-D68 most commonly causes **respiratory illness**, often indistinguishable from other viral bronchiolitis or pneumonia in children. Infected children (<16 years old account for >90% of cases) typically present with fever, cough, wheezing, and respiratory distress, often requiring hospitalization for asthma-like exacerbations. The infection is notable for triggering severe asthma exacerbations in both children and adults due to airway inflammation. During the 2014–2018 outbreaks, up to 10–15% of hospitalized children with EV-D68 had life-threatening respiratory disease with hypoxia.

A small but critical subset of EV-D68-infected children develop **acute flaccid myelitis (AFM)**, a polio-like syndrome. AFM manifests as sudden onset limb weakness (often asymmetric), decreased tone, and absent reflexes, typically following the respiratory illness by 5–10 days. Associated symptoms may include facial weakness, difficulty swallowing, or respiratory failure if spinal cord lesions affect breathing. MRI of AFM patients shows lesions in the anterior horns of the spinal cord gray matter. The proportion of EV-D68 infections leading to AFM is very low (<1%), but the association is clear in epidemiological patterns. Rarely, EV-D68 can cause non-paralytic encephalitis or acute flaccid paralysis without typical MRI findings.

### Complications

The most serious complication is AFM, which can result in residual limb paralysis and disability. Children with EV-D68–associated AFM often have incomplete recovery, with persistent motor deficits even after rehabilitation. In the respiratory domain, complications include bacterial superinfection (otitis media, pneumonia) and, rarely, ARDS requiring ventilatory support. Extrapulmonary complications (e.g. myocarditis) have not been commonly reported for EV-D68. Long-term sequelae of EV-D68 respiratory illness are not well-defined, but it may contribute to chronic reactive airway disease in susceptible individuals.

### Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis

EV-D68 infection is confirmed by RT-PCR testing of respiratory specimens (nasopharyngeal swab, sputum) using enterovirus-specific assays, often followed by sequencing to distinguish EV-D68. There are no widely available rapid tests for EV-D68 specifically. In AFM cases, EV-D68 RNA is often _not_ detected in the CSF, so diagnosis relies on respiratory samples. Standard CSF studies in AFM typically show mild pleocytosis and elevated protein. Radiologic studies (MRI of the spinal cord) are crucial for AFM diagnosis. Differential diagnoses for severe respiratory illness include influenza, RSV, and rhinovirus. For AFM, differentials include poliovirus (now rare), non-polio enteroviruses (e.g. EV-A71), adenovirus, West Nile virus, Guillain–Barré syndrome, and transverse myelitis.

### Treatment and Management

There is no specific antiviral treatment for EV-D68. Management of respiratory disease is supportive: oxygen, bronchodilators, steroids (for asthma exacerbation), and mechanical ventilation if needed. Standard viral prophylaxis (oseltamivir, etc.) does not apply. For AFM, treatment is also supportive: respiratory support and intensive physiotherapy. Intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) and steroids have been tried empirically in AFM, but their efficacy is unproven. Research on antiviral drugs (e.g. pleconaril-like capsid inhibitors or protease inhibitors) and vaccines for EV-D68 is ongoing, but none are yet available. Prevention relies on infection control (hand hygiene, avoiding exposure when ill). There is no EV-D68 vaccine.

## Rhinovirus

### Introduction

Rhinoviruses (human rhinoviruses, HRVs) are the predominant cause of the “common cold.” Over 150 serotypes are classified into three species (Rhinovirus A, B, C) within the genus Enterovirus of Picornaviridae. Rhinoviruses were discovered in the 1950s in nasal washings from patients with colds and have since been shown to infect all ages, causing upper respiratory tract (URT) disease. Rhinovirus infections are ubiquitous; virtually everyone is infected multiple times in childhood and adulthood.

### Characteristics / Profile

Rhinoviruses are small (≈30 nm), non-enveloped viruses with a positive-sense RNA genome (~7.2 kb). They have an icosahedral capsid composed of 60 protomers of VP1–VP4. Over 100 serotypes (now >150) exist, and they are traditionally divided by cell receptor usage into major and minor groups. Major-group rhinoviruses (∼90% of serotypes, mainly species A and B) bind to ICAM-1 on respiratory epithelium, while minor-group serotypes bind to the low-density lipoprotein receptor (LDLR). Rhinovirus C (discovered in 2006) uses a distinct receptor, cadherin-related family member 3 (CDHR3).

Rhinoviruses replicate best at the cooler temperatures (~33–35 °C) of the nasal mucosa; they are acid-labile and cannot survive stomach acidity. Transmission is via inhalation of respiratory droplets or direct contact (hands, fomites) with contaminated secretions. People with colds shed virus abundantly from the nose. The virus infects and replicates in ciliated respiratory epithelial cells from the nasopharynx down to the bronchi.

### Pathophysiology

Rhinovirus infection is usually restricted to the upper respiratory tract due to its temperature sensitivity, although it can infect lower airways in some cases. Entry is by receptor-mediated endocytosis (ICAM-1 or LDLR binding). After uncoating, the +RNA genome is translated and replicated as in other picornaviruses. Infection of epithelial cells triggers an innate immune response. Viral binding and replication induce the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines and chemokines (e.g. IL-8, RANTES, IFNs) from airway cells, leading to vasodilation, plasma extravasation, and attraction of inflammatory cells. The congestion and rhinorrhea of the common cold are largely due to this host inflammatory response rather than direct cytolysis. The innate response helps clear the virus in 5–7 days. Adaptive immunity (serotype-specific neutralizing antibodies) follows and prevents immediate reinfection by the same serotype.

### Clinical Manifestations

Rhinoviruses cause the classic **common cold**. Incubation is 1–3 days. Symptoms peak at 2–3 days and usually last about a week. Typical manifestations include:

- **Rhinorrhea and congestion** – runny or stuffy nose (most prominent symptom).
    
- **Sneezing and nasal irritation.**
    
- **Sore throat** – usually mild.
    
- **Cough** – occurs in ~30% of cases, sometimes productive.
    
- **Mild constitutional symptoms** – low-grade fever (uncommon in adults, up to 30–40% of children may have fever), malaise, headache.
    

Complications (in about 10–20% of cases) can include acute otitis media (especially in children), sinusitis, and lower respiratory symptoms (wheezing, bronchitis) in susceptible hosts. Rhinovirus is a leading trigger of asthma exacerbations and COPD exacerbations because it can infect lower airway epithelium. Although generally mild, rhinovirus infection can occasionally lead to severe lower respiratory tract disease (e.g. pneumonia) in immunocompromised patients. Rhinovirus C has been associated with more severe asthma flares in children.

### Complications

The common cold is usually benign, but complications arise from secondary bacterial infection or host factors. Otitis media and sinusitis are frequent sequelae. In individuals with reactive airway disease, rhinovirus often precipitates asthma exacerbations or reactive bronchospasm. Rarely, viral spread to the lower respiratory tract causes bronchitis or pneumonia. In immunosuppressed persons (e.g. transplant recipients), rhinovirus can cause prolonged shedding and more severe pneumonia.

### Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis

Routine clinical diagnosis of rhinovirus is by symptom pattern; laboratory testing is seldom performed except in research or outbreak settings. Molecular multiplex PCR panels can detect rhinovirus/enterovirus in nasal swabs, but distinguishing rhinovirus from other enteroviruses by PCR alone may require sequencing. Differential diagnosis for common-cold syndrome includes other viruses causing upper respiratory symptoms (adenovirus, coronavirus, RSV, parainfluenza, metapneumovirus). The lack of high fever and predominant nasal symptoms are clues favoring rhinovirus. In asthma flares, rhinovirus is often identified as the trigger.

### Treatment and Management

There is no specific antiviral against rhinovirus. Treatment is symptomatic: nasal saline, decongestants, analgesics, and rest. Antihistamines have limited effect except for relieving rhinorrhea. Ipratropium nasal spray can reduce rhinorrhea. Evidence for vitamin C, zinc, or echinacea is weak. Hand hygiene is effective prevention. Several antiviral compounds (e.g. pleconaril, capsid-binding agents) have been investigated but none are approved. Given the large number of serotypes, a vaccine is impractical. Management focuses on supportive care and preventing spread.

## Hepatitis A Virus (HAV)

### Introduction

Hepatitis A virus (HAV) is a hepatotropic picornavirus and the prototype of the genus _Hepatovirus_. It was identified in the early 1970s as the agent of “infectious hepatitis” using immune-electron microscopy. HAV is transmitted by the fecal–oral route, typically through ingestion of contaminated food or water, or by close personal contact. In contrast to hepatitis B and C viruses, HAV infection is acute and self-limited; it does **not** cause chronic hepatitis. Outbreaks have been described for centuries, often associated with poor sanitation. An effective inactivated vaccine has been available since 1995.

### Characteristics / Profile

HAV is a small, non-enveloped icosahedral virus about 27–32 nm in diameter. Its positive-sense RNA genome (~7.5 kb) encodes a polyprotein that is processed into structural (capsid VP1–4) and nonstructural proteins, similar to other picornaviruses. A unique feature of HAV is its quasi-enveloped life cycle: virions are released from hepatocytes cloaked in host-derived membrane in blood (“eHAV”) and become non-enveloped in the biliary tract and feces. The cellular receptor for HAV was long thought to be TIM1/HAVCR1, but recent studies show TIM1 is not essential for entry. TIM1 may facilitate uptake of quasi-enveloped HAV by binding phosphatidylserine on the viral membrane. Thus, the exact entry receptor remains uncertain.

After ingestion, HAV initially replicates in intestinal epithelial cells and Kupffer cells, then reaches the liver via portal blood. The incubation period is long, typically 4–6 weeks. Viral replication in hepatocytes is slow and non-cytolytic. Notably, HAV does not cause rapid cytopathic effect in cultured cells, consistent with the prolonged prodrome and incubation. The bulk of liver injury in hepatitis A is immune-mediated. Cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTLs) targeting HAV-infected hepatocytes induce apoptosis and inflammation. Recent studies also implicate innate effectors (NK, NKT cells) and even bystander (non–HAV-specific) CD8+ T cells in hepatocellular damage. Genetic factors (e.g. polymorphisms in TIM1 or IL-18 binding protein) influence disease severity.

### Clinical Manifestations

HAV infection causes acute hepatitis. After the long incubation, patients typically experience a prodrome of fever, malaise, anorexia, nausea, vomiting, and abdominal discomfort (especially right upper quadrant) for several days. Dark urine and light-colored stools often appear as bilirubin rises. Jaundice (yellowing of skin/eyes) usually emerges early in adults (up to 70–80%), though many young children (<6 yrs) remain asymptomatic or exhibit only mild illness. Physical exam may reveal hepatomegaly and tender liver. Laboratory tests show marked elevations of alanine aminotransferase (ALT) and aspartate aminotransferase (AST), often >1,000 IU/L, reflecting hepatocellular injury. The appearance of anti-HAV IgM antibody in serum coincides with symptom onset and ALT rise. Peak illness lasts 1–2 weeks, followed by a slow convalescence over 1–3 months during which ALT normalizes. Full recovery is the rule, leaving lifelong immunity.

In HAV infection, jaundice and systemic symptoms are more pronounced than in other picornaviral infections. The clinical spectrum ranges from subclinical (especially in children) to fulminant hepatitis. In fulminant cases, patients develop acute liver failure (encephalopathy, coagulopathy, very high bilirubin) within days. However, fulminant hepatitis A is rare (estimated ~0.1–0.3% of cases). Case-fatality overall is low; WHO estimates ~7,000 deaths globally in 2016 (0.5% of viral hepatitis deaths).

### Complications

Aside from rare fulminant hepatic failure, other complications include prolonged cholestatic hepatitis (weeks to months of jaundice and pruritus), relapsing hepatitis A (recurrence of symptoms weeks after initial recovery), and extrahepatic manifestations (e.g. rash, arthralgias, renal injury). Fulminant hepatitis, if it occurs, can be fatal or require liver transplantation. Post-infectious or relapsing cholestatic hepatitis is thought to be immune-mediated. There is no chronic hepatitis A; after acute infection, virus is cleared completely, and anti-HAV IgG confers lifelong immunity.

### Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis

HAV is diagnosed serologically. The hallmark is anti-HAV IgM, which appears at symptom onset and persists for ~3–6 months. Detection of IgM HAV antibody in serum confirms acute infection. Elevated liver enzymes (ALT/AST) and bilirubin support clinical hepatitis. HAV RNA can be detected by PCR in stool and blood early in infection but is not routinely done clinically. Differentiating hepatitis A from hepatitis B, C, E, or other causes relies on specific viral serologies (e.g. HBsAg/anti-HBc, HCV RNA, anti-HEV). Other differentials include drug-induced liver injury, autoimmune hepatitis, and other infections (EBV, CMV). In a patient with jaundice and elevated ALT, ordering a hepatitis panel including HAV IgM is standard. A mild HAV infection without jaundice in children may mimic viral gastroenteritis – here, testing is based on epidemiologic suspicion.

### Treatment and Management

There is no specific antiviral for HAV; management is supportive. Hospitalize patients with severe nausea, vomiting, or fulminant course. Provide rest, nutrition, and fluids. Avoid hepatotoxic drugs (e.g. acetaminophen). In fulminant cases, transfer to liver transplant center and consider transplant if encephalopathy worsens. For prevention and post-exposure, purified immune globulin (IG) or hepatitis A vaccine can be given within 2 weeks of exposure to susceptible persons. Vaccination (inactivated HAV vaccine) is highly effective for prevention and is routine in many countries. Outbreak control also relies on sanitary measures (clean water, proper food handling). Individuals typically develop immunity after infection or vaccination and do not require further therapy.
