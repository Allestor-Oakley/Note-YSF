
## Antiretroviral Drugs

**Tenofovir (TDF/TAF, NRTI).** Tenofovir is a nucleotide reverse-transcriptase inhibitor used in HIV and HBV. It is administered as prodrugs – tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (TDF) or tenofovir alafenamide (TAF) – that are converted intracellularly to active tenofovir‐diphosphate, which competes with deoxyadenosine triphosphate to cause viral DNA chain termination. Tenofovir (especially TAF) has become a first-line NRTI backbone in HIV therapy (e.g. in combination with emtricitabine or lamivudine) and a recommended first-line therapy for chronic hepatitis B. It is favored for its high potency and once-daily dosing, but choice between TDF and TAF depends on patient factors: TAF yields higher intracellular levels with ~90% lower plasma exposure and thus less renal/bone toxicity. Tenofovir is preferred over older NRTIs (e.g. lamivudine) due to its high barrier to HBV resistance and synergistic use in fixed-dose combos for HIV.

**Mechanism:** Tenofovir’s active diphosphate form inhibits HIV reverse transcriptase and HBV polymerase by chain termination.

**Adverse Effects:** Generally well tolerated; common AEs include nausea, headache, fatigue, rash and GI upset. TDF can cause dose-related renal toxicity (Fanconi syndrome) and bone mineral loss, whereas TAF is associated with elevated lipids and gastrointestinal symptoms. Rarely, NRTIs can cause lactic acidosis with hepatic steatosis.

**Pharmacodynamics/Pharmacokinetics:** TAF and TDF differ in PK: oral bioavailability of tenofovir is low, and both are prodrugs. TDF is rapidly hydrolyzed in plasma; TAF is stable in plasma and cleaved intracellularly by cathepsin A in lymphocytes. TAF produces ~10-fold higher intracellular tenofovir–diphosphate levels in target cells with ~90% less plasma TFV than TDF. Plasma half-life of active metabolite is long (∼150 hours in PBMCs), allowing once-daily dosing. Renal clearance is primary elimination route; >30% of drug excreted unchanged in urine.

**Drug Interactions:** Minimal CYP metabolism means fewer interactions. However, TAF is a P‑gp substrate; strong P-gp/inducers (e.g. rifampin, carbamazepine, St. John’s wort) markedly reduce TAF/TAF efficacy. Concomitant nephrotoxins (e.g. NSAIDs, cidofovir) may raise tenofovir levels and nephrotoxicity. Drugs that raise gastric pH have little effect; TAF absorption is enhanced by food.

**Efavirenz (NNRTI).** Efavirenz is an early-generation non-nucleoside reverse-transcriptase inhibitor approved in 1998. It was long a cornerstone of first-line HIV therapy (often in fixed-dose combination with TDF/FTC) but is now largely supplanted by integrase inhibitors due to its side-effect profile. Efavirenz is still used in resource-limited settings or select regimens, but it is **contraindicated in pregnancy** (teratogenic risk) and in neuropsychiatric disorders.

**Mechanism:** Efavirenz binds an allosteric (non-catalytic) pocket of HIV-1 reverse transcriptase, inducing a conformational change that inhibits DNA polymerase activity. It is a noncompetitive inhibitor and does not require intracellular activation.

**Adverse Effects:** Common are CNS effects (dizziness, vivid dreams, insomnia, impaired concentration) and rash. Fatigue, headache, nausea, and elevated lipids also occur. Efavirenz can rarely cause hepatotoxicity or worsening of psychiatric illness, and QT prolongation. Because of neuropsychiatric toxicity, dosing is recommended at bedtime on an empty stomach to mitigate side effects.

**Pharmacodynamics/Pharmacokinetics:** Efavirenz is well absorbed (peak ~5 h post-dose), highly protein-bound, and has a long half-life (~45–55 h) allowing once-daily dosing. It is extensively metabolized by CYP2B6 (and CYP3A4) to inactive metabolites.

**Drug Interactions:** Efavirenz is a **potent inducer** of CYP3A4 and CYP2B6, and also inhibits CYP2C9/19. This leads to many interactions: e.g. it reduces levels of protease inhibitors and certain statins, and is contraindicated with rifampin (which further induces CYPs). Conversely, CYP inhibitors (e.g. azoles) raise efavirenz levels. Efavirenz should not be coadministered with drugs metabolized by CYP2B6/3A4 without dose adjustment.

**Darunavir (Protease Inhibitor).** Darunavir (Prezista) is a second-generation HIV-1 protease inhibitor approved in 2006. It is used in combination with ritonavir or cobicistat (pharmacokinetic boosters) to achieve adequate plasma levels. Darunavir is indicated for treatment-experienced or naive HIV-infected adults and in post-exposure prophylaxis. It has a high genetic barrier to resistance and is often chosen in patients with prior PI resistance.

**Mechanism:** Darunavir reversibly inhibits the HIV-1 protease enzyme, preventing cleavage of Gag-Pol polyprotein into mature structural proteins. This yields non-infectious, immature virions.

**Adverse Effects:** Gastrointestinal upset (diarrhea, nausea, abdominal pain), headache, and rash are common. It can cause hepatic dysfunction and metabolic derangements (e.g. hyperlipidemia, insulin resistance) typical of PIs. A rare but serious hypersensitivity reaction (“sulfa rash” or Stevens-Johnson syndrome) has been reported.

**Pharmacodynamics/Pharmacokinetics:** Darunavir alone has moderate bioavailability (37%), but coformulation with ritonavir boosts oral bioavailability to ~82%. It is ~95% protein-bound, metabolized by CYP3A4, and has a half-life ~15 h when boosted, permitting once- or twice-daily dosing. It is excreted mainly via feces, with minimal renal elimination.

**Drug Interactions:** Because darunavir is **a CYP3A4 substrate**, coadministration with strong CYP3A4 inducers (e.g. rifampin, St. John’s wort) will dramatically lower its levels and is contraindicated. Conversely, coadministration with CYP3A4 inhibitors (e.g. ketoconazole) raises darunavir levels. Boosting with ritonavir/cobicistat is required to inhibit CYP3A4 and maintain effective levels. Darunavir coadministered with HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors like simvastatin or lovastatin can raise statin levels (muscle toxicity); such combinations should be avoided. The PI class tends to interact with many drugs via CYP3A4.

**Dolutegravir (INSTI).** Dolutegravir (DTG, Tivicay) is a second-generation HIV-1 integrase strand-transfer inhibitor approved in 2013. It is now a preferred component of first-line and second-line regimens because of its potency, once-daily dosing, and high resistance barrier (often co-formulated with lamivudine and/or abacavir). It is also used for post-exposure prophylaxis. WHO guidelines list DTG as preferred first- and second-line agent.

**Mechanism:** DTG binds the active site of HIV integrase, preventing strand transfer of viral DNA into the host genome.

**Adverse Effects:** DTG is generally well tolerated. Common AEs include insomnia, headache, diarrhea, fatigue, and increases in serum creatinine (via OCT2 inhibition, without true GFR reduction). Uncommonly, it can cause hypersensitivity or hepatotoxicity. Early concerns about neural-tube defects in pregnancy have largely been refuted, but caution is advised in women of childbearing potential.

**Pharmacodynamics/Pharmacokinetics:** DTG is highly protein-bound (≥98.9%), metabolized primarily by UGT1A1 with minor CYP3A involvement. It has a terminal elimination half-life of ~14 h. Oral bioavailability is not significantly affected by food; the drug maintains plasma levels above the in vitro IC₉₀ for >30 h after dosing. DTG penetrates well into cerebrospinal fluid.

**Drug Interactions:** Dolutegravir is not a major CYP inducer/inhibitor, so has fewer interactions. However, **cations** (Mg²⁺, Ca²⁺) in antacids, vitamin supplements or buffered drugs can chelate DTG and markedly reduce its absorption (administer DTG 2 h before or 6 h after such agents). Potent CYP/UGT inducers (rifampin) reduce DTG levels; if coadministered, DTG dose should be doubled (50 mg BID). No dose adjustment is needed for CYP inhibitors or mild-to-moderate hepatic impairment. DTG is contraindicated with dofetilide (risk of elevated dofetilide levels).

**Maraviroc (CCR5 Antagonist).** Maraviroc (Selzentry) is a CCR5 chemokine-receptor antagonist approved in 2007. It is indicated only for CCR5-tropic HIV-1 (requiring a tropism test before use) and is reserved for treatment-experienced patients or those who cannot tolerate other classes. Before therapy, a CCR5-tropism assay must confirm absence of CXCR4-tropic virus. Maraviroc may be used as part of combination regimens and is also indicated for post-exposure prophylaxis (though less commonly used for this).

**Mechanism:** Maraviroc selectively and reversibly binds the host CCR5 receptor on CD4⁺ cells, blocking interaction with gp120 and thereby preventing viral entry. It has no activity against CXCR4-tropic HIV or influenza/other viruses.

**Adverse Effects:** Generally well tolerated. Common AEs include cough, fever, rash, dizziness, and GI upset. Rarely, hepatotoxicity with hypersensitivity (similar to benoxaprofen-like reaction) can occur; liver enzymes should be monitored. Patients should also be monitored for signs of infection or allergic reactions.

**Pharmacodynamics/Pharmacokinetics:** Maraviroc is well absorbed (bioavailability ~23%); it is ~76% protein-bound and extensively metabolized by CYP3A4 (and P-glycoprotein). Its half-life is ~14–18 h. Dosing must be adjusted when combined with CYP3A4 modulators (e.g. 150 mg BID with ritonavir vs. 300 mg BID alone). It is excreted mainly in feces (unchanged drug) with minor renal elimination (renally adjusted in severe impairment).

**Drug Interactions:** Because of CYP3A4 metabolism, strong CYP3A4 inhibitors (e.g. ketoconazole) require dose reduction of maraviroc, while strong inducers (rifampin, St. John’s wort) are contraindicated (they greatly lower maraviroc levels). Co-administration with other CCR5 ligands or immunosuppressants is generally avoided. Maraviroc is contraindicated in patients with ESRD (CrCl<30 mL/min) if also taking potent CYP3A modulators. No significant interactions with statins or oral contraceptives have been reported.

## Anti-Hepatitis B Agents

**Tenofovir (TDF/TAF).** (See Antiretroviral section above.) Tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (Viread) and tenofovir alafenamide (Vemlidy) are first-line therapy for chronic hepatitis B. They provide potent viral suppression; clinical trials show ∼95% of patients achieving undetectable HBV DNA by week 48. Tenofovir has a high barrier to resistance and is preferred over lamivudine or adefovir. TAF is often chosen over TDF in patients with renal or bone disease due to its improved safety profile.

**Entecavir (ETV).** Entecavir (Baraclude) is a cyclopentyl guanosine nucleoside analog approved in 2005. It is a first-line agent for chronic HBV (with potent activity against lamivudine-resistant strains as well). Entecavir is dosed once daily (0.5–1 mg) and requires phosphorylation to the active triphosphate, which competes with dGTP to inhibit HBV DNA polymerase (reverse transcriptase), causing chain termination. Indications include chronic HBV with active replication; it must _not_ be used as monotherapy in HBV/HIV coinfected patients due to HIV resistance risk.

**Adverse Effects:** Entecavir is generally well tolerated; side effects are infrequent. Occasionally headache, fatigue or GI upset occur. Because it is renally excreted, dose adjustment is needed in renal impairment. Rarely, lactic acidosis (NRTI class effect) can occur. Withdrawal of therapy can cause HBV flares, so monitoring after discontinuation is critical.

**Pharmacodynamics/Pharmacokinetics:** Oral entecavir has near-100% bioavailability (fasted) and low protein binding. It is predominantly renally excreted unchanged. Its plasma half-life is long (∼128–149 h) owing to intracellular triphosphate retention; the effective half-life (~24 h) allows daily dosing. Volume of distribution is moderate (~23 L). Entecavir does not significantly inhibit or induce CYP enzymes, so drug interactions are minimal (other than with renally excreted drugs).

**Resistance:** Entecavir has a high barrier to resistance in nucleoside-naïve HBV – <2% at 5 years. However, in lamivudine-resistant HBV (YMDD mutants), entecavir resistance emerges rapidly (≈50% at 5 years). This underscores preferring tenofovir over entecavir in lamivudine-experienced patients.

**Other HBV Drugs:** Lamivudine and emtricitabine are older HBV NRTIs but are largely supplanted due to high resistance rates. Adefovir and telbivudine are rarely used now.

## Anti-Hepatitis C Agents (Direct-Acting Antivirals)

Hepatitis C is treated with combination regimens of direct-acting antivirals (DAAs). We highlight representative drugs from each class:

**Sofosbuvir (NS5B polymerase inhibitor).** Sofosbuvir (Sovaldi) is a uridine nucleotide analog that inhibits the NS5B RNA-dependent RNA polymerase of HCV. Approved in 2013, it is a backbone of many regimens across genotypes. Sofosbuvir is administered orally once daily; it is a prodrug converted in hepatocytes to the active triphosphate, which causes chain termination of HCV RNA. In clinical practice, it is used only in combination (e.g. with ledipasvir or velpatasvir). Sofosbuvir is recommended for all genotypes (often pangenotypic regimens) and shows >90–95% sustained virologic response (cure) rates in most patient populations.

**Adverse Effects:** Sofosbuvir is generally well tolerated. Common AEs are fatigue, headache, nausea, insomnia. It can reactivate latent hepatitis B, so HBV screening is necessary. Bradycardia (sometimes fatal) has been reported when co-administered with amiodarone (especially in regimens containing NS5A inhibitors).

**Pharmacodynamics/Pharmacokinetics:** Oral bioavailability is ~80%. Sofosbuvir’s plasma half-life is short (~0.4 h), but its inactive metabolite (GS-331007) has a long half-life (~27 h) and is the predominant circulating form. The drug is 61–65% protein-bound. Sofosbuvir is metabolized in the liver (via CES1) to GS-331007, which is renally excreted (≈80% of dose). No significant CYP-mediated interactions occur; however, strong P-gp or BCRP inducers (rifampin, St. John’s wort) markedly reduce sofosbuvir levels.

**Drug Interactions:** Sofosbuvir has a low propensity for interactions. The major warning is coadministration with amiodarone (as above). Otherwise, no major CYP interactions are seen.

**Ledipasvir (NS5A inhibitor).** Ledipasvir is an HCV NS5A replication complex inhibitor (part of Harvoni with sofosbuvir). It is active primarily against genotypes 1, 4, 5, 6. In Harvoni (ledipasvir 90 mg/sofosbuvir 400 mg), it achieved >95% cure rates in treatment-naïve genotype 1 patients. Ledipasvir inhibits the NS5A protein, disrupting viral replication and assembly.

**Adverse Effects:** As part of sofosbuvir/ledipasvir, it is very well tolerated. The most frequent side effects are fatigue and headache. There are no significant additional toxicities known for ledipasvir alone.

**Pharmacokinetics:** Ledipasvir is extremely protein-bound (>99%). It has a long half-life (~47 h), allowing once-daily dosing. It is minimally metabolized (not via CYP), and mostly eliminated unchanged in feces.

**Drug Interactions:** Ledipasvir is a substrate of P-gp and BCRP. Potent P-gp inducers (rifampin, St. John’s wort) significantly decrease ledipasvir levels. Patients on such drugs should not take ledipasvir-containing regimens. Ledipasvir’s absorption is pH-dependent, so antacids or PPIs can reduce its bioavailability (separate dosing is recommended). Clinically, ledipasvir increases tenofovir exposures (in fixed combos), so caution is needed when coadministering with TDF (monitor renal function). No significant CYP interactions.

**Glecaprevir (NS3/4A protease inhibitor).** Glecaprevir is an oral HCV NS3/4A protease inhibitor, used in combination with the NS5A inhibitor pibrentasvir (as Mavyret). It was FDA-approved in 2017 for all major genotypes (1–6). Glecaprevir blocks the HCV serine protease, preventing cleavage of viral polyprotein and blocking replication. It is active against wild-type and many resistant variants, giving it a high resistance barrier.

**Adverse Effects:** Common AEs (with pibrentasvir) include headache, fatigue, nausea, pruritus and diarrhea. It is generally well tolerated. A transient rise in liver enzymes can occur, so LFTs are monitored.

**Pharmacokinetics:** Oral glecaprevir has increased absorption with food; steady state is reached in ~5 days. It is highly protein-bound (>98%). Metabolism is primarily via CYP3A4, and elimination is almost entirely fecal (biliary excretion). The effective half-life is relatively short (~6 h). No renal dosing adjustment is needed (only trace renal excretion). Pibrentasvir extends overall regimen efficacy against NS5A variants.

**Drug Interactions:** Glecaprevir is both a substrate and inhibitor of drug transporters (P-gp, BCRP, OATP1B1/3). Strong inducers of CYP3A4/P-gp (rifampin, St. John’s wort) greatly reduce glecaprevir levels and are contraindicated. Co-administration with atazanavir (a UGT1A inhibitor) is contraindicated due to hyperbilirubinemia risk. Statins (especially simvastatin) should be avoided or dose-reduced, as glecaprevir can increase statin levels. Glecaprevir is also a weak CYP1A2 inhibitor; no major CYP interactions otherwise.

## Anti-Influenza Drugs

**Oseltamivir (Neuraminidase Inhibitor).** Oseltamivir (Tamiflu) is an oral neuraminidase inhibitor active against influenza A and B. It is FDA-approved for treatment and prophylaxis in children ≥2 weeks and adults, best initiated within 48 h of symptom onset. Oseltamivir is a prodrug (phosphate ester) rapidly converted in the liver to oseltamivir carboxylate, the active metabolite. It blocks the viral neuraminidase enzyme, preventing release of progeny virions from infected cells. Clinical use shortens symptom duration by ~1 day and reduces complications (hospitalization, pneumonia).

**Adverse Effects:** Oseltamivir is generally well tolerated. Common side effects are gastrointestinal (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea) and headache. Rarely, neuropsychiatric events (confusion, abnormal behavior) have been reported in children. Hypersensitivity reactions and cutaneous eruptions (e.g. Stevens-Johnson syndrome) are very rare.

**Pharmacodynamics/Pharmacokinetics:** Oral bioavailability is ~80%. The prodrug reaches peak levels in 1–3 h. Volume of distribution is ~23–26 L, and protein binding is low (42%). Oseltamivir carboxylate (active) has a half-life of ~6–10 h; it is eliminated >99% via renal excretion by glomerular filtration and tubular secretion. Dose adjustment is required for creatinine clearance <30 mL/min. Food does not significantly affect absorption, and it can be taken without regard to meals.

**Drug Interactions:** Few. Oseltamivir’s activation is by carboxylesterase-1; coadministered clopidogrel may inhibit this conversion, reducing efficacy. Oseltamivir does not inhibit or induce CYP enzymes. Concomitant use with the live attenuated influenza vaccine (administered 48 h before or 14 days after) is discouraged, as oseltamivir may interfere with vaccine virus replication.

**Zanamivir (Neuraminidase Inhibitor).** Zanamivir (Relenza) is an inhaled neuraminidase inhibitor for influenza A/B, given via inhaler. It is indicated for acute uncomplicated influenza in patients ≥7 years (treatment) or ≥5 years (prophylaxis) without underlying lung disease. Its mechanism is identical to oseltamivir’s (neuraminidase blockade). Because delivery is by dry powder inhalation, zanamivir has low systemic absorption; bioavailability is low (~10–20%).

**Adverse Effects:** The main concern is bronchospasm, especially in patients with asthma or COPD – use is contraindicated in such individuals. Common AEs include headache, throat irritation, cough, and nasal symptoms. Like oseltamivir, central neuropsychiatric events have been reported very rarely.

**Pharmacokinetics:** After inhalation, zanamivir peaks in plasma within 1–2 h; half-life is ~2–5 h. It is mostly excreted unchanged in urine. Because systemic exposure is low, drug interactions are minimal.

**Baloxavir Marboxil (Endonuclease Inhibitor).** Baloxavir (Xofluza) is a novel oral antiviral approved in 2018 for acute influenza A or B in patients ≥12 years of age, given as a single dose. It inhibits the cap-dependent endonuclease of the viral polymerase, blocking "cap-snatching" and viral mRNA synthesis. This host-polymerase-directed mechanism differs from neuraminidase inhibitors. Baloxavir shortens symptom duration by ~1–2 days.

**Adverse Effects:** Generally well tolerated. The most common side effects (in trials) were diarrhea, bronchitis, nausea, sinusitis, and headache. Hypersensitivity is rare. Notably, resistance-associated viral variants have been observed (particularly in treated children) but clinical significance is variable.

**Pharmacokinetics:** Baloxavir is administered as a prodrug; it is quickly converted to active baloxavir acid, whose plasma half-life is very long (~79–100 h). This allows a single-dose regimen (40 mg or 80 mg depending on weight). Baloxavir acid is highly protein-bound (~93–95%) and cleared primarily via hepatic metabolism (UGT1A3 and minor CYP3A4) and fecal excretion.

**Drug Interactions:** Absorption of baloxavir is **significantly reduced by polyvalent cations**. It should not be taken with dairy products, antacids, laxatives or supplements containing calcium, magnesium, aluminum, iron, etc. (administration should be separated). Baloxavir may modestly inhibit OATP1B1/3, but clinically relevant drug interactions are limited. No major CYP interactions have been identified, aside from minor effects.

## Anti-Herpes Medications

**Acyclovir/Valacyclovir (HSV/VZV antivirals).** Acyclovir (Zovirax) is the prototypical herpesvirus inhibitor. It is a guanosine analog that requires phosphorylation by viral thymidine kinase (TK) and then host kinases to become active. Acyclovir-triphosphate competitively inhibits viral DNA polymerase and causes chain termination. FDA-approved uses include genital herpes and HSV encephalitis; off-label, it treats chickenpox, shingles, and mucocutaneous herpes. Valacyclovir (Valtrex) is a prodrug with ~3–5× greater oral bioavailability (∼50%), allowing less frequent dosing.

**Indications:** Acyclovir is first-line for HSV-1, HSV-2, and VZV infections. It is the drug of choice for HSV encephalitis and neonatal herpes. Oral dosing is used for mucocutaneous disease and prophylaxis; IV therapy is used for severe or CNS infections. Prophylactic or suppressive regimens are used in immunocompromised patients or frequently recurring HSV. Valacyclovir is often used for outpatient herpes zoster and HSV genital therapy, given its dosing convenience.

**Adverse Effects:** Acyclovir is well tolerated. Oral therapy’s common AEs are malaise, headache, nausea and abdominal pain. Topical application can cause irritation. IV acyclovir can cause **nephrotoxicity** (crystal-induced acute kidney injury) if infused too rapidly or without hydration; hence it must be given over ≥1 hour with IV fluids. Rarely, neurotoxicity (confusion, tremors, seizures) occurs, especially in renal failure or high doses. Hypersensitivity is rare. Reversible nephrotoxicity can also occur with valacyclovir at very high doses.

**Pharmacodynamics/Pharmacokinetics:** Acyclovir’s oral bioavailability is low (~10–20%), whereas valacyclovir’s is ~54%. Peak plasma levels of acyclovir occur 1–2 h after oral dosing. Plasma half-life is ~2.5–3.3 h (doubled in renal impairment). It is 15–30% protein-bound and penetrates the CNS. Elimination is primarily renal (~90% unchanged in urine), so dosing must be reduced in renal dysfunction.

**Drug Interactions:** Few significant interactions. Probenecid reduces renal excretion of acyclovir, raising its levels. Other nephrotoxins (NSAIDs, cyclosporine, tacrolimus) increase risk of kidney injury. Concomitant zidovudine may slightly increase hematologic toxicity, and mycophenolate with valganciclovir/ganciclovir adds myelosuppression risk (see below).

**Ganciclovir/Valganciclovir (Anti-CMV).** Ganciclovir (Cytovene) is a guanosine analog active against CMV; it requires phosphorylation by a CMV-encoded kinase (UL97) and by host kinases to the active triphosphate. It inhibits viral DNA polymerase. Valganciclovir (Valcyte) is an oral prodrug (L-valine ester) with much better bioavailability (≈60%). Indications include CMV retinitis in AIDS patients, CMV prophylaxis/treatment in transplant recipients, and congenital CMV. Ganciclovir is also used for resistant HSV/VZV in rare cases.

**Adverse Effects:** The dose-limiting toxicity is severe bone marrow suppression: leukopenia, neutropenia (up to 40% of bone marrow transplant patients), thrombocytopenia and anemia are common. GI upset (nausea, diarrhea) and CNS effects (headache, confusion) can occur. Nephrotoxicity is less common than with acyclovir but can occur, especially if combined with other nephrotoxins. Genetic polymorphisms in UL97 (or UL54 polymerase) can confer resistance in CMV.

**Pharmacokinetics:** Oral ganciclovir has very poor bioavailability (6–10%); valganciclovir overcomes this (≈60%). Peak levels are reached in ~1 h (ganciclovir) or ~1–3 h (valganciclovir). Plasma half-life is ~3–5 h (longer in renal failure). Ganciclovir is ~2–9% protein-bound. It is eliminated renally as unchanged drug, so dosing must be adjusted for creatinine clearance.

**Drug Interactions:** Coadministration with zidovudine markedly increases hematologic toxicity. Mycophenolate and TMP-SMX also exacerbate cytopenias. Probenecid reduces ganciclovir clearance (like acyclovir) and can increase levels. Antiinfectives like amphotericin B or aminoglycosides (when given concurrently) increase nephrotoxic risk.

**Foscarnet (Pyrophosphate analog).** (Brief mention) Foscarnet directly inhibits viral DNA polymerase by binding the pyrophosphate site, active against CMV and acyclovir-resistant HSV/VZV. It is IV only, with major AEs including nephrotoxicity and electrolyte disturbances (hypocalcemia, hypomagnesemia). It requires close monitoring of renal function and electrolytes.

**Clinically Significant Interactions (Herpes Drugs):** Many interactions are pharmacodynamic: combining nephrotoxic or myelotoxic drugs potentiates toxicity. For example, co-administering nephrotoxic antibiotics with IV acyclovir requires caution. Drugs like probenecid reduce clearance of acyclovir and ganciclovir (useful in some cases to boost levels). **Valganciclovir/ganciclovir** potentiate AZT marrow toxicity. Always adjust doses in renal impairment.
