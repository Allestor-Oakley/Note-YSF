
## **Flaviviridae – Zika Virus**

---

### **1. Introduction**

Zika virus (ZIKV) is a **mosquito-borne flavivirus** first identified in Uganda in 1947. It became a major public health concern during the **2015–2016 outbreaks in the Americas**, when it was linked to **congenital Zika syndrome** and **neurological complications** such as **Guillain-Barré syndrome**.

ZIKV is **transmitted by Aedes mosquitoes** (primarily _Aedes aegypti_), but it can also be spread via **sexual contact**, **vertical transmission**, **blood transfusion**, and possibly **organ transplantation**. Unlike dengue, most Zika infections are **asymptomatic** or cause mild illness, but **pregnancy-related complications** are significant.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Flaviviridae
    
- **Genus**: Flavivirus
    
- **Species**: Zika virus
    

**Structure**:

- **Enveloped**, icosahedral
    
- **Positive-sense, single-stranded RNA (~10.8 kb)**
    

**Genome Organization**:

- One long ORF encoding a polyprotein
    
- Cleaved into:
    
    - **Structural proteins**: Capsid (C), Premembrane/Membrane (prM/M), Envelope (E)
        
    - **Non-structural proteins**: NS1, NS2A/B, NS3, NS4A/B, NS5
        

**Key Proteins**:

- **Envelope (E)**: Facilitates cell entry, target of neutralizing antibodies
    
- **NS1**: Immune evasion, possible role in endothelial dysfunction
    
- **NS3**: Helicase and protease
    
- **NS5**: RNA-dependent RNA polymerase and methyltransferase; critical for replication
    

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Transmission Routes**

- **Mosquito bite** (main vector: _Aedes aegypti_)
    
- **Sexual transmission**
    
- **Vertical transmission**: Mother to fetus
    
- **Blood transfusion**, lab exposure
    

#### **B. Host Cell Entry and Spread**

1. Entry via **Axl**, **Tyro3**, and other TAM receptors on dermal fibroblasts, dendritic cells, and neural progenitor cells
    
2. Endocytosis and fusion mediated by **E protein**
    
3. Translation of viral RNA in cytoplasm → polyprotein processing
    
4. Replication occurs in **ER-derived membrane vesicles**
    
5. Assembly and maturation in the ER and Golgi, release by exocytosis
    

#### **C. Neural Tropism**

- ZIKV uniquely targets **neural progenitor cells**, impairing proliferation and migration
    
- Causes **apoptosis** and **disruption of neurodevelopment**
    
- Basis for **microcephaly** and **other congenital CNS malformations**
    

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **A. Asymptomatic in ~80%**

- Most infections are **subclinical**
    

#### **B. Symptomatic (Zika fever)**

- Incubation: 3–14 days
    
- Mild self-limiting illness (2–7 days):
    
    - **Low-grade fever**
        
    - **Maculopapular rash**
        
    - **Conjunctivitis (non-purulent)**
        
    - **Arthralgia**, myalgia
        
    - Headache, retro-orbital pain
        

#### **C. Neurological Complications**

- **Guillain-Barré syndrome** (GBS): Immune-mediated demyelinating neuropathy
    
- **Meningoencephalitis**, myelitis (rare)
    

#### **D. Congenital Zika Syndrome**

- **Microcephaly**
    
- Intracranial calcifications
    
- Ventriculomegaly
    
- **Arthrogryposis**
    
- Hearing, visual impairments
    
- Neurodevelopmental delay
    
- Fetal loss, stillbirth
    

---

### **5. Complications**

- **Congenital anomalies** (Zika embryopathy)
    
- **GBS** and other autoimmune neuropathies
    
- **Miscarriage, intrauterine growth restriction**
    
- **Sexual transmission risks**, persistence in semen for months
    

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Laboratory Diagnosis**

**Molecular Tests**:

- **RT-PCR (serum, urine, saliva)**: Best within 7 days of symptom onset
    
- **RT-PCR in amniotic fluid**: For suspected congenital infection
    

**Serologic Tests**:

- **IgM ELISA** (after ~5 days): May cross-react with other flaviviruses
    
- **Plaque reduction neutralization test (PRNT)**: To distinguish from dengue or other flavivirus exposure
    

**Congenital Infection**:

- PCR on **amniotic fluid, placenta, CSF**, or infant blood/urine
    
- Neuroimaging (cranial ultrasound, MRI)
    

#### **B. Differential Diagnosis**

|Disease|Key Features|
|---|---|
|**Dengue**|Higher fever, more myalgia, thrombocytopenia|
|**Chikungunya**|Severe joint pain, arthritis-like symptoms|
|**Measles/Rubella**|Rash with respiratory symptoms or lymphadenopathy|
|**Leptospirosis**|Conjunctival suffusion, renal/hepatic involvement|
|**Other TORCH infections**|In congenital cases – CMV, toxoplasmosis, rubella, HSV|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. Supportive Care**

- No antiviral therapy
    
- **Rest**, **hydration**
    
- **Acetaminophen** for fever/pain
    
- Avoid NSAIDs/aspirin until dengue ruled out (bleeding risk)
    

#### **B. Management of Complications**

- **GBS**: May require IVIG, plasmapheresis
    
- **Neonatal care**: Developmental support for congenital Zika syndrome
    

#### **C. Prevention**

- **Mosquito control**: Eliminate breeding grounds, repellents, bed nets
    
- **Sexual precautions**:
    
    - Abstinence or condom use for:
        
        - Men: at least 3 months post-infection
            
        - Women: at least 2 months
            
- **Pregnancy guidance**:
    
    - Delay travel to endemic areas during outbreaks
        
    - Test pregnant women with symptoms or exposure
        

#### **D. Vaccine Status**

- No licensed vaccine yet
    
- Several candidates in clinical trials (e.g., DNA, inactivated, live-attenuated platforms)

## **Flaviviridae – Yellow Fever Virus (YFV)**

---

### **1. Introduction**

Yellow Fever Virus (YFV) is an **arbovirus** causing **acute viral hemorrhagic disease** with high mortality in severe cases. Endemic in **sub-Saharan Africa** and **South America**, YFV causes periodic **urban and sylvatic outbreaks**.

The term “yellow” refers to the **jaundice** seen in severe infections. Although a highly effective **live-attenuated vaccine** exists, outbreaks continue due to limited vaccination coverage.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Flaviviridae
    
- **Genus**: Flavivirus
    
- **Species**: Yellow Fever Virus
    

**Structure**:

- **Enveloped**, icosahedral symmetry
    
- **Positive-sense ssRNA (~11 kb)**
    

**Genome Organization**:

- Single ORF encodes a polyprotein, cleaved into:
    
    - **Structural proteins**: C, prM/M, E
        
    - **Non-structural proteins**: NS1, NS2A/B, NS3, NS4A/B, NS5
        

**Key Proteins**:

- **E protein**: Mediates receptor binding and membrane fusion
    
- **NS3**: Protease/helicase functions
    
- **NS5**: RNA polymerase and methyltransferase
    
- **NS1**: Involved in immune evasion and vascular dysfunction
    

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Transmission Cycle**

- **Vector**: _Aedes_ (urban) and _Haemagogus_ spp. (sylvatic)
    
- **Reservoirs**: Primates (especially in jungle cycles)
    

#### **B. Viral Entry and Spread**

1. Virus introduced via mosquito bite
    
2. Infects **dendritic cells**, travels to lymph nodes
    
3. Systemic **viremia** → targets **liver**, **spleen**, **kidney**, **heart**
    

#### **C. Tissue Tropism and Injury**

- **Hepatocytes**: Apoptosis and necrosis → **Councilman bodies** (eosinophilic apoptotic hepatocytes)
    
- Liver damage → impaired coagulation, jaundice
    
- **Kidney**: Acute tubular necrosis → renal failure
    
- **Heart**: Myocarditis, arrhythmias
    
- **Endothelial dysfunction** → plasma leakage, bleeding
    

#### **D. Immune Response**

- Early innate responses attempt viral clearance
    
- Severe disease: Dysregulated cytokine response, DIC-like picture
    

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

Incubation: 3–6 days  
Course divided into **three phases**:

#### **A. Infection Phase (Initial)**

- Sudden onset of:
    
    - High **fever**
        
    - **Chills**
        
    - **Myalgias** (especially back pain)
        
    - **Headache**
        
    - Nausea, vomiting
        
    - **Conjunctival injection**
        

#### **B. Remission Phase**

- Symptoms temporarily improve (~1 day)
    

#### **C. Toxic Phase (15–25% of patients)**

- Recurrence of high fever
    
- **Jaundice** (due to hepatic necrosis)
    
- **Hemorrhagic symptoms**:
    
    - Epistaxis
        
    - Hematemesis ("black vomit")
        
    - Melena
        
- **Hypotension**, **shock**
    
- **Multi-organ failure**
    

---

### **5. Complications**

- **Fulminant hepatic failure**
    
- **Renal failure**
    
- **Hemorrhagic shock**
    
- **DIC-like coagulopathy**
    
- **Cardiac involvement**
    
- Mortality in severe cases: **20–50%**
    

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Laboratory Diagnosis**

**Acute Phase (<7 days)**:

- **RT-PCR**: Detects viral RNA in blood
    
- **Virus isolation** (rare, BSL-3/4 lab)
    

**Later Phase (>7 days)**:

- **IgM ELISA**: Detectable ~5–7 days after onset
    
- **PRNT (Plaque Reduction Neutralization Test)**: Confirms YFV, differentiates from other flaviviruses
    

**Routine Labs**:

- Elevated **AST > ALT** (disproportionate)
    
- **Bilirubin** ↑
    
- **Prolonged PT/INR**
    
- **Thrombocytopenia**
    
- Proteinuria, elevated creatinine
    

#### **B. Differential Diagnosis**

|Disease|Distinguishing Features|
|---|---|
|**Viral hepatitis (A–E)**|No hemorrhagic signs, lower risk of shock|
|**Dengue**|Prominent rash, biphasic fever, lower hepatic necrosis risk|
|**Leptospirosis**|Conjunctival suffusion, myalgia, renal failure|
|**Malaria**|Travel history, parasites on smear, anemia|
|**Typhoid fever**|Rose spots, bradycardia, GI symptoms|
|**Ebola/Marburg**|More severe bleeding, person-to-person transmission|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. Supportive Care Only**

- No antiviral therapy available
    
- Management in **ICU** for severe cases
    

**Supportive Measures**:

- **Fluid resuscitation**
    
- Monitor for and manage **bleeding**, **renal failure**
    
- Treat secondary bacterial infections if suspected
    

#### **B. Avoid**

- **NSAIDs and aspirin** (risk of bleeding)
    
- **Unnecessary injections**
    

---

### **8. Prevention**

#### **A. Vaccine (Highly Effective)**

- **17D Live-attenuated vaccine**
    
    - Single dose confers **lifelong protection**
        
    - Protective antibody response within **10 days**
        
    - Required for travel to endemic areas (**IHR** regulations)
        

**Contraindications**:

- Infants <6 months
    
- Immunocompromised
    
- Allergy to egg proteins
    
- Pregnancy (relative)
    

#### **B. Vector Control**

- Mosquito habitat reduction
    
- Insect repellent, clothing, nets
    

---

## **Flaviviridae – West Nile Virus (WNV)**

---

### **1. Introduction**

West Nile Virus (WNV) is a **neurotropic arbovirus** transmitted primarily by **Culex mosquitoes**. First isolated in Uganda in 1937, it became a major public health concern in North America after the **1999 New York outbreak**, where it caused **encephalitis**, **meningitis**, and **acute flaccid paralysis**. WNV is now the **leading cause of arboviral neuroinvasive disease in the U.S.**

While most infections are **asymptomatic**, **neuroinvasive disease** occurs in a small but significant minority, especially **elderly and immunocompromised individuals**.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Flaviviridae
    
- **Genus**: Flavivirus
    
- **Species**: West Nile virus
    

**Structure**:

- **Enveloped**, icosahedral symmetry
    
- **Single-stranded, positive-sense RNA (~11 kb)**
    

**Genome**:

- Encodes a single polyprotein, cleaved into:
    
    - **Structural proteins**: C, prM/M, E
        
    - **Non-structural proteins**: NS1, NS2A/B, NS3, NS4A/B, NS5
        

**Key Proteins**:

- **Envelope (E)**: Receptor binding and fusion
    
- **NS3**: Protease and helicase
    
- **NS5**: RNA-dependent RNA polymerase and methyltransferase
    
- **NS1**: Modulates immune responses and vascular permeability
    

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Transmission Cycle**

- **Vectors**: _Culex_ spp. mosquitoes (esp. _Culex pipiens_, _C. quinquefasciatus_)
    
- **Reservoir hosts**: Birds (amplifying hosts)
    
- **Dead-end hosts**: Humans and horses
    

Transmission also occurs via:

- Blood transfusions
    
- Organ transplants
    
- Vertical transmission
    
- Breastfeeding (rare)
    

#### **B. Viral Entry and Spread**

1. Mosquito injects virus into dermis
    
2. Virus infects **keratinocytes**, **dendritic cells**
    
3. Dissemination to **lymph nodes**, then **viremia**
    
4. Crosses **blood-brain barrier (BBB)** by:
    
    - Cytokine-mediated endothelial disruption
        
    - Trojan horse mechanism via infected immune cells
        
    - Retrograde axonal transport from peripheral nerves
        

#### **C. Neuroinvasion**

- Virus targets **neurons** in the **cortex**, **brainstem**, **cerebellum**, **spinal cord**
    
- Induces **neuronal apoptosis**, **inflammation**, **microglial activation**
    

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

Most infections are **asymptomatic** (≈80%)

#### **A. West Nile Fever (~20%)**

- Incubation: 2–14 days
    
- Sudden onset:
    
    - Fever
        
    - Headache
        
    - **Malaise**, fatigue
        
    - **Rash** (truncal, maculopapular)
        
    - Myalgias, arthralgias
        
    - Nausea, vomiting
        
    - **Lymphadenopathy**
        

Self-limiting, lasts 3–6 days

#### **B. Neuroinvasive Disease (<1%)**

Primarily in elderly and immunosuppressed

1. **Meningitis**: Headache, neck stiffness, photophobia
    
2. **Encephalitis**: Confusion, seizures, focal deficits, coma
    
3. **Acute Flaccid Paralysis**:
    
    - Poliomyelitis-like
        
    - Rapid, asymmetric weakness
        
    - **No sensory loss**
        
    - Due to anterior horn cell involvement
        

---

### **5. Complications**

- **Long-term neurologic sequelae** in neuroinvasive cases:
    
    - Memory loss
        
    - Gait instability
        
    - Persistent weakness
        
- **Death** in 10% of neuroinvasive cases, especially encephalitis
    
- **Chronic fatigue syndrome–like symptoms**
    

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Laboratory Diagnosis**

**Preferred test**:

- **WNV IgM ELISA in CSF** or serum
    
    - Appears ~3–8 days after symptom onset
        
    - Persists for months (caution with past infections)
        

**RT-PCR**:

- Useful early (<7 days)
    
- Limited by short viremia window
    

**Other supportive labs**:

- CSF: Lymphocytic pleocytosis, ↑ protein, normal glucose
    
- Neuroimaging (MRI): Hyperintensities in basal ganglia, thalamus, brainstem, or anterior spinal cord
    

#### **B. Differential Diagnosis**

|Disease|Distinguishing Features|
|---|---|
|**Enteroviral meningitis**|Younger age, seasonal, normal MRI|
|**Herpes simplex encephalitis**|Temporal lobe involvement on MRI, rapid progression, seizures|
|**Japanese encephalitis**|Asia-endemic, different travel history|
|**Guillain-Barré Syndrome**|Symmetric, ascending weakness, absent reflexes, no fever|
|**Poliovirus**|Vaccine history, similar flaccid paralysis but rare today|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. Supportive Care**

- **No specific antiviral**
    
- Hospitalization for **neuroinvasive cases**
    
- Monitor and manage:
    
    - **Seizures**
        
    - **Intracranial pressure**
        
    - **Respiratory failure** (in flaccid paralysis)
        
    - **Secondary bacterial infections**
        

#### **B. Investigational Therapies**

- Interferons, IVIG, monoclonal antibodies — not currently standard of care
    

---

### **8. Prevention**

#### **A. Vector Control**

- Eliminate standing water
    
- Insect repellents (e.g., DEET)
    
- Protective clothing, window screens
    

#### **B. Blood Supply Screening**

- Nucleic acid amplification testing of blood donors
    

#### **C. Vaccine**

- No human vaccine yet
    
- Vaccines exist for **equines**
    
- Human vaccines under development (e.g., live-attenuated, DNA, mRNA)

## **Flaviviridae – Japanese Encephalitis Virus (JEV)**

---

### **1. Introduction**

**Japanese Encephalitis Virus (JEV)** is a leading cause of **viral encephalitis** in **Asia and parts of the Western Pacific**. It is a **mosquito-borne zoonotic flavivirus** that causes **severe neurologic disease**, especially in children. Despite a **low clinical attack rate**, JEV is associated with a **high case fatality** and **long-term neurologic deficits** in survivors.

JEV is a **vaccine-preventable disease**, with endemic transmission patterns influenced by **agricultural practices** and **vector populations**.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Flaviviridae
    
- **Genus**: Flavivirus
    
- **Species**: Japanese encephalitis virus
    

**Structure**:

- **Enveloped**, spherical, ~50 nm
    
- **Positive-sense single-stranded RNA (~11 kb)**
    

**Genome**:

- Encodes one large polyprotein, cleaved into:
    
    - **Structural proteins**: C (capsid), prM/M (membrane), E (envelope)
        
    - **Non-structural proteins**: NS1, NS2A/B, NS3, NS4A/B, NS5
        

**Key Proteins**:

- **E protein**: Host cell receptor binding and membrane fusion
    
- **NS3**: Protease/helicase
    
- **NS5**: RNA-dependent RNA polymerase and methyltransferase
    
- **NS1**: Involved in immune modulation and vascular leakage
    

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Transmission Cycle**

- **Vector**: _Culex tritaeniorhynchus_ and other _Culex_ spp.
    
- **Reservoir hosts**: Wild birds (e.g., herons, egrets)
    
- **Amplifying hosts**: Domestic pigs
    
- **Dead-end hosts**: Humans and horses
    

JEV transmission peaks during **monsoon and post-monsoon seasons**, particularly in **rice-growing rural areas** where mosquitoes and pigs coexist.

#### **B. Infection and CNS Invasion**

1. **Inoculation** via mosquito bite
    
2. Viral replication in local dendritic cells and **Langerhans cells**
    
3. **Viremia** → dissemination to lymphoid tissues, liver, and spleen
    
4. Crosses **blood-brain barrier (BBB)** by:
    
    - Direct infection of endothelial cells
        
    - Trojan horse via infected leukocytes
        
    - Cytokine-mediated barrier disruption
        

#### **C. CNS Pathogenesis**

- **Neurotropism**: Infects neurons, especially in **thalamus**, **hippocampus**, **brainstem**, **basal ganglia**
    
- Triggers **neuronal apoptosis**, **astrocytosis**, **microglial activation**
    
- Leads to **perivascular cuffing**, **necrosis**, and **gliosis**
    

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

Incubation: 5–15 days  
Only ~1 in 250 infected persons develop symptomatic disease

#### **A. Prodromal Phase (1–3 days)**

- Fever
    
- Headache
    
- Vomiting
    
- Malaise
    

#### **B. Encephalitic Phase**

- High fever
    
- **Seizures** (common in children)
    
- **Altered mental status**
    
- **Stiff neck**
    
- **Focal neurologic signs**: hemiparesis, ataxia, cranial nerve palsies
    
- **Movement disorders**: Parkinsonian features, choreoathetosis
    
- **Coma** in severe cases
    

---

### **5. Complications**

- **Case-fatality rate**: 20–30%
    
- **Neurologic sequelae** in 30–50% of survivors:
    
    - Cognitive impairment
        
    - Motor deficits
        
    - Speech and behavioral abnormalities
        
    - Seizure disorders
        

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Laboratory Diagnosis**

**CSF or serum** testing:

- **JEV-specific IgM ELISA** (MAC-ELISA)
    
    - Appears ~4–7 days after onset
        
    - CSF IgM confirms neuroinvasive disease
        

**RT-PCR**:

- Useful in early viremia (rare in CSF)
    
- Not reliable beyond first few days
    

**Neuroimaging (MRI)**:

- Bilateral thalamic hyperintensities (classic)
    
- Also basal ganglia, brainstem, hippocampus
    

**CSF findings**:

- Lymphocytic pleocytosis
    
- Elevated protein
    
- Normal or mildly low glucose
    

#### **B. Differential Diagnosis**

|Disease|Key Differences|
|---|---|
|**Herpes simplex encephalitis**|Temporal lobe involvement, hemorrhagic necrosis, HSV PCR +|
|**West Nile Virus**|Older patients, flaccid paralysis more common|
|**Dengue**|Prominent systemic features, less neurotropism|
|**Bacterial meningitis**|Neutrophilic CSF, high opening pressure|
|**Cerebral malaria**|Recent travel to endemic areas, parasitemia|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. Supportive Only**

- No specific antiviral available
    
- ICU management for severe neurologic symptoms
    

**Supportive Measures**:

- Airway protection
    
- Seizure control (e.g., benzodiazepines, antiepileptics)
    
- Management of raised intracranial pressure
    
- Nutritional and fluid support
    

#### **B. Investigational Therapies**

- Immunomodulators, IVIG, interferons: No proven benefit
    

---

### **8. Prevention**

#### **A. Vaccination (Highly Effective)**

**WHO recommends vaccination in all endemic areas**  
Types of vaccines:

1. **Live-attenuated SA14-14-2** (widely used in Asia)
    
2. **Inactivated Vero cell-derived (e.g., IXIARO)** – 2 doses
    
3. **Chimeric vaccines** under development
    

#### **B. Vector and Reservoir Control**

- Mosquito control: Larvicides, insecticides
    
- Personal protection: Bed nets, repellents
    
- Pig farm separation from human dwellings

## **Flaviviridae – Tick-Borne Encephalitis Virus (TBEV)**

---

### **1. Introduction**

**Tick-Borne Encephalitis Virus (TBEV)** is a neurotropic flavivirus transmitted via **Ixodes spp. ticks**, endemic in parts of **Europe, Russia, and Asia**. It causes **Tick-Borne Encephalitis (TBE)**, a potentially severe biphasic illness that can progress to **meningoencephalitis** or **myelitis**.

TBEV is a **public health concern in endemic regions**, especially for those engaged in **outdoor activities**, and is **vaccine-preventable**.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Flaviviridae
    
- **Genus**: Flavivirus
    
- **Species**: Tick-borne encephalitis virus
    

**Structure**:

- **Enveloped**, spherical virion (~50 nm)
    
- **Positive-sense single-stranded RNA** genome (~11 kb)
    

**Genome**:

- Encodes a single polyprotein cleaved into:
    
    - **Structural proteins**: C, prM/M, E
        
    - **Non-structural proteins**: NS1–NS5
        

**Key Proteins**:

- **E protein**: Receptor binding, fusion
    
- **NS3**: Protease/helicase
    
- **NS5**: RNA polymerase and methyltransferase
    
- **NS1**: Immune evasion, vascular dysfunction
    

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Transmission**

- **Vector**: Ixodes ricinus (Europe), Ixodes persulcatus (Asia)
    
- **Reservoir hosts**: Small mammals (rodents, shrews)
    
- **Human infection**:
    
    - Tick bite (primary)
        
    - Unpasteurized dairy products (rare)
        

#### **B. Infection Dynamics**

- Virus replicates in **dendritic cells** and **keratinocytes**
    
- Enters regional **lymph nodes**, causing **primary viremia**
    
- May be cleared or enter **secondary viremia**, enabling CNS invasion
    

#### **C. CNS Invasion**

- Entry into CNS via:
    
    - Infected immune cell trafficking
        
    - Cytokine-mediated BBB permeability
        
- Infects **neurons** in the **brainstem**, **spinal cord**, **cerebellum**
    
- Leads to **neuronal apoptosis**, **glial activation**, **inflammation**
    

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

Incubation: 7–14 days (up to 28 days)

**Biphasic Illness (Typical in 70–80% of Symptomatic Cases)**

#### **A. Phase 1 – Viremic Phase (~2–7 days)**

- Nonspecific flu-like symptoms:
    
    - Fever
        
    - Headache
        
    - Myalgia
        
    - Fatigue
        
- May resolve completely in mild cases
    

**Asymptomatic interval**: 1–20 days

#### **B. Phase 2 – Neurologic Phase**

- **Meningitis**: Fever, headache, photophobia, neck stiffness
    
- **Encephalitis**: Confusion, altered consciousness, ataxia, seizures
    
- **Myelitis**: Acute flaccid paralysis (asymmetric), cranial nerve palsies
    
- In children: More meningitic
    
- In adults: More encephalitic or mixed
    

---

### **5. Complications**

- Case-fatality rates:
    
    - European subtype: 0.5–2%
        
    - Far Eastern subtype: 20–30%
        
- Long-term neurologic sequelae (up to 30%):
    
    - Paralysis
        
    - Ataxia
        
    - Neuropsychiatric disturbances
        
    - Cognitive decline
        

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Laboratory Diagnosis**

**Gold standard**:

- **Serology (IgM and IgG ELISA)**:
    
    - Serum or CSF
        
    - CSF IgM indicates neuroinvasive disease
        
    - Cross-reactivity with other flaviviruses is possible
        

**RT-PCR**:

- Limited utility — only positive during early viremic phase
    

**CSF Findings**:

- Lymphocytic pleocytosis
    
- Elevated protein
    
- Normal glucose
    

**MRI**:

- May show signal abnormalities in basal ganglia, thalamus, brainstem, cerebellum, or spinal cord
    

#### **B. Differential Diagnosis**

|Disease|Distinct Features|
|---|---|
|**Herpes Simplex Encephalitis**|Temporal lobe involvement, PCR positive, hemorrhagic MRI lesions|
|**Enteroviral Meningitis**|Self-limiting, no encephalitis, younger age|
|**West Nile Virus**|Flaccid paralysis, older age group, geographic differences|
|**Bacterial Meningitis**|Higher CSF neutrophils and pressure, more fulminant course|
|**Rabies (paralytic)**|Hydrophobia, behavioral changes, exposure to animals|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. Supportive Care Only**

- No specific antiviral therapy
    
- Hospitalization for neurologic monitoring
    

**Supportive measures**:

- Antipyretics
    
- Seizure control (benzodiazepines, antiepileptics)
    
- Mechanical ventilation (if respiratory compromise)
    
- Physical therapy for neurologic recovery
    

---

### **8. Prevention**

#### **A. Vaccination (Highly Effective)**

**Inactivated vaccines available**:

- **FSME-IMMUN®** (Europe)
    
- **Encepur®** (Germany)
    
- **TBE-Moscow**, **TBE-China** (for Far Eastern subtype)
    

**Vaccination Schedule**:

- 2–3 doses + booster every 3–5 years
    
- Recommended for:
    
    - Residents/travelers to endemic areas
        
    - Occupational exposure (foresters, soldiers, farmers)
        

#### **B. Tick Prevention**

- Protective clothing
    
- Tick repellents (DEET, permethrin)
    
- Tick checks and prompt removal
    
- Avoid unpasteurized dairy in endemic zones
