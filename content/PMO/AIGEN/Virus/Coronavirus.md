
## **SARS-CoV-2 (Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome Coronavirus 2)**

### **1. Introduction**

SARS-CoV-2 is the causative agent of **Coronavirus Disease 2019 (COVID-19)**. It emerged in late 2019 in Wuhan, China, and led to a global pandemic. The virus is zoonotic in origin and closely related to bat coronaviruses. It primarily affects the respiratory system but has significant multi-organ involvement in severe cases.

### **2. Characteristics / Virological Profile**

- **Family:** Coronaviridae
    
- **Subfamily:** Orthocoronavirinae
    
- **Genus:** Betacoronavirus
    
- **Genome:** Positive-sense single-stranded RNA (~29.9 kb) – one of the largest known RNA genomes
    
- **Structure:**
    
    - Enveloped, spherical (~80–120 nm)
        
    - Characteristic spike (S) proteins forming a crown-like appearance
        

#### **Key Structural and Non-Structural Proteins:**

- **S (Spike) protein:** Mediates receptor binding and membrane fusion
    
    - **S1 subunit:** Contains the receptor-binding domain (RBD) – binds to **ACE2** receptor
        
    - **S2 subunit:** Facilitates membrane fusion
        
- **E (Envelope) protein:** Involved in assembly and release
    
- **M (Membrane) protein:** Maintains structural integrity
    
- **N (Nucleocapsid) protein:** Encapsulates viral RNA
    
- **Non-structural proteins (nsps):** e.g., **nsp3, nsp5 (Mpro), nsp12 (RdRp)** – vital for replication and transcription
    

### **3. Pathophysiology**

1. **Viral Entry:**
    
    - **S1 RBD** binds to **ACE2** receptor (expressed on alveolar cells, intestinal epithelium, endothelium, etc.)
        
    - **TMPRSS2**, a host serine protease, primes S protein for membrane fusion
        
    - Endocytosis or direct fusion leads to viral entry
        
2. **Translation and Replication:**
    
    - Viral RNA is translated into **polyproteins pp1a and pp1ab**
        
    - Cleaved by viral proteases (e.g., **Mpro/3CLpro** and **PLpro**) into nsps forming the replication-transcription complex (RTC)
        
    - **RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (nsp12)** synthesizes full-length genome and subgenomic RNAs
        
3. **Assembly and Release:**
    
    - Structural proteins synthesized in the ER and Golgi
        
    - Virions assembled in ER-Golgi intermediate compartment (ERGIC) and released via exocytosis
        
4. **Immune Dysregulation:**
    
    - Suppression of type I interferons
        
    - Excessive cytokine release (**cytokine storm**): IL-6, IL-1β, TNF-α
        
    - Endothelial dysfunction and hypercoagulability
        

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

Symptoms vary based on severity:

#### **Mild-to-Moderate Disease (~80% cases):**

- Fever, dry cough, fatigue, anosmia, ageusia, sore throat, myalgia, diarrhea
    

#### **Severe Disease (~15%):**

- Pneumonia with hypoxia
    
- Dyspnea, tachypnea
    
- Oxygen saturation <94%
    

#### **Critical Illness (~5%):**

- Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS)
    
- Septic shock
    
- Multi-organ failure
    

#### **Post-Acute Sequelae (Long COVID):**

- Persistent fatigue, cognitive dysfunction, dyspnea, chest pain
    

### **5. Complications**

- **Respiratory:** ARDS, secondary bacterial pneumonia
    
- **Cardiovascular:** Myocarditis, arrhythmias, thromboembolism (e.g., pulmonary embolism)
    
- **Renal:** Acute kidney injury
    
- **Neurological:** Stroke, encephalopathy, Guillain-Barré syndrome
    
- **Coagulopathy:** Elevated D-dimer, DIC-like picture
    
- **Multisystem Inflammatory Syndrome (MIS-C/MIS-A)** in children and adults
    

### **6. Diagnostics**

#### **Laboratory Testing:**

- **Nucleic acid amplification tests (NAATs):**
    
    - **RT-PCR** (gold standard): Detects viral RNA from nasopharyngeal, oropharyngeal, or lower respiratory samples
        
- **Antigen tests:** Faster, less sensitive
    
- **Serology:** Detects IgM/IgG; useful for retrospective diagnosis or epidemiology
    

#### **Imaging:**

- Chest X-ray: Bilateral infiltrates
    
- Chest CT: Ground-glass opacities, consolidation (often peripheral and bilateral)
    

#### **Differential Diagnosis:**

- **Influenza**
    
- **RSV**
    
- **Mycoplasma pneumonia**
    
- **Bacterial pneumonia**
    
- **Other coronaviruses** (e.g., OC43, NL63)
    
- **Sepsis of non-respiratory origin**
    

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **Supportive Care (mainstay for mild/moderate disease):**

- Antipyretics, hydration, monitoring
    
- Oxygen supplementation if hypoxic
    

#### **Antiviral Therapies:**

- **Remdesivir:** RNA polymerase inhibitor (for hospitalized patients on oxygen)
    
- **Nirmatrelvir/ritonavir (Paxlovid):** For early outpatient treatment
    
- **Molnupiravir:** RNA mutagen; alternative if Paxlovid not suitable
    

#### **Immunomodulatory Agents:**

- **Dexamethasone:** For patients requiring oxygen
    
- **Tocilizumab (IL-6 blocker):** For severe inflammation
    
- **Baricitinib (JAK inhibitor):** In certain cases with systemic inflammation
    

#### **Anticoagulation:**

- Prophylactic or therapeutic anticoagulation due to thrombotic risk
    

#### **Vaccination:**

- mRNA (Pfizer-BioNTech, Moderna), viral vector (J&J), protein subunit
    
- Effective in reducing severe disease and transmission

## **SARS-CoV-1 (Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome Coronavirus 1)**

### **1. Introduction**

SARS-CoV-1 was the causative agent of the first recognized outbreak of severe acute respiratory syndrome (SARS) in 2002–2003. Originating in Guangdong, China, the virus caused a global epidemic with a high mortality rate (~9.6%) before being contained by public health measures. It is closely related to SARS-CoV-2 but less transmissible.

---

### **2. Characteristics / Virological Profile**

- **Family:** Coronaviridae
    
- **Subfamily:** Orthocoronavirinae
    
- **Genus:** Betacoronavirus
    
- **Genome:** ~29.7 kb positive-sense ssRNA
    
- **Structure:**
    
    - Enveloped virus (~80–120 nm)
        
    - Spike (S) proteins give the characteristic crown-like appearance
        

#### **Key Structural Proteins:**

- **S (Spike):** Mediates entry via ACE2 receptor
    
- **E (Envelope):** Virion assembly, pathogenesis
    
- **M (Membrane):** Structural protein
    
- **N (Nucleocapsid):** Encapsulates genome, regulates RNA synthesis
    
- **Non-Structural Proteins (nsps):** Derived from ORF1a and ORF1b polyproteins (e.g., nsp12 = RNA-dependent RNA polymerase)
    

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

1. **Viral Entry:**
    
    - **S protein** binds to **ACE2** (angiotensin-converting enzyme 2) on epithelial cells (especially alveoli)
        
    - **Host proteases** (e.g., TMPRSS2) cleave S protein to facilitate membrane fusion
        
2. **Genome Translation & Replication:**
    
    - Translation of ORF1a/1b → polyproteins pp1a/pp1ab → processed by **viral proteases** (e.g., **3CLpro**, **PLpro**)
        
    - Formation of replication-transcription complex (**RTC**) in double-membrane vesicles
        
3. **Transcription & Assembly:**
    
    - Subgenomic RNAs encode structural/accessory proteins
        
    - Assembly in ER-Golgi intermediate compartment (ERGIC)
        
4. **Immune Dysregulation:**
    
    - Suppression of **type I interferon** response
        
    - Overproduction of cytokines: **IL-6**, **IL-8**, **TNF-α**
        
    - Apoptosis of infected epithelial and immune cells
        

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **Incubation Period:** ~2–10 days (average 5–6 days)

#### **Phases:**

- **Prodromal Phase (first week):**
    
    - High fever, chills, myalgia, headache
        
    - Malaise, sometimes diarrhea
        
    - Minimal upper respiratory symptoms
        
- **Pulmonary Phase (second week):**
    
    - Dry cough, dyspnea, hypoxia
        
    - Radiographic pneumonia: bilateral infiltrates
        
    - ~20% progress to **ARDS**
        

#### **Laboratory Findings:**

- Lymphopenia
    
- Elevated LDH, AST/ALT
    
- Elevated CRP and D-dimer
    

---

### **5. Complications**

- **Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS)**
    
- **Respiratory failure**
    
- **Multiorgan failure** (especially liver and kidneys)
    
- **Nosocomial transmission** (especially in healthcare settings)
    
- **Long-term pulmonary fibrosis** in some survivors
    

---

### **6. Diagnostics**

#### **Viral Detection:**

- **RT-PCR**: Detects viral RNA in nasopharyngeal or lower respiratory samples
    

#### **Serological Testing:**

- ELISA or IFA (detect anti-SARS-CoV antibodies)
    

#### **Imaging:**

- **Chest X-ray/CT:** Bilateral ground-glass opacities or consolidations
    

#### **Differential Diagnosis:**

- **Influenza**
    
- **Atypical pneumonia** (e.g., Mycoplasma, Legionella)
    
- **COVID-19** (distinguished by onset year and diagnostic PCR/serology)
    
- **Bacterial pneumonia**
    
- **Hantavirus pulmonary syndrome**
    

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **Supportive Care:**

- Oxygen therapy
    
- Mechanical ventilation for ARDS
    
- Fluids, fever control, and ICU monitoring
    

#### **Antiviral and Adjunctive Therapies (investigational in 2003):**

- Ribavirin (limited evidence; toxic)
    
- Interferon-α or β
    
- Corticosteroids (controversial; used in severe inflammation)
    
- Convalescent plasma (used in some severe cases)
    

#### **Infection Control:**

- Isolation, contact and droplet precautions
    
- Quarantine of exposed individuals
    
- Effective containment helped eliminate human transmission by 2004

## **MERS-CoV (Middle East Respiratory Syndrome Coronavirus)**

### **1. Introduction**

MERS-CoV is the causative agent of **Middle East Respiratory Syndrome (MERS)**, first reported in Saudi Arabia in 2012. It is a zoonotic virus, with **dromedary camels** identified as a major reservoir. Although less transmissible than SARS-CoV-1 and SARS-CoV-2, MERS-CoV causes a **high case fatality rate (~35%)**, making it a critical public health threat, especially in healthcare settings.

---

### **2. Characteristics / Virological Profile**

- **Family:** Coronaviridae
    
- **Subfamily:** Orthocoronavirinae
    
- **Genus:** Betacoronavirus (lineage C)
    
- **Genome:** Positive-sense, single-stranded RNA (~30.1 kb)
    
- **Structure:**
    
    - Enveloped, spherical (~80–120 nm)
        
    - Spike glycoproteins (S) project outward giving corona-like appearance
        

#### **Key Structural Proteins:**

- **S (Spike):** Binds to **DPP4 (CD26)** receptor for cell entry
    
- **E (Envelope):** Assembly and pathogenesis
    
- **M (Membrane):** Viral envelope integrity
    
- **N (Nucleocapsid):** RNA binding and genome packaging
    

#### **Non-Structural Proteins:**

- Derived from **ORF1a/1b** polyproteins → cleaved into:
    
    - **nsp3:** Papain-like protease (PLpro)
        
    - **nsp5:** 3C-like protease (3CLpro or Mpro)
        
    - **nsp12:** RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (RdRp)
        
    - **nsp14:** Exoribonuclease (proofreading)
        

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

1. **Viral Entry:**
    
    - **S1 subunit** binds to **DPP4 (CD26)** on epithelial cells of the respiratory tract, kidney, liver, and intestine
        
    - **S2 subunit** mediates membrane fusion (assisted by host proteases like **furin** and **TMPRSS2**)
        
2. **Translation and Replication:**
    
    - Genomic RNA → translated to **pp1a and pp1ab** → cleaved into nsps by **PLpro** and **3CLpro**
        
    - Formation of **replication-transcription complex (RTC)** → synthesis of genomic and subgenomic RNAs
        
3. **Immune Response and Evasion:**
    
    - Downregulation of **interferon type I response**
        
    - Viral proteins (e.g., **nsp3, ORF4a, ORF4b**) inhibit host RNA sensing (RIG-I/MDA5)
        
    - Induces **cytokine storm**: elevated **IL-6, IL-8, MCP-1, TNF-α**
        
    - Lymphopenia and T-cell apoptosis may occur in severe cases
        

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **Incubation Period:** 2–14 days (typically 5–6 days)

#### **Spectrum of Disease:**

- **Asymptomatic/Mild (~20–25%)**
    
- **Moderate-to-Severe (~75%)**
    

#### **Symptoms:**

- Fever, chills, rigors
    
- Cough (often non-productive), dyspnea
    
- Myalgia, fatigue
    
- Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea (in ~25%)
    
- Severe cases: **pneumonia, hypoxemia, respiratory failure**
    

#### **Laboratory Findings:**

- Lymphopenia, thrombocytopenia
    
- Elevated AST, ALT, creatinine
    
- High CRP, ferritin, LDH
    

---

### **5. Complications**

- **Severe pneumonia and ARDS**
    
- **Acute kidney injury (AKI)** – distinctive compared to other CoVs
    
- **Septic shock**
    
- **Multiorgan failure**
    
- **Nosocomial outbreaks** – significant concern in hospitals
    
- **High mortality** in:
    
    - Immunocompromised patients
        
    - Elderly
        
    - Patients with diabetes, renal or cardiovascular disease
        

---

### **6. Diagnostics**

#### **Molecular Testing:**

- **Real-time RT-PCR (rRT-PCR)**:
    
    - Targets: **UpE**, **ORF1a**, **ORF1b**, **N** genes
        
    - Samples: nasopharyngeal swab, tracheal aspirate, bronchoalveolar lavage
        

#### **Serology:**

- ELISA, IFA, microneutralization assays
    
- Useful for surveillance and retrospective diagnosis
    

#### **Imaging:**

- **Chest X-ray:** Unilateral or bilateral infiltrates
    
- **Chest CT:** Ground-glass opacities, consolidation, especially in lower lobes
    

#### **Differential Diagnosis:**

- **COVID-19**
    
- **SARS-CoV-1**
    
- **Influenza**
    
- **Legionella, Mycoplasma pneumonia**
    
- **Other community-acquired pneumonia**
    

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **Supportive Care:**

- Oxygen therapy
    
- Mechanical ventilation if required
    
- Hemodynamic support (fluids, vasopressors)
    

#### **Experimental / Investigational Therapies:**

- **Ribavirin + interferon-α2b** (limited and inconclusive evidence)
    
- **Lopinavir/ritonavir** (not clearly effective)
    
- **Monoclonal antibodies** targeting S protein (in development)
    
- **Convalescent plasma** (experimental)
    

#### **Infection Control:**

- Isolation of patients
    
- Use of **contact, droplet, and airborne precautions**
    
- Strict hospital hygiene and monitoring of healthcare worker exposure
    

#### **Vaccines:**

- No licensed vaccine as of now (though research ongoing)
    
- Candidate vaccines under evaluation using platforms like MVA, DNA, and protein subunits
