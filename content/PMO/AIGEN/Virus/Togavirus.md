
## **Togaviridae – Chikungunya Virus (CHIKV)**

---

### **1. Introduction**

**Chikungunya virus (CHIKV)** is an **arthropod-borne virus (arbovirus)** belonging to the **Togaviridae** family, genus **Alphavirus**. It causes **Chikungunya fever**, an acute febrile illness characterized by **fever, rash, and severe arthralgia**. The word "chikungunya" comes from the Makonde language, meaning "that which bends up" – describing the stooped posture from joint pain.

CHIKV is endemic in **Africa**, **Asia**, and has caused recent epidemics in **Europe** and the **Americas**. Transmission is mainly via **Aedes aegypti** and **Aedes albopictus** mosquitoes.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Togaviridae
    
- **Genus**: Alphavirus
    
- **Virus**: Chikungunya virus
    

#### **Structure**:

- **Enveloped**, spherical virion (~60–70 nm)
    
- **Positive-sense single-stranded RNA (~11.8 kb)**
    
- **Capsid**: icosahedral symmetry
    

#### **Genome Organization**:

- Two open reading frames (ORFs):
    
    - **5' ORF**: Non-structural proteins (nsP1–nsP4)
        
    - **3' ORF**: Structural proteins (C, E3, E2, 6K, E1)
        

#### **Key Proteins**:

- **nsP1**: RNA capping enzyme
    
- **nsP2**: Helicase, protease, and shut-off of host transcription
    
- **nsP3**: Role in replication complex
    
- **nsP4**: RNA-dependent RNA polymerase
    
- **E1/E2 glycoproteins**: Viral attachment, entry, and fusion
    
- **Capsid (C)**: RNA encapsidation and virion assembly
    

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Transmission and Entry**

- Spread by **mosquito bites**, primarily Aedes spp.
    
- Virus replicates initially in **fibroblasts**, **muscle progenitor cells**, **dermal dendritic cells**
    
- Disseminates via **viremia** to joints, liver, lymphoid tissue, CNS (rare)
    

#### **B. Cellular Entry**

- **E2 glycoprotein** binds to cellular receptors (e.g., MXRA8, TIM-1)
    
- Entry via **receptor-mediated endocytosis**
    
- Acidification triggers **E1-mediated membrane fusion**
    

#### **C. Replication Cycle**

1. **Translation** of nsP1–4 from genomic RNA
    
2. Formation of **replication complex**
    
3. Synthesis of **subgenomic RNA** encoding structural proteins
    
4. Assembly at **plasma membrane**, **budding**, and **release**
    

#### **D. Immunopathogenesis**

- **Acute phase**: High viremia, innate immune activation (IFN-α/β)
    
- **Chronic arthritis**: Viral RNA persists in **macrophages, synoviocytes**
    
    - Associated with **IL-6**, **MCP-1**, **TNF-α**
        
    - Immune-mediated damage to **cartilage and joint capsule**
        

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **A. Incubation Period**: 2–7 days

#### **B. Acute Phase (3–10 days)**:

- **High-grade fever** (often abrupt)
    
- **Polyarthralgia/polyarthritis**:
    
    - Severe, symmetric
        
    - Involves small joints (wrists, ankles, fingers)
        
- **Rash**: Maculopapular, face and trunk
    
- **Other symptoms**:
    
    - Myalgia
        
    - Headache
        
    - Conjunctivitis
        
    - Gastrointestinal upset
        

#### **C. Chronic Phase (post-viral syndrome)**:

- Lasts weeks to months (sometimes >1 year)
    
- Persistent **arthralgia**, **morning stiffness**, **tenosynovitis**
    
- Mimics **rheumatoid arthritis** in chronic cases
    

---

### **5. Complications**

- **Chronic arthritis** (most common long-term effect)
    
- **Neurological involvement** (rare):
    
    - Meningoencephalitis
        
    - Guillain-Barré syndrome
        
    - Myelitis
        
- **Myocarditis**
    
- **Mother-to-child transmission** (esp. during delivery): neonatal meningoencephalitis
    
- **Severe disease** more common in elderly, neonates, and immunocompromised
    

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Laboratory Diagnosis**

- **RT-PCR** (first 5–7 days of illness): Detects viral RNA in serum
    
- **Serology**:
    
    - **IgM ELISA**: Positive from day 5–7, peaks at 3–5 weeks
        
    - **IgG**: Indicates past exposure
        

#### **B. Hematology**

- Lymphopenia
    
- Mild thrombocytopenia
    
- Elevated liver enzymes
    

#### **C. Differential Diagnosis**

|Condition|Distinctive Features|
|---|---|
|**Dengue virus**|Retro-orbital pain, petechiae, marked thrombocytopenia|
|**Zika virus**|Conjunctivitis, mild symptoms, more fetal risk|
|**Rheumatoid arthritis**|Positive RF, anti-CCP; chronic progression|
|**Leptospirosis**|Conjunctival suffusion, renal involvement|
|**Malaria**|Cyclical fever, positive thick/thin smear|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. Supportive Only (No Specific Antiviral)**

- **Acute phase**:
    
    - Acetaminophen (avoid NSAIDs until dengue is ruled out)
        
    - Hydration
        
    - Bed rest
        
- **Chronic phase**:
    
    - NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen)
        
    - Corticosteroids for severe arthritis (low-dose, short-term)
        
    - DMARDs (methotrexate, hydroxychloroquine) in **refractory arthritis**
        

---

### **8. Prevention**

#### **A. Vector Control**

- Eliminate mosquito breeding sites (standing water)
    
- Insecticide spraying
    
- **Personal protection**: DEET repellents, long sleeves, bed nets
    

#### **B. Vaccine Development**

- **No licensed vaccine** yet (several candidates in clinical trials)

## **Togaviridae – Rubella Virus**

---

### **1. Introduction**

**Rubella virus**, the sole member of the **Rubivirus** genus within the **Togaviridae** family, is the etiologic agent of **Rubella** (also known as German measles), a generally mild exanthematous illness. However, **congenital rubella syndrome (CRS)**—caused by maternal infection during early pregnancy—can lead to severe fetal anomalies or death.

Unlike most Togaviridae members, **Rubella virus is not an arbovirus**; it is **transmitted via respiratory droplets**, primarily affecting children and unvaccinated adults.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Togaviridae
    
- **Genus**: Rubivirus
    
- **Virus**: Rubella virus
    

#### **Structure**:

- **Enveloped**, spherical virion (~60 nm)
    
- **Positive-sense, single-stranded RNA (~9.7 kb)**
    
- **Capsid**: Icosahedral symmetry
    

#### **Genome Organization**:

- Two open reading frames (ORFs):
    
    - **5' ORF**: Non-structural proteins (p150, p90)
        
    - **3' ORF**: Structural proteins (C, E2, E1)
        

#### **Key Proteins**:

- **Capsid protein (C)**: RNA packaging
    
- **Envelope proteins**:
    
    - **E1**: Major surface glycoprotein (binds host cell receptors; induces neutralizing antibodies)
        
    - **E2**: Role in virion assembly
        

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Entry and Spread**

- Transmission via **respiratory droplets**
    
- Virus infects **nasopharyngeal epithelium**
    
- Initial replication in **respiratory mucosa and regional lymph nodes**
    
- Viremia follows, spreading to skin and other organs
    

#### **B. Host Immune Response**

- **Type I interferon** response controls early replication
    
- **Cell-mediated immunity** important in viral clearance
    
- **Humoral immunity** confers lifelong protection
    

#### **C. Congenital Infection**

- Occurs when **rubella virus crosses placenta**, especially during **first trimester**
    
- Infects **placental and fetal endothelial cells**, leading to:
    
    - Growth restriction
        
    - Organogenesis disruption
        
    - Chronic infection of fetal tissues
        

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **A. Postnatal Rubella**

- **Incubation**: 14–21 days
    
- **Prodrome** (in teens/adults): Mild fever, malaise, lymphadenopathy (suboccipital, posterior cervical, postauricular)
    
- **Exanthem**:
    
    - Pink maculopapular rash, begins on face, spreads caudally
        
    - Rash lasts ~3 days
        
- **Enanthem**:
    
    - **Forchheimer spots**: Petechiae on soft palate
        
- **Arthralgia/arthritis** (especially in adolescent/adult females)
    

#### **B. Congenital Rubella Syndrome (CRS)**

- Occurs when maternal infection happens in the **first 12 weeks**
    
- **Classic triad**:
    
    - **Sensorineural deafness**
        
    - **Congenital cataracts**
        
    - **Cardiac defects** (e.g., PDA, pulmonary artery stenosis)
        
- Other findings:
    
    - Microcephaly
        
    - Hepatosplenomegaly
        
    - "Blueberry muffin" rash (dermal erythropoiesis)
        
    - Growth retardation
        
    - Intellectual disability
        

---

### **5. Complications**

- **Arthritis/arthralgia** in adults
    
- **Encephalitis** (rare, ~1 in 6,000 cases)
    
- **Thrombocytopenic purpura**
    
- **Progressive rubella panencephalitis** (very rare, years after infection)
    
- **Congenital rubella syndrome**: lifelong disability or death
    

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Clinical Diagnosis**

- Based on characteristic rash, lymphadenopathy, epidemiology
    
- History of vaccination reduces likelihood
    

#### **B. Laboratory Diagnosis**

- **RT-PCR**: Nasopharyngeal swab or blood (acute phase)
    
- **Serology**:
    
    - **IgM**: Detected during acute illness (up to 6 weeks)
        
    - **IgG**: Indicates past infection or vaccination
        
    - **IgG avidity testing**: Helps determine recent vs. past infection
        

#### **C. Diagnosis of CRS**

- Detection of **rubella-specific IgM** in infant serum
    
- Persistent **rubella IgG >6 months**
    
- Viral isolation or PCR from nasopharynx, urine, CSF
    

#### **D. Differential Diagnosis**

|Condition|Distinguishing Features|
|---|---|
|**Measles**|Koplik spots, confluent rash, higher fever|
|**Erythema infectiosum (B19)**|"Slapped cheek" rash; arthropathy in adults|
|**Roseola (HHV-6)**|High fever preceding rash|
|**Enteroviral rash**|Often involves palms/soles, oral ulcers|
|**Drug eruption**|Often pruritic, history of recent medication|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

- **Supportive care only**:
    
    - Antipyretics
        
    - Analgesics for arthralgia
        
    - Isolation to prevent transmission (especially pregnant women)
        
- **CRS**:
    
    - Multidisciplinary management (audiology, ophthalmology, cardiology)
        
    - No antiviral therapy available
        

---

### **8. Prevention**

#### **A. Vaccination (Key Strategy)**

- **Live attenuated rubella vaccine**, part of:
    
    - **MMR** (Measles-Mumps-Rubella)
        
    - **MMRV** (includes Varicella)
        
- **Schedule**:
    
    - First dose: 12–15 months
        
    - Second dose: 4–6 years
        
- **Contraindications**:
    
    - Pregnancy
        
    - Immunosuppression (live virus)
        
- **Maternal Screening**:
    
    - All pregnant women should have rubella IgG tested
        
    - Vaccinate **postpartum** if non-immune
        

#### **B. Public Health**

- Surveillance of CRS cases
    
- Outbreak containment through ring vaccination
