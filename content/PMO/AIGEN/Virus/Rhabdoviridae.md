## Rotavirus – Reoviridae

---

### 1. Introduction

**Rotavirus** is the leading cause of severe gastroenteritis in infants and young children worldwide. It belongs to the **Reoviridae** family and **Rotavirus** genus. This virus is highly contagious and is responsible for hundreds of thousands of hospitalizations and deaths globally, particularly in developing countries.

---

### 2. Virological Profile / Characteristics

- **Family:** Reoviridae
    
- **Genus:** Rotavirus
    
- **Genome:** Segmented double-stranded RNA (11 segments)
    
- **Capsid:** Triple-layered icosahedral (core, intermediate, outer layer)
    
- **Envelope:** None
    
- **Transmission:** Fecal-oral route; highly stable in the environment
    

#### Key Proteins

|Protein|Function|
|---|---|
|**VP4**|Forms outer spikes, mediates attachment and entry (cleaved into VP5* and VP8*)|
|**VP6**|Middle layer; used for serotyping (group A–G)|
|**VP7**|Outer capsid glycoprotein; defines G-type specificity|
|**NSP4**|Non-structural protein; acts as an enterotoxin, induces diarrhea|
|**VP1**|RNA-dependent RNA polymerase|

---

### 3. Pathophysiology

1. **Entry**: Ingested virus infects mature enterocytes in the small intestine
    
2. **Attachment**: VP4 binds to sialic acid receptors and is cleaved by trypsin to activate infectivity
    
3. **Replication**: Occurs in the cytoplasm; viral RNA segments are transcribed within the core
    
4. **Cellular damage**:
    
    - Enterocyte death leads to malabsorption
        
    - **NSP4** acts as a viral enterotoxin, stimulating chloride and water secretion
        
    - Disruption of intestinal tight junctions
        
5. **Immune response**: Innate (interferons, NK cells) and adaptive immunity (IgA)
    

---

### 4. Clinical Manifestations

- **Incubation period**: ~2 days
    
- **Gastroenteritis**:
    
    - Vomiting
        
    - Watery diarrhea (non-bloody)
        
    - Fever
        
    - Dehydration (can be severe)
        
- Most severe in children <5 years; adults and older children often asymptomatic or mild
    

---

### 5. Complications

- **Severe dehydration** and electrolyte imbalance
    
- **Hypovolemic shock**
    
- **Seizures** (febrile or afebrile, possibly from neuroinvasion or electrolyte imbalance)
    
- **Death**, especially in malnourished children in low-resource settings
    

---

### 6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis

#### Diagnostics:

- **Stool antigen detection**: Enzyme immunoassay (EIA)
    
- **RT-PCR**: Detects and genotypes virus
    
- **Electron microscopy**: Research or outbreak investigation
    

#### Differential Diagnosis:

|Condition|Distinguishing Features|
|---|---|
|Norovirus|Affects all ages, shorter duration, explosive outbreaks|
|Adenovirus 40/41|Longer incubation and illness duration, more common in older children|
|Bacterial gastroenteritis|Blood/mucus in stool, higher CRP, leukocytosis|
|Giardia infection|Greasy, foul-smelling stools, chronic course|

---

### 7. Treatment and Management

- **Rehydration therapy**:
    
    - Oral rehydration salts (ORS)
        
    - IV fluids for severe dehydration
        
- **Zinc supplementation** (especially in developing countries)
    
- **Antiemetics** (if vomiting is severe)
    
- No antiviral therapy exists
    

---

### 8. Prevention

- **Vaccination**:
    
    - Live attenuated oral vaccines (e.g., Rotarix, RotaTeq)
        
    - Reduces severe disease, hospitalizations, and mortality
        
- **Hygiene**:
    
    - Handwashing
        
    - Surface disinfection
        
- **Breastfeeding**: Confers protective antibodies
