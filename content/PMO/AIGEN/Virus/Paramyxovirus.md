
## **Paramyxoviridae – Measles Virus (MeV)**

---

### **1. Introduction**

**Measles virus (MeV)** is a highly contagious **negative-sense, single-stranded RNA virus** responsible for **measles (rubeola)**, a serious systemic illness characterized by **fever, rash, cough, and immunosuppression**. It has a high **basic reproduction number (R₀ ~12–18)**, making it one of the most infectious human pathogens.

Before widespread vaccination, measles was a major cause of **childhood mortality** globally. Despite the availability of a highly effective vaccine, outbreaks still occur due to **vaccine hesitancy**, **waning immunity**, or **incomplete vaccination coverage**.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Paramyxoviridae
    
- **Genus**: Morbillivirus
    
- **Species**: Measles morbillivirus
    
- **Genome**: Linear **negative-sense, single-stranded RNA (~16 kb)**
    
- **Envelope**: Yes
    
- **Capsid symmetry**: Helical
    
- **Replication site**: Cytoplasm
    

#### **Structural Proteins and Functions**

|Protein|Function|
|---|---|
|**H (Hemagglutinin)**|Binds to cellular receptors (SLAM/CD150, Nectin-4)|
|**F (Fusion)**|Mediates membrane fusion and syncytium formation|
|**N (Nucleocapsid)**|Encapsidates viral RNA|
|**P (Phosphoprotein)**|Polymerase co-factor; part of replication complex|
|**L (Large protein)**|RNA-dependent RNA polymerase|
|**M (Matrix)**|Assembly and budding of virions|

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Entry and Primary Replication**

1. **Attachment**:
    
    - Virus binds via **H protein** to **SLAM/CD150** on immune cells (macrophages, dendritic cells).
        
2. **Fusion and Entry**:
    
    - **F protein** facilitates fusion of viral envelope with host membrane.
        
3. **Replication**:
    
    - In cytoplasm using viral RNA polymerase (L + P proteins)
        
    - Transcription and translation occur for viral proteins
        

#### **B. Systemic Spread**

- Infected immune cells carry virus to **lymphoid tissue** → **viremia**
    
- Secondary spread to **respiratory epithelium**, **skin**, **CNS**, **conjunctiva**
    

#### **C. Immunosuppression**

- MeV depletes preexisting immune memory (termed **“immune amnesia”**)
    
- Infection of **T and B lymphocytes** impairs immunity for months
    

#### **D. CNS Invasion**

- Mechanism unclear, may be via infected leukocytes or olfactory neurons
    
- Basis for rare complications like **subacute sclerosing panencephalitis (SSPE)**
    

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **Incubation Period**: 7–14 days

#### **Stages**

1. **Prodromal (Catarrhal) Phase**:
    
    - High **fever**, **malaise**, **conjunctivitis**, **coryza**, **cough** (the 3 C’s)
        
    - **Koplik spots**: Bluish-white lesions on buccal mucosa (pathognomonic)
        
2. **Exanthem Phase**:
    
    - **Maculopapular rash** starting at **hairline**, spreading **downward**
        
    - Rash becomes **confluent**, fades in the same order
        
    - Rash typically lasts 5–6 days
        
3. **Recovery / Convalescent Phase**:
    
    - Symptoms resolve; rash may desquamate
        
    - Immune suppression persists for weeks
        

---

### **5. Complications**

|Complication Type|Examples|
|---|---|
|**Respiratory**|Pneumonia (most common cause of measles-related death), bronchiolitis|
|**Neurologic**|Acute disseminated encephalomyelitis (ADEM), **SSPE** (fatal, years later)|
|**Ophthalmologic**|Keratitis, blindness (especially in vitamin A–deficient children)|
|**Other**|Otitis media, diarrhea, myocarditis, laryngotracheobronchitis (croup)|

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Clinical Diagnosis**

- Characteristic **rash**, **fever**, **Koplik spots**, **3 C’s**
    
- Known exposure or outbreak context
    

#### **B. Laboratory Confirmation**

- **RT-PCR**: Nasopharyngeal swabs, urine, or blood (preferred)
    
- **Serology**: Measles-specific **IgM** (acute) and **IgG** (seroconversion or past infection)
    
- **Leukopenia** common on CBC
    

#### **C. Histology (rarely needed)**:

- **Warthin-Finkeldey giant cells**: Multinucleated giant cells with inclusion bodies in lymphoid tissue
    

#### **D. Differential Diagnosis**

|Disease|Distinguishing Features|
|---|---|
|**Rubella**|Rash less intense, no Koplik spots, shorter prodrome|
|**Roseola (HHV-6)**|Rash appears after fever resolves|
|**Scarlet fever**|Strawberry tongue, sandpaper rash, Group A Strep|
|**Dengue**|Hemorrhagic features, retro-orbital pain|
|**Drug reaction**|History of medication use, lacks systemic prodrome|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. Supportive Care**

- Hydration, antipyretics, antitussives
    
- Isolate for **4 days after rash onset**
    

#### **B. Vitamin A Supplementation**

- Reduces morbidity and mortality
    
- **WHO recommendation**:
    
    - **<6 months**: 50,000 IU daily for 2 days
        
    - **6–12 months**: 100,000 IU
        
    - **>12 months**: 200,000 IU
        

#### **C. Treat Secondary Infections**

- Bacterial pneumonia, otitis media
    

#### **D. Hospitalization**

- Required for complications, infants, or immunocompromised
    

---

### **8. Prevention**

#### **A. Vaccination**

- **MMR vaccine**: Live attenuated
    
    - First dose: 9–12 months (6 months in outbreak/travel setting)
        
    - Second dose: At least 4 weeks after first
        
    - **Effectiveness**: ~95% after two doses
        
- Combined with **mumps** and **rubella**
    

#### **B. Post-exposure Prophylaxis**

- **MMR vaccine** within 72 hours of exposure (if unimmunized)
    
- **Immunoglobulin** within 6 days for high-risk individuals:
    
    - Pregnant women, infants <6 months, immunocompromised

## **Mumps Virus (MuV)**

---

### **1. Introduction**

**Mumps virus (MuV)** is a **highly contagious** RNA virus that primarily affects the **salivary glands**, especially the **parotid glands**, and can spread to other organs. It is a **human-only pathogen**, with outbreaks often occurring in **close-contact settings** such as schools, colleges, and military barracks.

Despite widespread vaccination, **mumps outbreaks continue to occur**, especially among **young adults** in partially immune populations.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Paramyxoviridae
    
- **Genus**: Rubulavirus
    
- **Genome**: Negative-sense, single-stranded RNA (~15.3 kb)
    
- **Envelope**: Yes
    
- **Capsid symmetry**: Helical
    
- **Replication site**: Cytoplasm
    

#### **Key Proteins and Their Functions**

|Protein|Function|
|---|---|
|**HN (Hemagglutinin-Neuraminidase)**|Mediates binding to **sialic acid receptors**; promotes release of virions|
|**F (Fusion)**|Facilitates membrane fusion, syncytium formation|
|**N (Nucleocapsid)**|RNA genome encapsidation|
|**P (Phosphoprotein)**|Polymerase cofactor|
|**L (Large protein)**|RNA-dependent RNA polymerase|
|**M (Matrix)**|Assists in viral assembly and budding|
|**V protein**|Interferon antagonist; inhibits IFN response and apoptosis|

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Entry and Initial Replication**

- Virus enters through the **respiratory tract**
    
- Binds via **HN protein** to **sialic acid** on host cells
    
- Fusion via **F protein** → cytoplasmic release of RNP complex
    
- Replication occurs entirely in the **cytoplasm** using the **L + P complex**
    

#### **B. Viremia and Dissemination**

- Primary replication in **upper respiratory epithelium** and **local lymph nodes**
    
- Enters bloodstream → **viremia**
    
- Disseminates to **parotid glands**, **testes**, **CNS**, **pancreas**, and other organs
    

#### **C. Immunomodulation**

- **V protein** inhibits **interferon signaling**, enhancing viral replication
    
- Cell-mediated immunity clears the virus but also contributes to inflammation
    

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **Incubation Period**: ~16–18 days (range: 12–25 days)

#### **Typical Course**:

|Phase|Clinical Features|
|---|---|
|**Prodrome**|Low-grade fever, malaise, headache, myalgia|
|**Parotitis**|Unilateral → bilateral painful **parotid gland swelling** (within 1–2 days), worse with chewing or sour foods|
|**Other organ involvement**:||

- **Orchitis**: Painful swelling of testis (1/3 of post-pubertal males), can lead to infertility
    
- **Oophoritis**: Lower abdominal pain in females (rare)
    
- **Meningitis**: Aseptic; usually mild, can occur without parotitis
    
- **Encephalitis**: Rare, more severe
    
- **Pancreatitis**: Epigastric pain, vomiting
    
- **Hearing loss**: Sensorineural, usually unilateral (rare)
    

---

### **5. Complications**

|Complication|Notes|
|---|---|
|**Orchitis**|Most common complication; unilateral; may cause testicular atrophy|
|**Meningitis**|~10% of cases; typically resolves without sequelae|
|**Encephalitis**|Rare but serious; risk of seizures, neurologic damage|
|**Deafness**|Rare but can be permanent|
|**Pancreatitis**|Self-limiting; previously suspected in T1DM etiology|
|**Infertility**|Rare; mostly if bilateral orchitis occurs|
|**Miscarriage**|1st-trimester mumps infection may increase miscarriage risk|

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Clinical Suspicion**

- Classic triad: **Fever**, **parotid swelling**, **pain with chewing**
    
- Recent exposure or outbreak
    

#### **B. Laboratory Confirmation**

- **RT-PCR**: Buccal/throat swab within 3 days of symptom onset
    
- **Serology**:
    
    - **IgM**: Detectable after symptom onset (1–2 days)
        
    - **IgG**: For past infection or post-vaccination status
        
- **CSF analysis** (if CNS symptoms): Lymphocytic pleocytosis; PCR for viral RNA
    

#### **C. Differential Diagnosis**

|Condition|Distinguishing Features|
|---|---|
|**Bacterial parotitis (e.g., Staph)**|Unilateral, purulent discharge, systemic toxicity|
|**CMV / EBV infection**|Generalized lymphadenopathy, atypical lymphocytes|
|**Sialolithiasis**|Stone in duct, worse after meals, no fever|
|**HIV salivary disease**|Chronic bilateral swelling without pain|
|**Sjogren’s syndrome**|Dry eyes/mouth, older females, autoimmune profile|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. Supportive Therapy**

- **Analgesics and antipyretics**
    
- **Hydration**
    
- **Warm or cold compresses** for parotid swelling
    
- **Scrotal support and NSAIDs** for orchitis
    
- Isolation for **5 days after parotitis onset** (to reduce spread)
    

#### **B. Hospitalization**

- Severe CNS symptoms
    
- Intractable pain or complications
    

#### **C. No Antiviral Available**

---

### **8. Prevention**

#### **A. Vaccination**

- **MMR vaccine** (Measles-Mumps-Rubella)
    
    - Live attenuated
        
    - **First dose**: 12–15 months
        
    - **Second dose**: 4–6 years
        
    - **Effectiveness**: ~88% after two doses
        
    - Administered subcutaneously
        

#### **B. Post-exposure**

- No specific post-exposure prophylaxis
    
- MMR may be offered to unvaccinated individuals within 72 hours
    
- Immunoglobulin is **not** effective for mumps prevention
    

#### **C. Outbreak Control**

- Vaccinate high-risk populations (e.g., university students)
    
- Consider **third MMR dose** in outbreak settings for enhanced protection

## **Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV)**

---

### **1. Introduction**

**Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV)** is a **leading cause of lower respiratory tract infections** in **infants, young children, and the elderly**. It is responsible for **bronchiolitis**, **pneumonia**, and significant **hospitalizations** worldwide. RSV is particularly severe in **infants under 6 months**, **premature infants**, and **immunocompromised individuals**.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Paramyxoviridae
    
- **Genus**: Orthopneumovirus
    
- **Genome**: Linear, negative-sense, single-stranded RNA (~15.2 kb)
    
- **Envelope**: Yes
    
- **Capsid symmetry**: Helical
    
- **Replication site**: Cytoplasm
    
- **Notable trait**: **Lacks hemagglutinin-neuraminidase (HN)**—unlike many paramyxoviruses
    

#### **Key Viral Proteins**

|Protein|Function|
|---|---|
|**G protein**|Mediates attachment to host epithelial cells (via CX3CR1 and heparan sulfate)|
|**F protein**|Causes membrane fusion and **syncytia** formation|
|**N (nucleocapsid)**|Encapsulates RNA|
|**P (phosphoprotein)**|Polymerase cofactor|
|**L (large protein)**|RNA-dependent RNA polymerase|
|**NS1 and NS2**|Interferon antagonists—block IFN response, suppress innate immunity|
|**SH protein**|Viroporin; contributes to inflammation and cell death|

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Entry and Replication**

- **Entry**: G protein binds to **CX3CR1** on airway epithelial cells
    
- **Fusion**: F protein mediates fusion with host membrane → cytoplasmic entry
    
- **Replication**: Transcription and replication occur entirely in cytoplasm using L + P complex
    

#### **B. Epithelial Infection**

- Targets **bronchiolar epithelial cells** → cell damage, necrosis, and **syncytia**
    
- **Sloughing of epithelium**, **mucus plugging**, and **narrowing of airways**
    

#### **C. Immune Response and Immunopathology**

- NS1 and NS2 proteins inhibit type I interferon response
    
- Infiltration of **mononuclear cells**, **T cells**, and **eosinophils**
    
- Immunopathology contributes to **wheezing**, **airway obstruction**, and **recurrent wheezing** post-infection
    

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **Incubation Period**: 4–6 days

#### **Typical Presentations by Age**

|Age Group|Common Presentations|
|---|---|
|**Infants (<1 year)**|**Bronchiolitis** (wheezing, crackles, hypoxia, retractions), apnea|
|**Toddlers**|Cough, wheezing, pneumonia|
|**Older children/adults**|Mild cold-like illness|
|**Elderly / Immunocompromised**|Severe pneumonia, respiratory failure|

#### **Symptoms**

- Rhinorrhea
    
- Cough
    
- Wheezing
    
- Tachypnea
    
- Fever
    
- Apnea (especially in neonates)
    
- Cyanosis in severe cases
    

---

### **5. Complications**

|Complication|Notes|
|---|---|
|**Bronchiolitis**|Most common severe manifestation in infants|
|**Pneumonia**|Viral; bacterial superinfection possible|
|**Apnea**|Particularly in neonates and preemies|
|**Respiratory failure**|Especially in premature, elderly, or immunocompromised|
|**Asthma / Recurrent wheezing**|Post-infection sequelae linked to early RSV infection|
|**Otitis media**|Common in toddlers and infants|
|**Cardiac involvement**|Rare (myocarditis, arrhythmias)|

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Clinical Diagnosis**

- Suspected in infants with bronchiolitis during RSV season (late fall to early spring)
    

#### **B. Laboratory Testing**

- **RT-PCR**: Preferred method; sensitive and specific
    
- **Rapid antigen testing**: Available, especially in hospital settings
    
- **Viral culture**: Time-consuming, rarely used
    
- **Chest X-ray** (if needed): Hyperinflation, peribronchial thickening, patchy infiltrates
    

#### **C. Differential Diagnosis**

|Condition|Distinguishing Features|
|---|---|
|**Influenza**|Higher fever, myalgias, abrupt onset|
|**Parainfluenza (HPIV)**|Croup-like symptoms (barking cough, stridor)|
|**Adenovirus**|Conjunctivitis, GI symptoms, longer fever|
|**Human metapneumovirus**|Clinically similar, co-circulates with RSV|
|**Asthma**|Recurrent, not seasonal; personal/family history|
|**Bacterial pneumonia**|Focal findings, higher inflammatory markers, consolidation on X-ray|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. Supportive Care (Mainstay)**

- **Oxygen therapy** if hypoxic
    
- **Suctioning** of nasal secretions
    
- **Hydration support** (oral or IV)
    
- Monitor for apnea or respiratory failure
    

#### **B. Pharmacologic Therapies**

- **Bronchodilators**: Trial possible; not routinely recommended
    
- **Corticosteroids**: Generally not effective in bronchiolitis
    
- **Antivirals**:
    
    - **Ribavirin** (inhaled): Limited to severe cases in immunocompromised patients
        

#### **C. Hospitalization Criteria**

- Severe respiratory distress
    
- Hypoxia
    
- Inability to feed
    
- Apnea
    
- Underlying heart/lung disease
    

---

### **8. Prevention**

#### **A. Monoclonal Antibody Therapy**

- **Palivizumab** (Synagis): Humanized monoclonal antibody against **F protein**
    
    - Given **monthly IM during RSV season**
        
    - **Indications**:
        
        - Premature infants (<29 weeks gestation)
            
        - Infants with chronic lung disease of prematurity
            
        - Infants with hemodynamically significant congenital heart disease
            
- **Nirsevimab (Beyfortus)**:
    
    - New long-acting monoclonal antibody (approved in 2022)
        
    - **Single dose** protection for an entire season
        
    - Recommended for all infants in some countries
        

#### **B. RSV Vaccine (New Developments)**

- **RSVPreF3 (Arexvy)** and **Abrysvo**:
    
    - **Approved for elderly adults and pregnant women** (to protect newborns)
        
    - Target prefusion F protein

## **Human Parainfluenza Viruses (HPIVs)**

---

### **1. Introduction**

**Human Parainfluenza Viruses (HPIVs)** are important respiratory pathogens causing **upper and lower respiratory tract infections** across all age groups, especially in **infants and young children**. HPIVs are second only to RSV as causes of **hospitalizations for viral respiratory disease** in children under 5 years.

There are **four main serotypes**:

- **HPIV-1** and **HPIV-2**: Common causes of **croup (laryngotracheobronchitis)**
    
- **HPIV-3**: Associated with **bronchiolitis and pneumonia**
    
- **HPIV-4**: Milder, often underdiagnosed
    

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Paramyxoviridae
    
- **Genus**: Respirovirus (HPIV-1 and -3), Rubulavirus (HPIV-2 and -4)
    
- **Genome**: Linear, negative-sense, single-stranded RNA (~15.5 kb)
    
- **Envelope**: Yes
    
- **Capsid symmetry**: Helical
    
- **Replication site**: Cytoplasm
    

#### **Key Viral Proteins**

|Protein|Function|
|---|---|
|**HN (Hemagglutinin-Neuraminidase)**|Attaches to sialic acid receptors, promotes viral entry and release|
|**F (Fusion protein)**|Facilitates fusion of viral and host membranes; responsible for syncytia formation|
|**N (Nucleocapsid)**|Binds and encapsidates viral RNA|
|**P (Phosphoprotein)**|Cofactor for RNA polymerase|
|**L (Large protein)**|RNA-dependent RNA polymerase|
|**C/V proteins (in HPIV-2/4)**|Modulate host immunity, suppress interferon signaling|

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Entry and Spread**

- **Attachment** via HN to sialic acid on respiratory epithelial cells
    
- **F protein** mediates membrane fusion → syncytia formation
    
- Replication occurs in **cytoplasm**
    
- Local infection → inflammation, epithelial necrosis, edema, and mucus plugging
    

#### **B. Viral Tropism**

- **HPIV-1 and 2**: Predominantly upper airway (larynx, trachea)
    
- **HPIV-3**: Lower airway (bronchioles, alveoli)
    
- No systemic viremia → disease limited to respiratory tract
    

#### **C. Immune Evasion**

- V and C proteins inhibit interferon responses
    
- Virus persists in airways, can lead to recurrent infections
    

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **Incubation Period**: 2–6 days

|Virus|Peak Age|Common Diseases|
|---|---|---|
|**HPIV-1**|7–36 months|**Croup**, laryngotracheobronchitis|
|**HPIV-2**|6–36 months|Croup (milder than HPIV-1)|
|**HPIV-3**|<12 months|**Bronchiolitis**, **pneumonia**|
|**HPIV-4**|All ages|Mild upper respiratory illness|

#### **Symptoms**

- **Croup**: Barking cough, hoarseness, inspiratory stridor, low-grade fever
    
- **Bronchiolitis/Pneumonia**: Cough, wheezing, tachypnea, hypoxia
    
- URI symptoms: Rhinorrhea, sore throat, fever
    

---

### **5. Complications**

|Complication|Notes|
|---|---|
|**Severe croup**|May cause airway obstruction, require intubation|
|**Pneumonia**|Particularly with HPIV-3; often bilateral and interstitial|
|**Bronchiolitis**|In infants; clinically overlaps with RSV|
|**Otitis media**|Common coinfection in young children|
|**Asthma exacerbation**|In children with reactive airway disease|
|**Immunocompromised patients**|Risk for severe, disseminated lower respiratory disease|

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Clinical Suspicion**

- Typical presentation of croup, bronchiolitis, or pneumonia in infants or toddlers
    
- Seasonality:
    
    - **HPIV-1**: Fall (biennial outbreaks)
        
    - **HPIV-3**: Spring/summer
        
    - **HPIV-2 and 4**: Sporadic, year-round
        

#### **B. Laboratory Testing**

- **RT-PCR**: Preferred; highly sensitive
    
- **Rapid antigen detection**: Nasopharyngeal swab (less sensitive)
    
- **Viral culture**: Slower but still used in some labs
    
- **Chest X-ray** (if needed):
    
    - **Croup**: Steeple sign (subglottic narrowing)
        
    - **Pneumonia**: Patchy infiltrates or hyperinflation
        

#### **C. Differential Diagnosis**

|Condition|Key Distinctions|
|---|---|
|**RSV**|Younger infants, more wheezing, apnea|
|**Influenza**|Sudden high fever, myalgias, more systemic|
|**Epiglottitis (Hib)**|Drooling, high fever, tripod position, rapid onset|
|**Bacterial tracheitis**|Toxic appearance, purulent secretions, poor response to croup treatment|
|**COVID-19**|Variable; testing required to rule out|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. Supportive Care**

- **Croup**:
    
    - **Mild**: Humidified air, supportive care
        
    - **Moderate/severe**:
        
        - **Dexamethasone**: Single dose (0.6 mg/kg PO/IM/IV)
            
        - **Nebulized epinephrine**: For stridor at rest
            
- **Bronchiolitis/Pneumonia**:
    
    - Oxygen therapy
        
    - Suctioning, hydration
        
    - Monitor for respiratory distress
        

#### **B. Antivirals**

- No specific antiviral available
    
- Ribavirin has shown limited in vitro activity; not routinely used
    

---

### **8. Prevention**

#### **A. Vaccine**

- No approved vaccine yet
    
- Research ongoing for live-attenuated and vector-based candidates
    

#### **B. Infection Control**

- Hand hygiene
    
- Droplet precautions in healthcare settings
    
- Avoidance of sick contacts for vulnerable individuals

## **Nipah Virus (NiV)**

---

### **1. Introduction**

**Nipah virus (NiV)** is a highly pathogenic zoonotic virus causing **severe encephalitis and respiratory disease** in humans. It was first identified in **Malaysia in 1998–99** during an outbreak among pig farmers. Since then, recurrent outbreaks have occurred in **Bangladesh, India**, and occasionally **Southeast Asia**, often with high case-fatality rates (40–75%).

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

- **Family**: Paramyxoviridae
    
- **Genus**: Henipavirus
    
- **Genome**: Linear, non-segmented, negative-sense single-stranded RNA (~18.2 kb)
    
- **Envelope**: Yes
    
- **Capsid symmetry**: Helical
    
- **Host range**: Broad, including humans, pigs, bats, and other mammals
    
- **Natural reservoir**: **Fruit bats (Pteropus spp.)**
    

#### **Key Viral Proteins**

|Protein|Function|
|---|---|
|**G (attachment glycoprotein)**|Binds to host ephrin-B2/B3 receptors (highly expressed in endothelial and neuronal tissue)|
|**F (fusion protein)**|Mediates membrane fusion and syncytia formation|
|**N (nucleocapsid)**|Encapsidates viral genome|
|**P (phosphoprotein)**|Cofactor for L, interferon antagonist|
|**L (large protein)**|RNA-dependent RNA polymerase|
|**C/V/W proteins**|Accessory proteins that antagonize host immune response|

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Entry and Spread**

- **Attachment**: G protein binds to **ephrin-B2/B3** receptors—present in neurons, endothelial cells, and smooth muscle
    
- **Fusion**: F protein allows fusion of viral envelope with host membrane
    
- **Replication**: Occurs in cytoplasm using L + P complex
    

#### **B. Systemic Spread**

- Virus spreads via **bloodstream** and **lymphatics** → disseminates to **brain**, **lungs**, and **endothelium**
    
- **Endothelial infection** leads to **vasculitis**, thrombosis, and hemorrhage
    
- **Neuroinvasion** causes **severe encephalitis**, often through:
    
    - Olfactory nerve spread
        
    - Hematogenous dissemination
        

#### **C. Immune Evasion**

- **C, V, and W proteins** interfere with **type I interferon** signaling
    
- **Suppresses innate and adaptive immune responses**, contributing to high virulence
    

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **Incubation period**: 4–14 days (up to 45 days)

#### **Presentations**

|Syndrome|Features|
|---|---|
|**Encephalitis**|Fever, headache, vomiting → confusion, myoclonus, seizures, coma|
|**Respiratory illness**|Cough, dyspnea, ARDS (common in Bangladesh strain)|
|**Asymptomatic**|Rare, but seroconversion possible in exposed individuals|

- Rapid progression to **coma** within 24–48 hours is common
    
- **Relapsing encephalitis** may occur months later
    

---

### **5. Complications**

|Complication|Notes|
|---|---|
|**Severe encephalitis**|Necrotizing vasculitis, brain edema, neuronal death|
|**Seizures, coma**|High intracranial pressure|
|**ARDS**|Common in recent outbreaks; rapid respiratory failure|
|**Multi-organ failure**|In severe systemic cases|
|**Relapsing encephalitis**|Seen in survivors weeks to months after recovery|
|**Death**|Case fatality ~40–75%, depending on care availability and viral strain|

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Laboratory Diagnosis**

- **RT-PCR**: Gold standard for acute diagnosis (blood, CSF, throat swab, urine)
    
- **ELISA**: IgM and IgG detection for retrospective or seroprevalence studies
    
- **Virus isolation**: Requires BSL-4 laboratory
    
- **Neuroimaging**: MRI may show multiple small white matter lesions (brainstem, cortex)
    

#### **B. Differential Diagnosis**

|Condition|Distinguishing Features|
|---|---|
|**Japanese encephalitis**|More gradual onset, vector exposure|
|**Herpes simplex encephalitis**|Frontal/temporal lobe involvement, HSV PCR+|
|**Rabies**|More prodrome, hydrophobia, longer course|
|**COVID-19**|Less CNS involvement in acute stages|
|**Bacterial meningitis**|Different CSF profile, less vasculitis|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. Supportive Care**

- Intensive supportive care: fluids, electrolytes, ventilation
    
- Management of **raised intracranial pressure**, **seizures**, and **shock**
    
- Mortality is high even with ICU-level support
    

#### **B. Antiviral Therapy (Experimental/Compassionate Use)**

- **Ribavirin**: Some use during Malaysian outbreak; unclear efficacy
    
- **Remdesivir**: Promising in animal models; under evaluation
    
- **Favipiravir**: Being investigated
    

#### **C. Monoclonal Antibodies**

- **m102.4**: Human monoclonal antibody targeting G protein
    
    - Demonstrated protection in animal models
        
    - Used under emergency compassionate protocols
        

---

### **8. Prevention**

#### **A. No Licensed Vaccine Yet**

- Several candidates under development:
    
    - **Subunit vaccines** (soluble G protein)
        
    - **ChAdOx1 NiV** (chimpanzee adenoviral vector)
        
    - **mRNA-based vaccines**
        

#### **B. Infection Control**

- Strict isolation and barrier precautions for suspected cases
    
- Avoid consumption of **raw date palm sap** (can be contaminated by bat secretions)
    
- **No direct contact with pigs or sick animals** in endemic areas
    

#### **C. Surveillance and Public Health**

- Important in **Bangladesh, India, Malaysia**
    
- **Zoonotic spillover** risk necessitates **One Health** approach (animals–humans–environment)
