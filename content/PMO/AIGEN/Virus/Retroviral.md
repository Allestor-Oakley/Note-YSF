
## **HIV-1**

### **1. Introduction**

HIV-1 is the primary causative agent of the global AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome) pandemic. It belongs to the **Lentivirus** genus within the **Retroviridae** family. First identified in the early 1980s, HIV-1 is responsible for over 95% of HIV infections globally.

### **2. Characteristics / Virological Profile**

- **Family:** Retroviridae
    
- **Genus:** Lentivirus
    
- **Genome:** Two identical copies of single-stranded, positive-sense RNA (~9.7 kb)
    
- **Structure:**
    
    - Enveloped virus
        
    - Icosahedral capsid
        
    - Diameter: ~100–120 nm
        
- **Key Proteins:**
    
    - **Env (gp160 → gp120 & gp41):** Envelope glycoproteins for host cell binding and fusion
        
    - **Gag (p24, p17):** Core and matrix proteins
        
    - **Pol:** Reverse transcriptase, integrase, and protease
        
    - **Tat, Rev, Nef, Vif, Vpr, Vpu:** Regulatory and accessory proteins critical for viral replication and immune evasion
        

### **3. Pathophysiology**

1. **Viral Entry:**
    
    - **gp120** binds to **CD4 receptors** on T-helper cells, macrophages, and dendritic cells.
        
    - Co-receptors **CCR5** (macrophage-tropic strains) or **CXCR4** (T-cell-tropic strains) facilitate entry.
        
    - **gp41** mediates fusion of viral and host membranes.
        
2. **Reverse Transcription:**
    
    - **Reverse transcriptase** converts viral RNA into double-stranded DNA (dsDNA) in the cytoplasm.
        
3. **Integration:**
    
    - **Integrase** inserts the viral DNA into the host genome, forming the **provirus**.
        
4. **Transcription & Translation:**
    
    - Host machinery transcribes and translates viral RNA.
        
    - **Tat** enhances transcription.
        
    - **Rev** facilitates nuclear export of unspliced/partially spliced transcripts.
        
5. **Assembly & Budding:**
    
    - Gag and Gag-Pol precursors assemble at the plasma membrane.
        
    - Immature virions bud and mature post-budding through **protease**-mediated cleavage.
        
6. **Immune Dysfunction:**
    
    - Progressive depletion of **CD4+ T cells** due to:
        
        - Direct viral killing
            
        - Apoptosis
            
        - CD8+ cytotoxic T cell-mediated killing
            
    - Chronic immune activation and lymphoid tissue destruction
        

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### Acute HIV Infection (2–4 weeks post-exposure):

- Fever, lymphadenopathy, pharyngitis, rash, myalgia, mucocutaneous ulcers
    
- Often resembles infectious mononucleosis
    

#### Clinical Latency:

- Asymptomatic period lasting years
    
- Persistent viral replication and gradual CD4+ decline
    

#### AIDS (CD4 < 200 cells/μL or AIDS-defining illness):

- Opportunistic infections: PCP, TB, CMV, cryptococcosis
    
- Malignancies: Kaposi’s sarcoma, NHL, cervical cancer
    
- HIV-associated neurocognitive disorder (HAND)
    

### **5. Complications**

- **Opportunistic infections** (based on CD4 count)
    
- **Malignancies** (esp. virus-associated like Kaposi’s sarcoma)
    
- **Cardiovascular disease** (due to chronic inflammation)
    
- **Renal complications** (HIV-associated nephropathy)
    
- **Neurocognitive decline**
    
- **Immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome (IRIS)** after ART initiation
    

### **6. Diagnostics**

#### Initial Screening:

- **4th-gen ELISA** (detects p24 antigen + HIV antibodies)
    
- **Rapid diagnostic tests** (point-of-care)
    

#### Confirmatory:

- **HIV-1 RNA PCR** (viral load)
    
- **Western blot** (now less used)
    
- **HIV-1/2 differentiation immunoassay**
    

#### Monitoring:

- **CD4+ T cell count**
    
- **Viral load (HIV RNA copies/mL)**
    

#### Differential Diagnosis:

- Infectious mononucleosis (EBV/CMV)
    
- Influenza
    
- Syphilis (rash, mucocutaneous lesions)
    
- Lymphoma (lymphadenopathy, fever, weight loss)
    

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### Antiretroviral Therapy (ART):

- **Recommended for all** HIV-positive patients regardless of CD4 count
    
- **Initial regimen** typically includes:
    
    - **2 NRTIs** (e.g., tenofovir + emtricitabine)
        
    - Plus one of:
        
        - **INSTI** (e.g., dolutegravir, bictegravir) – preferred
            
        - **NNRTI** (e.g., efavirenz)
            
        - **PI** (e.g., darunavir/ritonavir)
            

#### Management Principles:

- Lifelong adherence
    
- Monitor CD4 count and viral load
    
- Screen and treat co-infections (e.g., HBV, HCV, TB)
    
- Preventive care (e.g., vaccines, prophylaxis for opportunistic infections)

## **HIV-2**

### **1. Introduction**

HIV-2 is a less common type of HIV, primarily endemic to West Africa. It is less transmissible and has a slower progression to AIDS than HIV-1.

### **2. Characteristics / Virological Profile**

- **Family:** Retroviridae
    
- **Genus:** Lentivirus
    
- **Genome:** Two identical ssRNA molecules
    
- **Structure:** Similar to HIV-1, but genetically distinct
    
- **Key Differences:**
    
    - Shares ~55% sequence homology with HIV-1
        
    - Different **env** and **accessory proteins**
        
    - Uses **CD4** and **CCR5** (less often CXCR4) for entry
        

### **3. Pathophysiology**

Same basic replication cycle as HIV-1, with differences:

- **Slower replication rate**
    
- **Lower plasma viral load**
    
- **Accessory proteins:** No **Vpu** (uses **Vpx** instead)
    
- **Immune evasion** is less effective, leading to better immune control in many patients
    

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

- Similar to HIV-1, but:
    
    - **Longer asymptomatic phase**
        
    - **Lower viral load**
        
    - **Delayed progression to AIDS**
        
- Opportunistic infections and AIDS-defining illnesses may occur, often later than in HIV-1
    

### **5. Complications**

Same as HIV-1, but generally less frequent and delayed

- Opportunistic infections
    
- Malignancies
    
- HIV-associated dementia and nephropathy (less common)
    

### **6. Diagnostics**

#### Specific Challenges:

- Standard HIV-1 tests may miss HIV-2 or yield indeterminate results
    

#### Recommended Tests:

- **HIV-1/2 differentiation immunoassay**
    
- **HIV-2–specific nucleic acid testing (NAT)** (PCR for RNA)
    
- **HIV-2–specific Western blot**
    

#### Differential Diagnosis:

Same as HIV-1, but include HIV-1 as a key differential

### **7. Treatment and Management**

- **ART is still essential**; WHO recommends treating all HIV-2 patients
    
- **INSTIs** (e.g., dolutegravir) are effective
    
- **NNRTIs are NOT effective** (HIV-2 is intrinsically resistant)
    
- Monitoring HIV-2 viral load may be less accessible but is important
    
- Same preventive and supportive care as HIV-1
