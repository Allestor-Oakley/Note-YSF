	
## **Rotavirus**

**Family**: Reoviridae  
**Genus**: _Rotavirus_

---

### **1. Introduction**

**Rotavirus** is a **double-stranded RNA virus** and a **leading cause of severe dehydrating gastroenteritis** in infants and young children worldwide. Prior to widespread vaccination, rotavirus was responsible for **over 500,000 deaths annually** in children under 5. Even in high-income countries, it was a major cause of hospitalizations.

---

### **2. Virological Profile / Characteristics**

|Feature|Description|
|---|---|
|**Genome**|Segmented dsRNA, 11 segments|
|**Capsid**|Triple-layered, icosahedral symmetry|
|**Envelope**|**No** (non-enveloped)|
|**Size**|~70–75 nm in diameter|
|**Replication site**|Cytoplasm|
|**Serogroups**|9 identified (A–I); **Group A** is the most common in humans|

#### **Key Structural Proteins**

|Protein|Function|
|---|---|
|**VP4 (spike)**|Mediates attachment to cell receptors and penetration; cleaved into VP5* and VP8*|
|**VP6**|Major group-specific antigen, forms middle capsid|
|**VP7**|Glycoprotein, outer capsid layer; defines **G serotype**|
|**VP2**|Core protein, surrounds RNA|
|**VP1**|RNA-dependent RNA polymerase|
|**VP3**|Capping enzyme (guanylyltransferase/methyltransferase)|
|**NSP4**|Enterotoxin-like activity, key in pathogenesis|
|**NSP1–NSP5**|Non-structural proteins for replication and host response modulation|

---

### **3. Pathophysiology**

#### **A. Entry and Replication**

- **VP8*** portion of VP4 binds to sialic acid and integrins on intestinal epithelial cells
    
- Virus enters via **endocytosis**
    
- **Outer layer is shed** in endosome → activation of transcription machinery
    
- **VP1 and VP3** begin viral mRNA synthesis within the **core particle**
    
- Viral proteins and genomes are assembled into new particles in cytoplasmic inclusions (**viroplasms**)
    

#### **B. Pathogenic Mechanisms**

1. **Infection of mature enterocytes** → cell lysis → **villous atrophy** → reduced absorptive capacity
    
2. **NSP4**:
    
    - Functions as an **enterotoxin**
        
    - Disrupts calcium homeostasis → chloride secretion → **secretory diarrhea**
        
    - Stimulates **ENS (enteric nervous system)** → increased gut motility
        
3. **Immune response**:
    
    - Both **innate (interferon, NK cells)** and **adaptive (IgA, T cells)** responses involved
        
    - Immunity is incomplete; reinfections occur but are generally milder
        

---

### **4. Clinical Manifestations**

#### **Incubation period**: ~1–3 days

#### **Typical Presentation (in children 6 months to 2 years)**:

- **Abrupt onset** of:
    
    - **Watery diarrhea** (4–8 stools/day, can exceed 20)
        
    - **Vomiting** (often precedes diarrhea)
        
    - **Fever**
        
    - **Abdominal pain/irritability**
        
- Duration: **3–8 days**
    
- Can cause **severe dehydration**, especially in infants
    

#### In adults:

- Often asymptomatic or mild gastroenteritis (unless immunocompromised)
    

---

### **5. Complications**

|Complication|Notes|
|---|---|
|**Severe dehydration**|Leading cause of morbidity/mortality|
|**Electrolyte imbalance**|Especially hyponatremia, metabolic acidosis|
|**Seizures**|Febrile or electrolyte-related|
|**Intussusception**|Very rare; more often associated with early versions of the vaccine|
|**Nosocomial spread**|Highly contagious in pediatric wards and daycare settings|

---

### **6. Diagnosis and Differential Diagnosis**

#### **A. Laboratory Diagnosis**

- **Enzyme Immunoassay (EIA)**: Detects **VP6 antigen** in stool (rapid and sensitive)
    
- **RT-PCR**: For genotyping (G and P types)
    
- **Electron microscopy**: Visualizes virus in stool (rarely used now)
    
- **Stool culture**: Not useful (rotavirus is not culturable in routine labs)
    

#### **B. Differential Diagnosis**

|Pathogen|Key Differences|
|---|---|
|**Norovirus**|Affects all ages, sudden onset, short duration (1–3 days), more common in adults|
|**Adenovirus (types 40/41)**|Longer-lasting diarrhea, less vomiting|
|**Astrovirus**|Usually milder illness, primarily affects young children|
|**Bacterial enteritis (Salmonella, Shigella, EHEC)**|Blood in stool, higher fevers, leukocytosis|
|**Parasites (Giardia)**|Prolonged diarrhea, not usually febrile, foul-smelling stool|

---

### **7. Treatment and Management**

#### **A. Supportive Care (Mainstay)**

- **Oral rehydration therapy (ORT)**:
    
    - WHO-recommended glucose-electrolyte solutions
        
- **IV fluids**: For severe dehydration or inability to tolerate ORT
    
- **Antiemetics**: Ondansetron in children (under guidance)
    
- **Zinc supplementation**: Reduces duration and severity of diarrhea (especially in malnourished children)
    

#### **B. Avoid**

- **Antibiotics**: Not effective
    
- **Antidiarrheals**: Contraindicated in young children (e.g., loperamide)
    

---

### **8. Prevention**

#### **A. Vaccination (Highly Effective)**

- **Rotarix (monovalent, live attenuated)** – 2 doses (6 and 10 weeks)
    
- **RotaTeq (pentavalent, live attenuated reassortant)** – 3 doses (6, 10, and 14 weeks)
    
- Reduces **hospitalizations and deaths** dramatically
    
- **Contraindicated** in children with a history of **intussusception**, **severe combined immunodeficiency (SCID)**
    

#### **B. Hygiene and Sanitation**

- Handwashing, surface disinfection
    
- Isolation of symptomatic individuals in hospital settings
