# Diagnosis
## Dx
[[Trypanosoma brucei#Dx]]
## CM
### Trypanosomal Chancre
- Painful chancre at the site of tsetse fly bite.
- Appears 1 to 2 weeks after bite, resolves over several weeks.
- Chancre may ulcerate, reach several centimeters, and regional lymphadenopathy may develop.
- Rarely seen in clinical practice; most patients develop systemic trypanosomiasis without localized symptoms.
### Stage 1: Hemolymphatic Disease
- Marked by intermittent fever, which may appear weeks or months after infection.
- Fever lasts for several days, with afebrile periods in between.
- **Lymphadenopathy**:
  - Common in gambiense trypanosomiasis.
  - Nodes are discrete, movable, rubbery, nontender, and may become indurated over time.
  - Supraclavicular and cervical nodes visibly discernible; Winterbottom sign (posterior cervical triangle enlargement).
  - Hepatosplenomegaly may be present.
- **Transient Edema**:
  - Common during hemolymphatic phase.
  - Occurs in face, hands, feet, and periarticular areas.
- **Rash**:
  - Irregular circinate rash, typically on trunk, shoulders, buttocks, and thighs.
  - Erythematous areas 5 to 10 cm in diameter with clear centers.
- **Other Symptoms**:
  - Malaise, headache, weakness, weight loss, arthralgias, and tachycardia.
### Stage 2: Meningoencephalitic Disease
- Insidious development of neurological symptoms and progressive alterations in CSF.
- Symptoms may develop months or years after infection.
- **Early CNS Symptoms**:
  - Irritability, personality changes, loss of concentration.
  - Progressive indifference, daytime somnolence, restlessness, and insomnia.
  - Severe headache, listless gaze, indistinct speech.
- **Extrapyramidal Signs**:
  - Choreiform movements, tremors, and fasciculations.
  - Ataxia, shuffling gait, hypertonia, tremors, slurred speech (Parkinson-like symptoms).
- **Final Phase**:
  - Progressive neurologic impairment, coma, and death.
  - Signs and symptoms of hypothyroidism and adrenal insufficiency, often not supported by lab data.
### Trypanosomiasis in Children
- Relatively uncommon due to less exposure to vectors.
- Clinical illness similar to adults but more acute.
- Distinction between hemolymphatic and CNS stages may be difficult.
- Diagnosis often missed in early stages, made after neurological impairment.
# Epidemiologi
[[Trypanosoma brucei#Epidemiologi]]
- Transmitted primarily by tsetse flies of the palpalis group: Glossina palpalis, Glossina tachinoides, and Glossina fuscipes.
- Vectors inhabit forests and wooded areas along rivers with favorable conditions of temperature, moisture, and darkness.
- Restricted to tropical rain forests of Central and West Africa.
- Infected humans are the only epidemiologically important reservoir, despite tsetse flies feeding on various mammals and T. b. gambiense found in wild animals.
- Risk of acquiring infection is determined by the frequency of contact with the vector.
- Risk increases during the dry season due to increased density of vectors and humans around water holes.
- Primarily affects rural populations; tourists rarely infected with T. b. gambiense.
- The illness caused by T. b. gambiense is less severe than that caused by T. b. rhodesiense, but both forms almost always lead to death if untreated.
- Many persons infected with T. b. gambiense are asymptomatic for long periods, maintaining contact with vectors.
- Asymptomatic carriers may contribute to the persistence of the infection in the reservoir between epidemics.
