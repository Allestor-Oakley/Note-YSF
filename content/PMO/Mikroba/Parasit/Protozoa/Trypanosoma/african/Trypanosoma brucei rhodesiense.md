## Dx

## CM
- **General Differences:**
	- East African trypanosomiasis (T. b. rhodesiense) tends to follow a more acute course.
	- Less effective adaptation of T. b. rhodesiense to humans.
	- **Read =>**[[Trypanosoma brucei gambiense#CM]]
- **Onset of Symptoms:**
	- Symptoms usually appear a few days after an infected tsetse fly bite; incubation can be several weeks.
	- Tourists often experience systemic signs (fever, malaise, headache) before or shortly after returning home.
- **Progression of Illness:**
	- Intermittent fever pattern develops as the illness progresses.
	- Rash is a nearly constant feature in the early weeks.
	- Lymph node swelling is not prominent; Winterbottom sign generally absent.
	- Persistent tachycardia unrelated to fevers is common.
	- Death may result from arrhythmias and congestive heart failure due to pancarditis before CNS disease develops.
- **Outcome:**
	- Untreated rhodesiense trypanosomiasis usually leads to death within weeks to months.
	- Often no clear distinction between hemolymphatic and CNS stages.
# Epidemiologi
[[Trypanosoma brucei#Epidemiologi]]
- Transmitted by tsetse flies of the morsitans group: Glossina morsitans, Glossina pallidipes, and Glossina swynnertoni.
- Vectors are widely distributed in savanna and woodland areas of Central and East Africa.
- Wild animals, principally antelope like bushbuck and hartebeest, are the reservoir.
- These animals are trypanotolerant and generally do not suffer significant morbidity unless weakened by other illnesses.
- Cattle are the only domestic animals that can serve as a reservoir, with infection usually leading to death if untreated.
- Other wild and domestic animals can be infected, but their importance as reservoirs is minimal due to low parasitemias or quick death.
- The presence of reservoirs in wild game makes opening lands for livestock difficult.
- Humans become infected incidentally, primarily from contact with tsetse flies that feed on wild animals.
- The illness is an occupational hazard for persons such as game wardens in areas with infected wild animals and vectors.
- Sporadic cases occur among non-African tourists visiting game parks in East Africa.
