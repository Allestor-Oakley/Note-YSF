# Pendahuluan
[[Trypanosoma#Pendahuluan]]
“African trypanosomes” or “Old World trypanosomes” are protozoan hemoflagellates of the genus _Trypanosoma,_ in the subgenus _Trypanozoon_. Two subspecies that are morphologically indistinguishable cause distinct disease patterns in humans: [[Trypanosoma brucei gambiense]], causing chronic African trypanosomiasis (“West African sleeping sickness”) and [[Trypanosoma brucei rhodesiense]], causing acute African trypanosomiasis (“East African sleeping sickness”). The third subspecies [[Trypanosoma brucei brucei]] is a parasite primarily of cattle and occasionally other animals, and under normal conditions does not infect humans.
# Morfologi
- **Agents of HAT:
  - Flagellated protozoan parasites: Trypanosoma brucei brucei, T. b. rhodesiense, T. b. gambiense.
  - Morphologically indistinguishable, collectively called the T. brucei complex.
  - [[Trypanosoma brucei brucei]]: Infects animals, not humans.
  - **[[Trypanosoma brucei rhodesiense]]**: Primarily infects wild game, can infect humans.
  - **[[Trypanosoma brucei gambiense]]
    - Long, slender organisms capable of dividing.
    - Short, stumpy forms thought to be nondividing, infective for insect vectors.
  - Developmental Cycle:
    - Stumpy trypomastigotes transform into slender procyclic trypomastigotes in the midgut of flies.
    - Procyclic forms multiply, migrate to salivary glands, become epimastigotes.
    - Epimastigotes transform into nondividing metacyclic trypomastigotes.
  - Transmission occurs during blood meal; metacyclic forms become bloodstream trypomastigotes and multiply.
- **Differences and Immune Evasion**:
  - African trypanosomes can multiply in mammalian bloodstream, exposed to humoral defenses.
  - Evasion of immune destruction through antigenic variation, changing the antigenic structure of glycoprotein coats.
  - **Molecular Mechanisms**:
    - Parasites synthesize surface coats with antigenic glycoproteins, called variant antigen types (VATs).
    - After injection, parasites express metacyclic VATs for ~5 days, then switch to bloodstream VATs.
    - Host mounts humoral responses against expressed VATs, parasite population survives by switching VATs.
# Transmisi
  - Mostly cyclic transmission involving tsetse flies.
  - No evidence of transmission by insects other than tsetse flies.
  - Occasional mechanical transmission, rare congenital transmission, and blood transfusion transmission.
  - Few laboratory accidents reported resulting in infection.
# Penyakit
Human African trypanosomiasis (HAT), also known as sleeping sickness, is caused by two protozoan parasite subspecies: [[Trypanosoma brucei gambiense]] (West African trypanosomiasis) and [[Trypanosoma brucei rhodesiense]] (East African trypanosomiasis).
# Patho
**Initial Infection:**
- Trypanosomal chancre appears a week after an infected tsetse fly bite.
- Intense mononuclear cell reaction, edema, and local tissue destruction at the site.
- Chancre resolves spontaneously over several weeks.
**Stage 1: Systemic Hemolymphatic Illness:**
- Infection evolves into a systemic illness over weeks and months.
- Parasites disseminate through lymphatic system and bloodstream.
- Parasites travel to regional lymph nodes, causing an inflammatory response and then move to the bloodstream.
- Increased vascular permeability facilitates parasite egress into interstitial spaces.
- Widespread lymphadenopathy, histiocytic proliferation, and fibrosis.
- Morular cells (Mott cells) present; they produce immunoglobulin M (IgM).
- Enlarged spleen, cellular proliferation, congestion, and focal necrosis.
- Endarteritis and perivascular infiltration in lymph nodes and spleen.
**Heart Involvement:**
- Heart involvement, especially with [[Trypanosoma brucei rhodesiense]].
- Pancarditis involving all layers of the heart, including the conduction system and autonomic innervation.
- Pathologic changes: intense mononuclear infiltration, myocytolysis, fibrosis.
- Normocytic anemia, often with brisk reticulocytosis.
- Immune-mediated hemolysis may contribute to anemia.
- Reduced platelet counts, disseminated intravascular coagulation.
- Moderate leukocytosis with polyclonal B-cell activation.
- High titers of immunoglobulins, mainly polyclonal IgM.
- Circulating antigen-antibody complexes, elevated erythrocyte sedimentation rates, hypocomplementemia.
**Stage 2: CNS Involvement:**
- Parasites invade CNS, causing meningoencephalitis or meningomyelitis.
- Parasites found mainly in frontal lobes, pons, and medulla.
- Edema and hemorrhages may be evident on gross examination.
- Parasites present in perivascular areas and nests without relation to blood vessels.
- Mononuclear cell infiltration in CNS: lymphocytes, plasmacytes, morular cells.
- Abnormal cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) findings: increased pressure, elevated protein concentration, predominance of mononuclear cells, presence of trypanosomes.
**Alternative Model:**
- Rat model of T. brucei infection highlights role of pia mater as an immune-privileged site.
- Prostaglandin D2 may influence sleeping sickness development.
- Brain parenchymal disease proceeds from chronic infection.
- Understanding of disease physiology might influence assumptions about drug pharmacokinetics.
# Diagnosis
## Dx
**Diagnosis:**
- Epidemiologic information and clinical findings often suggest the diagnosis.
- High index of suspicion should be maintained for persons who have been in endemic areas.
- Definitive diagnosis requires demonstration of the parasite.
**Chancre Examination:**
- Fluid should be expressed from the chancre and examined under light microscopy for trypanosomes.
- Specimen should be fixed and stained with Giemsa.
- Aspiration of soft lymph nodes can also demonstrate parasites, more effective in West African trypanosomiasis.
- Enlarged node should be punctured, kneaded, and examined both directly and after staining.
**Peripheral Blood Examination:**
- Wet preparations and Giemsa-stained thin and thick smears of blood can detect infection.
- More successful in the hemolymphatic stage and in patients with T. b. rhodesiense due to higher parasitemias.
- Serial specimens should be examined due to variability in parasitemias.
- Efforts to concentrate organisms if not seen in blood; microscopic examination of buffy coat or use of anion exchange columns.
**PCR and LAMP Assays:**
- Development of PCR-based assays for detection shows high sensitivity and specificity.
- Implementation in the field difficult due to lack of technical facilities.
- LAMP methodology applied for HAT diagnosis but requires further work for field application.
**CSF Examination:**
- Mandatory in all suspected cases.
- Increase in CSF cell count is the first abnormality.
- Increased opening pressure, elevated IgM, and total protein concentration develop later.
- Single or double centrifugation of CSF often reveals trypanosomes in patients with CNS involvement.
- Any CSF abnormality in patients with trypanosomes in other specimens indicates CNS involvement.
**Staging:**
- Accurate diagnostic staging crucial for treatment.
- WHO criteria for stage 2: presence of trypanosomes in CSF or WBC >5 cells/µL.
- Controversy on WBC count; some experts recommend >10 cells/µL as indicative of stage 2.
- Search for biomarkers for accurate staging continues.
**Bone Marrow Aspiration:**
- Used if parasites cannot be demonstrated by other methods.
- Careful examination of Giemsa-stained specimens can reveal trypanosomes.
- Bone marrow, blood, CSF, or lymph node aspirates can be inoculated into special culture medium.
**Agglutination and Rapid Diagnostic Tests:**
- CATT available commercially, cornerstone for screening populations at risk.
- Immune trypanolysis detects specific antibodies but performed only in reference centers.
- Commercial RDTs for T. b. gambiense have the advantage of no cold chain but not suitable for large-scale screening.
- HAT Sero-K-Set and SD Bioline HAT compared, both with acceptable sensitivity but insufficient specificity.
- RDTs require confirmation and cost concerns remain paramount.

# Epidemiologi
- HAT is limited to sub-Saharan Africa.
- Major epidemics occurred around the beginning of the 20th century and between 1920 and 1948, causing hundreds of thousands of deaths.
- Colonial powers invested heavily in HAT control between World War II and independence, almost eliminating the disease.
- After independence, control programs were neglected due to poverty and civil strife, leading to a resurgence peaking in the late 1990s.
- Angola, Uganda, South Sudan, and the Democratic Republic of Congo were particularly affected, with 80% of reported cases occurring in the Democratic Republic of Congo.
- WHO, governments of endemic countries, and NGOs have resulted in effective control in many areas.
- Annual reported cases decreased to 9878 in 2009 and to 2184 by 2016, similar to the low point of the early 1960s.
- Underreporting remains a persistent problem.
- WHO and involved organizations have made significant efforts to control the disease.
- In 2012, WHO convened a panel of experts to develop a vision for eliminating HAT.
- WHO aims to eliminate HAT as a public health problem by 2020, defined as fewer than 1000 reported cases per year.

# Pencegahan
**Public Health Problem:**
- Trypanosomiases are complex public health and epizootic problems in many developing countries in Africa.
- Control programs focus on vector eradication and drug treatment of infected people.
- These programs have been in operation in most endemic regions for decades.
**Progress and Vision:**
- Enormous progress has been made, leading to the development of a vision for the elimination of HAT as a public health problem by 2020.
**Risk Reduction:**
- Individuals can reduce their risk of infection by:
	- Avoiding areas known to harbor infected insects.
	- Using insect repellent.
	- Wearing clothing that reduces the biting of flies.
**Chemoprophylaxis and Vaccine:**
- Chemoprophylaxis is not recommended due to the high toxicity of drugs active against African trypanosomes.
- No vaccine is available to prevent the transmission of the parasites.

**Overview of HAT Treatment:**
- Complex and difficult to assess the efficacy of treatment.
- Suramin, pentamidine, and organic arsenicals have been mainstay treatments for over 50 years.
- Eflornithine added in 1990, effective in both hemolymphatic and CNS stages of West African trypanosomiasis.
- Therapy must be individualized based on CNS disease presence, side effects, and drug resistance.
**Drug Use and Recommendations:**
- Suramin and pentamidine do not penetrate the CNS adequately.
- Eflornithine is recommended for gambiense trypanosomiasis with CNS disease.
- Melarsoprol, highly toxic, is used for stage 2 rhodesiense trypanosomiasis.
- Drugs available from the Drug Service of the CDC in the United States.
**Pentamidine Treatment:**
- Stage 1 gambiense trypanosomiasis: pentamidine isethionate (Lomidine), 4 mg/kg (max 300 mg/day) IV or IM for 10 days.
- Side effects: pain, sterile abscesses, nausea, vomiting, hypotension, tachycardia, nephrotoxicity, hepatic transaminase elevation, neutropenia, rashes, hypoglycemia.
**Eflornithine Treatment:**
- Effective in hemolymphatic and CNS stages of gambiense trypanosomiasis.
- Dosage: adults 400 mg/kg/day IV in four doses for 14 days; children 500-600 mg/kg/day on the same schedule.
- Side effects: anemia, leukopenia, thrombocytopenia, seizures, hearing loss.
- Challenges: IV administration, large drug amounts, therapy duration.
**NECT (Nifurtimox-Eflornithine Combination Therapy):**
- Eflornithine 200 mg/kg every 12 hours IV for 7 days plus nifurtimox 15 mg/kg/day orally in three doses for 10 days.
- Recommended for stage 2 T. b. gambiense disease if available.
- Less complicated and costly compared to eflornithine monotherapy.
**Suramin Treatment:**
- First-choice for stage 1 rhodesiense trypanosomiasis.
- Treatment regimen: 5 mg/kg on day 1, 10 mg/kg on day 3, and 20 mg/kg on days 5, 11, 17, 23, and 30 (1 g/day max).
- Side effects: severe reactions (nausea, vomiting, seizures, shock), renal damage, proteinuria, fever, pruritus, photophobia, arthralgias, skin eruptions.
- Not for use in patients with renal insufficiency.
**Melarsoprol Treatment:**
- Used for rhodesiense trypanosomiasis with CNS involvement.
- Short-course regimen: 10 daily doses of 2.2 mg/kg IV with prednisolone 1 mg/kg.
- Side effects: reactive encephalopathy (fever, headache, tremor, seizures, coma, death), local reactions, abdominal pain, vomiting, nephrotoxicity, abnormal liver function, myocardial damage.
- Prednisolone reduces encephalopathy risk.
**Fexinidazole:**
- Oral drug shown to be noninferior to NECT in randomized phase 2/3 trial for stage 2 gambiense HAT.
- Potential definitive tool for West African HAT elimination if approved and available commercially.
