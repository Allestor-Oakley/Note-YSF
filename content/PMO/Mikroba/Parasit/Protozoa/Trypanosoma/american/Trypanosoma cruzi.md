# Pendahuluan
[[Trypanosoma#Pendahuluan]]
# Morfologi
- **Appearance in Blood**:
  - Trypanosoma appears as trypomastigotes in the blood.
  - They have elongated bodies with a longitudinal lateral undulating membrane.
  - A flagellum borders the free edge of the membrane and emerges at the anterior end as a whiplike extension.
- **Kinetoplast**:
  - Contains circular DNA inside the single mitochondrion.
  - Is a darkly staining body located adjacent to the basal body from which the flagellum arises.
# Transmisi
- **Transmission of T. cruzi**:
  - Causative agent of American trypanosomiasis.
  - Transmitted by blood-sucking triatomine insects, or kissing bugs.
- **Vectors**:
  - Found in large numbers in the wild.
  - Transmit the parasite among many mammalian species (natural reservoir).
  - Live in the nooks and crannies of substandard dwellings in endemic areas.
  - Become infected by sucking blood from humans or other mammals with circulating trypomastigotes.
- **Lifecycle in Insects**:
  - Ingested parasites multiply in the midgut as epimastigotes (distinct morphologic type).
  - Transform into infective metacyclic trypomastigotes in the hindgut.
  - Discharged with feces during subsequent blood meals.
- **Transmission to Vertebrate Hosts**:
  - Occurs through contamination of mucous membranes, conjunctivae, or breaks in the skin with bug feces containing infective forms.
  - Parasites enter host cell types and multiply in the cytoplasm after transforming into amastigotes.
  - Amastigotes fill the host cell, differentiate into trypomastigotes, and cause cell rupture.
  - Released parasites invade local tissues or spread hematogenously to distant sites, initiating further cycles of multiplication (largely in muscle cells), and maintaining a parasitemia infective for vectors.
- **Additional Transmission Routes**:
  - **Blood Transfusions**: 
    - Occurs in cities when infected but asymptomatic migrants from endemic rural areas donate blood.
    - Serologic screening has eliminated transmission by this route in most endemic countries.

# Patho
## Acute Chagas Disease
- Inflammatory lesion at site of entry is called a chagoma.
- Local histologic changes:
    - Intracellular parasitism of muscle and subcutaneous tissues.
    - Interstitial edema.
    - Lymphocytic infiltration.
    - Reactive hyperplasia of adjacent lymph nodes.
- Trypomastigotes detected by microscopic examination of fresh blood.
- Most heavily parasitized tissues are muscles, including the myocardium.
- Myocarditis may develop with patchy areas of infected cells and necrosis.
- Characteristic pseudocysts are aggregates of intracellular amastigotes.
- Lymphocytosis accompanies high parasitemias, with occasional mild elevation of transaminase levels.
- Parasites can sometimes be found in cerebrospinal fluid.
## Chronic Chagas Disease
- The heart is the organ most commonly affected.
- Gross examination reveals:
	- Marked bilateral ventricular enlargement.
	- Often involves the right side of the heart more than the left.
	- Thinning of the ventricular walls.
	- Apical aneurysms and mural thrombi.
- Histologic examination reveals:
    - Widespread lymphocytic infiltration.
    - Diffuse interstitial fibrosis.
    - Atrophy of myocardial cells.
- Parasites rarely seen in stained sections, but PCR assays show parasites in areas of focal inflammation.
- Pathologic changes in the conduction system include:
    - Dense fibrosis and chronic inflammatory lesions.
    - Right branch and left anterior branch of the bundle of His most frequently involved.
    - Similar lesions found in other parts of the conduction system.
## Digestive Tract in Chronic Chagas Disease (Megadisease)
- Gross examination reveals enormous dilatation and muscular hypertrophy of affected organs (esophagus or colon).
- Microscopic examination reveals:
	- Focal inflammatory lesions with lymphocytic infiltration.
    - Marked reduction in the number of neurons in the myenteric plexus.
    - Periganglionic and intraganglionic fibrosis.
    - Schwann cell proliferation and lymphocytosis.
- Clinical effects of parasympathetic denervation usually confined to esophagus or colon, but similar lesions observed in biliary tree, ureters, and other hollow viscera.
# Prognosis, Komplikasi
- **Acute Chagas Disease**:
    - Often mild and nonspecific symptoms, leading to many unrecognized cases.
    - Acute illness typically resolves over weeks to months.
    - After resolution, patients enter the indeterminate phase, characterized by asymptomatic parasitemia and antibody presence.
    - Prognosis improves with early treatment using benznidazole or nifurtimox.
- **Chronic Chagas Disease**:
    - Symptoms may appear years or decades after initial infection.
    - Chronic infection leads to complications such as cardiac and gastrointestinal issues.
    - Long-term monitoring for cardiac and gastrointestinal complications is crucial.
    - Prognosis varies based on the severity of organ involvement and response to treatment.
# Diagnosis
## CM
### Clinical Syndromes of Acute vs. Chronic Chagas Disease
  - Acute illness results from the first encounter with T. cruzi.
  - Chronic disease involves persistent infection and late sequelae in many individuals.
### Acute Chagas Disease
- Usually affects children but can occur at any age.
- Symptoms often mild and nonspecific, leading to unrecognized acute infections.
- First signs appear a week after parasite invasion:
    - Chagoma: indurated area of erythema and swelling with local lymph node involvement.
    - Romaña sign: painless edema of palpebrae and periocular tissues (when conjunctiva is the portal of entry).
- Followed by fever, malaise, anorexia, edema of face and lower extremities, generalized lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly.
- Overt CNS signs are rare but can include meningoencephalitis (poor prognosis).
- Severe myocarditis develops in some patients, leading to congestive heart failure.
- Nonspecific ECG changes; life-threatening arrhythmias are rare.
- Symptoms resolve gradually over weeks to months.
- Indeterminate phase: asymptomatic with subpatent parasitemia and T. cruzi antibodies.
### Chronic Chagas Disease
- Becomes apparent years or decades after initial infection.
- **Heart involvement**:
    - Rhythm disturbances, congestive heart failure, thromboembolism.
    - Symptoms: dizziness, syncope, seizures (due to arrhythmias).
    - Cardiomyopathy primarily affects the right ventricle.
    - Gradual progression of symptoms; validated risk-score tool available.
    - Clinical course complicated by emboli to brain or other areas.
- **Megaesophagus**:
    - Symptoms similar to idiopathic achalasia: dysphagia, odynophagia, chest pain, cough, regurgitation.
    - Hypersalivation and salivary gland hypertrophy.
    - Aspiration, repeated pneumonitis, weight loss, cachexia.
    - Increased incidence of esophageal cancer.
- **Megacolon**:
    - Chronic constipation and abdominal pain.
    - Advanced disease: several weeks between bowel movements.
    - Acute obstruction, volvulus, perforation, septicemia, death.
### Immunocompromised
- **Reactivation of T. cruzi in Immunosuppressed Patients**:
  - Reactivation can occur with greater severity than acute Chagas disease in immunocompetent patients.
  - Incidence of reactivation in immunosuppressed T. cruzi-infected patients is unknown.
  - Often develop skin lesions and cerebral mass lesions, not seen in immunocompetent persons.
  - Several reports of reactivation after renal transplantation; CNS involvement in two cases.
  - T. cruzi infection is not a contraindication for kidney transplantation.
  - Infected patients should be monitored for acute Chagas disease signs and symptoms postoperatively.
- **HIV and T. cruzi Coinfection**:
  - HIV immunosuppression can lead to recrudescence of chronic T. cruzi infection.
  - Dozens of such patients described; many developed T. cruzi brain abscesses.
  - Calculations suggest low incidence of brain abscesses in coinfected persons in endemic countries.
  - Incidence of T. cruzi brain abscesses in HIV-infected patients likely decreased with highly active antiretroviral therapy.
## Dx
- **Diagnosis of Acute Chagas Disease**:
  - Requires history of exposure to T. cruzi.
  - Exposure scenarios: residence in endemic areas, recent transfusion in endemic areas, birth to T. cruzi-infected mother, foodborne outbreak risk, laboratory accident.
- **Diagnosis in Immunocompetent Patients**:
  - Made by detecting motile parasites in blood.
  - Wet preparations of anticoagulated blood or buffy coat, Giemsa-stained smears.
  - Examination of blood preparations is essential.
- **Diagnosis in Immunocompromised Patients**:
  - Other specimens (lymph node, bone marrow aspirates, pericardial fluid, cerebrospinal fluid) examined microscopically.
  - Hemoculture attempted when parasites are not detected; sensitivity ~50%, takes weeks.
- **PCR Assays**:
  - Higher sensitivity and shorter completion time than hemoculture.
  - Useful for detecting T. cruzi when microscopy fails.
  - No commercial PCR test available, but detailed guidance for optimization exists.
- **Diagnosis of Congenital Chagas Disease**:
  - Parasitologic (microscopic examination of cord blood or PCR assay) right after birth.
  - Serologic testing for specific IgG at least 9 months later if initial tests are negative.
- **Diagnosis of Chronic T. cruzi Infection**:
  - Detected by IgG antibodies binding specifically to parasite antigens.
  - Detection of the organism is not of primary importance.
- **Serologic Assays**:
  - Over 30 commercially available assays (ELISA, indirect hemagglutination, chemiluminescent, and indirect immunofluorescence).
  - Sensitivity and specificity generally high, false positives possible (leishmaniasis, malaria, syphilis).
  - WHO recommends testing samples in two assays before diagnostic decisions.
- **FDA-Cleared Serologic Assays**:
  - Hemagen Chagas Kit, Chagatest ELISA recombinante, Chagas Detect Plus Rapid Test.
  - Abbott Architect Chagas Assay, Ortho T. cruzi ELISA Test System, Abbott Prism Chagas Assay used for donor testing.
# Epidemiologi
- Chagas disease is endemic in Mexico, as well as all of Central and South America.
- The infection is not endemic in any of the Caribbean islands.
- About 8 million persons are chronically infected with T. cruzi, roughly 56,000 new infections occur each year, and about 12,000 persons die of the illness annually. An estimated 238,000 immigrants with Chagas disease currently live in the United States.
- During recent decades, large numbers of people in the endemic countries have been migrating from rural areas to cities, and at the same time many millions of people have emigrated from endemic countries to industrialized regions, particularly the United States and the European Union, thus urbanizing and globalizing the disease.
# Pencegahan
### Screening and Identification
- **Screening Recommendations**:
  - All immigrants from endemic regions.
  - Children of immigrant mothers with geographic risk for Chagas disease.
  - Identification of infected persons for monitoring and appropriate treatment.
  - Justification for screening due to possible congenital transmission.
- **Evidence for Treating Women of Childbearing Age**:
  - Reduces the probability of congenital transmission to babies conceived after treatment.
  - Prepregnancy treatment has a marked suppressive effect on congenital transmission likelihood.
  - Current consensus: treat all infected women of childbearing age and persons ≤18 years old.
  - Precautions: ensure women are not pregnant when treatment is initiated and do not become pregnant during treatment due to potential fetal toxicity of benznidazole and nifurtimox.
### Transfusion-Associated Transmission
- **Historical Cases**:
  - FDA-approved screening test available since December 2006.
  - Serologic screening implemented to eliminate T. cruzi transmission risk via transfusion in the US.
### Organ Transplantation
- **Recommendations**:
  - Transplantation of livers and kidneys from T. cruzi-infected donors is reasonable with informed consent.
  - Careful postoperative and long-term monitoring for infection in recipients.
  - Hearts from T. cruzi-infected donors should not be transplanted.
### Risk Group Classification and Safety Measures
- **Laboratory Work**:
  - T. cruzi is a Risk Group 2 organism.
  - Personal protective measures consistent with classification required for laboratorians.
- **Travel Precautions**:
  - Avoid sleeping in dilapidated dwellings in endemic areas.
  - Use insect repellent and bed nets to reduce vector exposure.
  - No vaccine available for T. cruzi transmission prevention.
  - No special precautions needed for outdoor activities in the US beyond usual insect exposure reduction measures.
# Manajemen
- **Heart Transplantation for Chagas Disease**:
  - Option for patients with end-stage Chagas cardiac disease.
  - Several hundred patients, mostly in Brazil, have undergone the procedure.
  - T. cruzi cannot be reliably eliminated by antiparasitic treatment before transplantation, leading to frequent reactivation.
  - Parasitological approaches used to detect acute T. cruzi infections often not sensitive for reactivation.
  - Higher-than-expected incidence of malignancies observed in Brazilian patients.
  - Patients with Chagas heart transplants sometimes develop cutaneous lesions with large numbers of parasites.
  - Long-term survival of Chagas disease patients with heart transplants is greater than that of persons receiving transplants for other reasons.
### Drugs for Treating T. cruzi Infection
- **Benznidazole**:
  - Severe side effects: peripheral neuropathy, rash, granulocytopenia.
  - Drug of choice by most specialists.
  - Reduces duration, severity, and mortality in acute and congenital Chagas disease.
  - Parasitologic cure in ~70% of acute patients.
  - Should not be given to pregnant women.
  - Oral dosage: 5 mg/kg/day for 60 days.
  - High discontinuation rates due to adverse reactions.
  - Progressive regimen proposed to reduce rash-related discontinuation.
  - 
- **Nifurtimox**:
  - Adverse side effects: gastrointestinal complaints, neurologic symptoms.
  - Similar efficacy to benznidazole.
  - Should not be given to pregnant women.
  - Oral dosage:
    - Adults: 8-10 mg/kg/day.
    - Adolescents: 12.5-15 mg/kg/day.
    - Children (1-10 years): 15-20 mg/kg/day.
  - Available from the CDC's Parasitic Disease Drug Service.
  - Treatment duration: 90-120 days.
### Groups Recommended for Treatment
- All persons with acute T. cruzi infection, including infants with congenital Chagas disease.
- All chronically infected persons up to 18 years old.
- FDA approval of benznidazole for chronically infected 2- to 12-year-old children based on antibody level reduction data.
### Debate on Treating Adults with Chronic T. cruzi Infection
- Parasitologic cure in <10% of long-standing infections.
- No convincing evidence of improved long-term outcomes.
- CDC panel (2007) suggested treatment for adults with long-standing indeterminate phase infections.
- BENEFIT Multicenter Trial found no significant difference in outcomes between benznidazole and placebo recipients.
### Other Drugs
- Fluconazole, ketoconazole, itraconazole, posaconazole, and allopurinol:
  - Studied extensively but not recommended for treating acute or chronic Chagas disease.
### Management of Acute Chagas Disease
- Generally self-limited symptoms; therapy with benznidazole or nifurtimox.
- Supportive management for severe cases with myocarditis or meningoencephalitis.
### Management of Chronic Chagas Disease
- Supportive treatment for chronic chagasic heart disease.
- Electrocardiograms every 6 months.
- Pacemakers for arrhythmia management.
- Treatment of congestive heart failure similar to other cardiomyopathies.
- Controversy over the use of implantable cardioverter-defibrillators.
### Management of Megaesophagus
- First approach: balloon dilation of the lower esophageal sphincter.
- Surgical options for non-responsive cases:
  - Wide esophagocardiomyectomy with valvuloplasty.
  - Esophageal resection with esophagogastroplasty.
- Laparoscopic myotomy used in industrialized countries.
### Management of Megacolon
- High-fiber diet, laxatives, enemas for early colonic dysfunction.
- Manual disimpaction for fecal impaction.
- Surgical treatment for toxic megacolon.
- **Volvulus management**:
  - Endoscopic emptying for uncomplicated cases.
  - Surgical decompression for complicated cases.
  - Surgical treatment eventually required to prevent recurrence.
