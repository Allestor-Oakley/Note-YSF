# Definisi singkat
Entamoeba histolytica adalah amoebozoa parasit anaerobik, bagian dari genus Entamoeba. Terutama menginfeksi manusia dan primata lain yang menyebabkan amoebiasis.
# Taksonomi
Domain:	Eukaryota
Phylum:	Amoebozoa
Family:	Entamoebidae
Genus:	Entamoeba
Species:	E. histolytica
# Morfologi
- **Cysts**:
    - Location: Lumen of the colon, present in mushy or formed feces.
    - Size: 10 to 20 μm.
    - Contents: Glycogen vacuole and chromatoid bodies (ribonucleoprotein masses with rounded ends).
    - Nuclear Division: Results in a quadrinucleated cyst; chromatoid bodies and glycogen vacuoles disappear.
    - Diagnosis: Based on cyst characteristics, as trophozoites appear only in diarrheic feces and survive for a few hours.
- **Trophozoites**:
    - Presence: Only in tissues.
    - Cytoplasm: Two zones – a hyaline outer margin and a granular inner region.
    - Pathognomonic Feature: Contains red blood cells, but usually no bacteria.
    - Nuclear Membrane: Lined with fine, regular chromatin granules and a small central body (endosome or karyosome).

# PP
- **Incidence**: Approximately 50 million cases of invasive disease occur each year, with up to 100,000 deaths (Marie and Petri, 2014).
- **Pathogenesis**: 
  - Disease results from trophozoites of *E. histolytica* invading the intestinal epithelium.
  - Formation of discrete ulcers with pinhead-sized centers and raised edges.
  - Trophozoites multiply, accumulate above the muscularis mucosae, and spread laterally.
  - Causes =="flask-shaped" ulcers==: small entry points with expanded necrotic areas in the submucosa.
  - Spread can lead to large areas of undermined mucosal surface and secondary bacterial invasion.
  - **Ingestion**: Cysts pass through the stomach; gastric acid releases pathogenic trophozoites in the duodenum.
  - **Local Necrosis**: Trophozoites divide and cause extensive local necrosis in the large intestine.
  - **Cytotoxin Production**: Tissue destruction attributed to the production of cytotoxins.
  - **Attachment**: E. histolytica trophozoites attach to host cells via a galactose-inhibitable adherence protein, necessary for cytolysis and tissue necrosis.
  - **Cell Lysis**: Trophozoites lyse colonic epithelial cells, neutrophils, lymphocytes, and monocytes, causing an irreversible increase in intracellular calcium levels and toxic neutrophil constituent release, contributing to tissue destruction.
- **Symptoms**: 
  - Vary depending on the site and intensity of lesions.
  - Extreme abdominal tenderness, dysentery, dehydration, and incapacitation in severe cases.
  - Gradual onset in less acute disease with episodes of diarrhea, abdominal cramps, nausea, vomiting, and urge to defecate.
  - Chronic cases may involve weeks of cramps, discomfort, loss of appetite, weight loss, and malaise.
  - Symptoms can develop within 4 days of exposure, up to a year later, or never.
  - **Intestinal Ulcers**: Flask-shaped ulcerations with inflammation, hemorrhage, and secondary bacterial infection.
  - **Deeper Invasion**: May invade deeper mucosa and extend into the peritoneal cavity, potentially involving liver, lungs, brain, and heart.
  - **Low Oxygen Environments**: Trophozoites are found in low oxygen pressure environments as they are killed by ambient oxygen concentrations.
- **Complications**: 
  - Extraintestinal infection is metastatic, commonly amebic hepatitis or liver abscess.
  - Amebic liver abscess is progressive, nonsuppurative, and destructive without compression.
  - Abscess content is necrotic and bacteriologically sterile, with active amebae in the walls.
  - Hepatic abscess observed by ultrasonography, CT, MRI, or radioisotope scanning.
  - Serologic tests for amebic liver abscess are usually strongly positive.
- **Risk Factors**:
  - Number of ingested amebae, pathogenic capacity of parasite strain, host factors like gut motility and immune competence, and presence of suitable enteric bacteria.
- **Identification**:
  - Critical to identify the *Entamoeba* species.
  - Trophozoites with red blood cells in the cytoplasm in liquid or semi-formed stools are pathognomonic.
  - **Markers**: Identified using lectin binding, zymodeme analysis, genome DNA analysis, and specific monoclonal antibodies staining.
  - **Species Distinction**: E. histolytica identified as four species—pathogenic E. histolytica and nonpathogenic E. dispar, E. moshkovskii, and E. bangladeshi. These species are morphologically indistinguishable but have stable zymodeme profiles and biochemical, molecular, and immunologic differences
# Epidemiologi
### Distribution and Prevalence

- **Worldwide Distribution**: Found globally, including cold regions like Alaska, Canada, and Eastern Europe.
- **Higher Incidence**: Most common in tropical and subtropical areas with poor sanitation and contaminated water.
- **Prevalence**: 10%-15% in endemic areas, with some regions seeing up to 50% infected. In the United States, prevalence is 1%-2%.
- **Asymptomatic Carriers**: Many infected individuals are asymptomatic carriers, serving as reservoirs for transmission.
### Transmission
- **Infectious Forms**: Patients pass noninfectious trophozoites and infectious cysts in stools.
- **Environmental Survival**: Trophozoites do not survive external environments or stomach passage if ingested.
- **Main Source of Contamination**: Asymptomatic carriers passing cysts.
- **High-Risk Locations**: Hospitals for the mentally ill, military and refugee camps, prisons, crowded day-care centers.
- **Mechanical Vectors**: Flies and cockroaches can transmit cysts.
- **Water Contamination**: Sewage containing cysts can contaminate water systems, wells, springs, and agricultural areas where human waste is used as fertilizer.
- **Sexual Transmission**: Cysts transmitted via oral-anal sexual practices; prevalent in homosexual populations.
- **Direct Trophozoite Transmission**: In sexual encounters, can produce cutaneous amebiasis.
# SS
### Outcome of Infection
- **Carrier State**:
  - Occurs if the strain has low virulence, low inoculum, or the patient's immune system is intact.
  - Organisms may reproduce, and cysts may be passed in stools without clinical symptoms.
  - Most asymptomatic infections involve noninvasive *E. dispar* or *E. moshkovskii*.
- **Asymptomatic Infections**:
  - Characterized by specific isoenzyme profiles (zymodemes), DNA-based assays, susceptibility to complement-mediated lysis, and non-agglutination with concanavalin A.
  - Detection of carriers is crucial for epidemiologic purposes, especially in areas with low endemicity.
### Intestinal Amebiasis
- **Clinical Symptoms**:
  - Abdominal pain, cramping, colitis with diarrhea.
  - Severe disease characterized by numerous bloody stools per day.
### Extraintestinal Amebiasis
- **Systemic Signs**:
  - Fever, leukocytosis, rigors.
- **Liver Involvement**:
  - Trophozoites in the blood are filtered by the liver, leading to abscess formation.
  - The right lobe is most commonly involved.
  - Symptoms include pain over the liver, hepatomegaly, and elevation of the diaphragm.
# Diagnosis
### Identification and Diagnosis of E. histolytica
- **Diagnostic Methods**:
  - Identification of trophozoites and cysts in stools and tissues.
  - Distinguishing between E. histolytica and commensal amebae or polymorphonuclear leukocytes.
- **Stool Examination**:
  - Inherently insensitive due to non-homogeneous distribution of protozoa.
  - Parasites concentrated in intestinal ulcers and abscess margins, not in stool.
  - Multiple stool specimens should be collected for accurate diagnosis.
- **Extraintestinal Amebiasis**:
  - Diagnosed using liver and organ scanning procedures.
  - Specific serologic tests and microscopic examination of abscess material confirm the diagnosis.
  - Almost all patients with hepatic amebiasis and most (>80%) with intestinal disease have positive serologic findings at clinical presentation.
  - Positive serologic results may be less useful in endemic areas.
- **Newer Diagnostic Approaches**:
  - Immunologic tests for fecal antigen detection.
  - Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) and DNA-probe assays for detecting pathogenic strains of E. histolytica (versus nonpathogenic E. dispar and E. moshkovskii).
  - These newer diagnostic methods are promising and commercially available.

## StatPearls
- Metronidazole dosing for adults is 500 mg orally every 6 to 8 hours for 7 to 14 days.
- Tinidazole adult dosing is 2 g orally each day for 3 days.
## Murray
### Treatment and Prevention of Amebiasis
- **Acute Fulminating Amebiasis**:
  - Treated with metronidazole followed by iodoquinol, diloxanide furoate, or paromomycin.
- **Asymptomatic Carriage**:
  - Can be eradicated with iodoquinol, diloxanide furoate, or paromomycin.
- **Infection Transmission**:
  - Results from ingestion of contaminated food or water, or specific sexual practices.
  - Preventing infection requires adequate sanitation measures and education about transmission routes.
  - Chlorination and filtration of water supplies may help but are often not feasible in many developing countries.
- **Travel Precautions**:
  - Travelers to developing countries should be warned about the risks associated with consuming water (including ice cubes), unpeeled fruits, and raw vegetables.
  - Water should be boiled and fruits and vegetables thoroughly cleaned before consumption.
