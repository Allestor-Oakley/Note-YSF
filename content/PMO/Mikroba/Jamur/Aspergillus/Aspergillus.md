# Pendahuluan
The genus **Aspergillus** was first identified in 1729 by Micheli. **Aspergillus flavus** was named by Link in 1809. Thom and Church classified the genus in 1926 into 11 groups, now called "sections." Molecular techniques have expanded Aspergillus species to over 250, organized into 8 subgenera and multiple sections. Most Aspergillus species reproduce asexually, but some have teleomorphs (sexual forms). Despite correct taxonomic nomenclature, the generic name **Aspergillus** is often retained for simplicity. Molecular studies have reclassified Aspergillus, and molecular sequencing can lead to familiar species being reported as unfamiliar teleomorphs, leading to the practice of assigning one name to one fungus. **Aspergillus** is an anamorphic member of the family **Trichocomaceae**. Teleomorphs are classified in eight genera within the order **Eurotiales** in the phylum **Ascomycota**. Identifying common pathogenic species is straightforward, but species-level identification of less common members can be challenging.

# Transmisi
- **Inhalation**: The usual route of infection for invasive aspergillosis (IA) is through inhalation of Aspergillus conidia into the lungs.
- **Local Tissue Invasion**: Less commonly, invasive infection may occur through local tissue invasion.
- **Surgical Wounds**: Infections can follow local tissue invasion through surgical wounds.
- **Contaminated IV Catheters/Armboards**: Infections can also occur via contaminated intravenous (IV) catheters or armboards.
- **Cutaneous Infection**: Local tissue invasion can lead to cutaneous (skin) infection.
# Penyakit
Invasive aspergillosis (IA) remains a major cause of morbidity and mortality in the immunosuppressed population. These syndromes range from 
- colonization, such as
	- a fungus ball due to aspergillosis (also known as aspergilloma)
	- allergic responses to Aspergillus, including allergic bronchopulmonary aspergillosis (ABPA)
	- semi-invasive or invasive infections, from chronic necrotizing pneumonia to invasive pulmonary aspergillosis and other invasive syndromes.
- **Immunosuppressed Population**: The rise in Aspergillus infections coincides with the growing immunosuppressed population.
- **Poor Outcomes**: Patients with established invasive aspergillosis (IA) often have poor outcomes.
- **Therapy**: Successful therapy depends on early diagnosis and reversal of underlying immune defects.
- **Outcomes**: Outcomes are often poor even with prompt therapy, especially in patients with disseminated or central nervous system (CNS) disease and those who remain profoundly immunosuppressed.
# Patho
### Inhalation and Infection Process
- **Pulmonary Exposure**: Inhaled small resting conidia enlarge and germinate, transforming into hyphae with vascular invasion and disseminated infection.
- **Incubation Period**: Variable from 2 days to months, varying by species and strain.
- **Growth Rate**: At 37°C, may determine disease progression and pathogenicity.
- **Hydrocortisone**: Significantly increases growth rates, enhancing corticosteroids as a risk factor.
- **Hallmark Feature of IA**: Hyphal growth and tissue invasion lead to vascular invasion and pulmonary infarction due to angioinvasive nature.
### Host Defense and Pathogenicity
- **Infection in Normal Hosts**: IA is uncommon in immunocompetent hosts due to effective pulmonary defense mechanisms.
- **First Line of Defense**: Ciliary clearance from the airways; limited access to alveoli due to conidial size.
- **Pulmonary Macrophages**: Ingest and kill Aspergillus conidia in alveoli.
- **Leukocytes**: Extracellularly kill swollen conidia and hyphae after germination.
- **Enhancing Defense**: Opsonization of conidia with complement or molecules like mannose-binding protein and surfactant proteins.
- **Pathogenicity Factors**: Hydrophobic component inhibiting fungal recognition; melanin inhibiting phagocytosis; complement inhibitor increasing pathogenicity.
### Toxins and Virulence Factors
- **Toxin Production**: Includes aflatoxins, ochratoxin A, fumagillin, and gliotoxin.
- **Role of Toxins**: Gliotoxin may reduce macrophage and neutrophil function, though major virulence factors are not established.
- **Virulence Factors**: Production of proteases, phospholipases, and metabolites, still capable of causing infection even when deficient in these genes.
- **Diagnostic Utility**: Metabolites under investigation for diagnosis.
### Immune Response and Genetics
- **T-helper (Th) Cytokines**: Vital roles in innate and adaptive defense; Th1 response associated with favorable outcomes.
- **Pathogen Recognition Receptors**: TLRs and dectin-1 mediate defense; recognition leads to proinflammatory cytokine production.
- **Polymorphisms**: Influence susceptibility to IA; TLR4 haplotypes and plasminogen gene polymorphisms linked to increased risk in HSCT patients.
- **Genomic Studies**: Identified pathways impacting transplant donor screening and recipient risk stratification.
### Allergic Reactions
- **ABPA and Allergic Fungal Sinusitis**: Related to aberrant inflammatory host responses.
- **Th2 Response**: Allergic inflammatory response in asthmatic and cystic fibrosis patients; allergens processed by HLA-DR2 or HLA-DR5.
- **Inflammatory Response**: Leads to mucin production, eosinophil extravasation, intermittent bronchial obstruction, and bronchiectasis over time.
### Aspergilloma
- **Fungus Ball**: Mass of hyphae in a preexisting cavity, causing brisk IgG antibody response.
- **Chronic and Noninvasive Aspergillosis**: Linked to subtle immune defects, including mannose-binding protein and TLR polymorphisms.
# Prognosis, Komplikasi
### Prognosis of Allergic Bronchopulmonary Pulmonary Aspergillosis (ABPA)
- **Good Prognosis**: In patients with mild alterations in function.
- **Steroid Use**: Many patients may require steroids for a prolonged time if diagnosis is delayed.
- **Delays in Diagnosis**: Can lead to steroid resistance and the development of lung fibrosis.
### Prognosis of Invasive Aspergillosis
- **Poor Prognosis**: For patients with invasive aspergillosis.
- **Mortality Rate**: Approximately 20% six weeks from diagnosis despite appropriate antifungal therapy.
- **Central Nervous System Spread**: Mortality is close to 100% once the infection has spread to the central nervous system.

## CM
### ABPA
#### Characteristics and Diagnosis
- **Long-term Allergic Response**: ABPA is characterized by mucoid impaction of the bronchi, eosinophilic pneumonia, and transient pulmonary infiltrates due to atelectasis.
- **Central Bronchiectasis**: Occurs in some patients after several years of disease.
- **Diagnostic Criteria**:
  1. Asthma
  2. Central bronchiectasis on chest CT
  3. Immediate skin test reactivity to Aspergillus spp. (or A. fumigatus)
  4. Total serum immunoglobulin E (IgE) concentration > 417 IU/mL (1000 ng/mL)
  5. Elevated serum IgE and/or IgG antibody to A. fumigatus
  6. Fleeting infiltrates on chest radiography
  7. Serum precipitating antibodies to A. fumigatus
  8. Peripheral blood eosinophilia
- **Supporting Clinical Features**: Positive sputum cultures for Aspergillus, smears with hyphae consistent with Aspergillus, brown mucus plugs with degenerated eosinophils (Charcot-Leyden crystals) in sputa, and chest radiographic findings suggesting bronchial inflammation.
#### Diagnosis in Cystic Fibrosis
- **Difficulty**: Diagnosis of ABPA in cystic fibrosis is challenging due to overlapping diagnostic criteria with common manifestations of cystic fibrosis.
- **Eosinophilia**: Not a useful diagnostic tool because it may be elevated from other causes or suppressed from corticosteroid therapy.
#### Disease Progression and Management
- **Progression**: ABPA progresses through remissions and exacerbations, potentially leading to pulmonary fibrosis and chronic pulmonary aspergillosis.
- **Management Goals**: Reducing acute asthmatic symptoms and avoiding end-stage fibrosis.
- **Corticosteroid Therapy**: Commonly used for treating exacerbations; few randomized trials conducted. Guidelines suggest worsening diagnostic or clinical parameters may warrant a trial.
- **Antifungal Therapy**: Itraconazole at 200 mg/day for 16 weeks significantly reduced daily corticosteroid use, levels of IgE, and improved exercise tolerance and pulmonary function in a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial.
- **Severe Asthma and Fungal Sensitization**: Itraconazole resulted in significant clinical improvement in approximately 60%.
- **Other Antifungals**: Voriconazole, posaconazole, and isavuconazole have not been extensively evaluated but showed approximately 70% responses in ABPA or asthma patients who previously failed itraconazole.
### Aspergilloma
#### Pulmonary Fungus Ball (Aspergilloma)
- **Definition**: Solid mass of hyphae growing in a preexisting pulmonary cavity.
- **Development**: Typically develops in preexisting cavities in the pulmonary apex of patients with chronic lung disease (e.g., bullous emphysema, sarcoidosis, tuberculosis, histoplasmosis, congenital cyst, bacterial lung abscess, or pulmonary bleb from Pneumocystis pneumonia in AIDS patients).
- **Radiographic Appearance**: Appears as a solid round mass in a cavity on chest radiograph.
- **Diagnosis**: Further evidence from detection of Aspergillus in sputum cultures or high titers of Aspergillus antibodies, usually making a biopsy unnecessary except for diagnosing underlying lung disease.
#### Symptoms
- **Asymptomatic**: Many patients remain asymptomatic.
- **Hemoptysis**: Occurs in a significant number of patients and can be fatal.
#### Sinus Fungus Ball
- **Association**: Aspergillus can cause fungus balls of the sinuses without tissue invasion.
- **Common Site**: The maxillary sinus is the most common site.
- **Clinical Presentation**: Similar to chronic sinusitis.
- **Diagnosis**: Confirmed by CT of the sinus and cultures of Aspergillus (usually [[Aspergillus flavus]] or [[Aspergillus fumigatus]]).
- **Management**: Surgical removal and a generous maxillary antrostomy for sinus drainage, ensuring invasive disease has not occurred.
#### Chronic Pulmonary Aspergillosis Spectrum
- **Chronic Cavitary Pulmonary Aspergillosis**: Characterized by multiple cavities and fungal balls.
- **Chronic Fibrosing or Necrotizing Aspergillosis**: Slowly progressive infection, usually in a single thin-walled cavity with tissue invasion by hyphae.
- **Differentiation**: Based on patient symptoms, radiographic evidence of inflammation, and radiographic stability.
- **Global Burden**: Thought to be substantial post-tuberculosis, important due to potential response to antifungal agents.
#### Diagnosis
- **Suggested Diagnosis**: Based on radiologic and clinical features.
### Otomycosis
- **Definition**: Condition of superficial colonization typically due to [[Aspergillus niger]].
- **Clinical Features**: Similar to other causes of external otitis; mold growing on cerumen and desquamated epithelial debris in the external auditory canal.
- **Appearance**:
  - [[Aspergillus fumigatus]]: Greenish appearance.
  - [[Aspergillus niger]] Forms a black tuft.
- **Treatment**: Focuses on underlying chronic otitis externa and debridement rather than the fungus.
- **Immunocompromised Patients**: Invasive otitis externa resembles that due to Pseudomonas aeruginosa and may respond to antifungal therapy.
### Onychomycosis
- **Definition**: Superficial condition due to Aspergillus, which can become chronic and responds poorly to antifungal agents.
- **Antifungal Agents**: Often have a limited spectrum of activity to yeasts in nail infections.
- **Nail Culture**: Useful in patients with nonresponsive disease to establish specific fungal etiology for appropriate therapy.
### Keratitis
- **Occurrence**: Aspergillus is an occasional cause of keratitis, particularly after trauma or corneal surgery.
- **Diagnosis**: Established with smears demonstrating hyphae; culture results usually positive and confirm diagnosis.
- **Therapy**:
  - **Topical Antifungal Agents**: Usually topical natamycin 5% or topical voriconazole; efficacy studies limited.
  - **Surgical Intervention**: May be required for deep lesions or those nonresponsive to medical therapy.
  - **Amphotericin B**: Injected intracamerally when corneal penetration has occurred.
### Invasive Pulmonary Aspergillosis (IPA)
#### IPA
- **Manifestation**: The most common manifestation of invasive aspergillosis (IA) is invasive pulmonary aspergillosis (IPA).
- **Neutropenia**: IPA rarely manifests before 10 to 12 days of profound neutropenia, a major risk factor.
- **Incubation Period**: Estimated at approximately 15 days in patients with neutropenia.
- **Community-acquired Exposure**: Significant number of patients show manifestations of IPA on admission or within the first 2 weeks, suggesting common community-acquired exposure.
#### Symptoms and Clinical Features
- **Symptoms**: Progressive cough, dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain, fever despite broad-spectrum antibiotics, and pulmonary infiltrates.
- **Reduced Symptoms**: Symptoms may be reduced in patients with profound neutropenia unable to mount an inflammatory response.
- **Fever**: Common but may be absent in patients receiving high doses of corticosteroids.
- **Additional Clinical Features**: Hemoptysis, pleural effusion, pneumothorax.
- **Physical Findings**: Nonspecific and may lag behind the disease process.
- **Pulmonary Embolism Resemblance**: Symptoms may resemble a pulmonary embolism due to the angioinvasive nature.
- **Laboratory Studies**: Nonspecific but may include elevation in bilirubin, lactate dehydrogenase, C-reactive protein, and coagulation abnormalities.
- **Life-threatening Hypoxia**: Possible in patients with extensive or progressive infection.
#### Radiographic Findings
- **Diffuse Nodular Pulmonary Infiltrates**: Readily seen in extensive infection on chest radiographs or chest CT; not diagnostic, but dense nodular lesions associated with poorer prognosis.
- **Pleural-based Densities or Cavitary Lesions**: Classic findings, but pleural-based densities are not commonly detected, and cavitary lesions are present late in the course of infection.
- **Pleural Effusions**: More common than previously considered; specific manifestation of IPA not established.
- **“Halo” Sign**: Early finding in IA; a halo of low attenuation surrounding a nodular lesion on CT.
- **“Air-crescent” Sign**: Nodular lesions may cavitate later, usually with neutrophil recovery.
- **Similar Findings**: Radiographic features can also occur with other angioinvasive organisms like Mucorales, Fusarium, and Scedosporium, as well as bacterial pathogens.
### Tracheobronchitis
- **Aspergillus in the Airways**:
  - Can range from colonization, common in lung transplantation, to ulcerative tracheobronchitis.
- **Aspergillus Tracheobronchitis**:
  - Typically occurs in lung transplant patients and patients with AIDS.
  - Characterized by extensive pseudomembranous or ulcerative lesions due to Aspergillus.
  - In lung transplant patients, infection often occurs at the suture line and can lead to dehiscence of the anastomotic site.
- **Symptoms of Tracheobronchitis**:
  - Nonspecific symptoms include dyspnea with pulmonary function abnormalities, cough, chest pain, fever, or hemoptysis.
  - Symptoms may be mild and can be confused with other causes, including rejection.
- **Diagnosis**:
  - Plain radiographs may be normal, so clinical suspicion is needed.
  - Diagnosis is accomplished by bronchoscopy with biopsy to document tissue invasion.
### Disseminated infection
- Progressive invasive pulmonary aspergillosis often results in disseminated IA, a complication associated with an **extremely high mortality**.
- In patients with severe and ongoing immunosuppression, disseminated infection can occur with mortality rates historically >90%. 
- Even with advances in antifungal therapy, mortality rates in disseminated disease occurred in approximately 50% in the TRANSNET study.

## Diagnosis
- **Diagnosis of IA**:
  - Requires a tissue biopsy showing invasion with hyphae and a positive culture for Aspergillus.
  - Can also be established with positive cultures from a normally sterile site (e.g., needle biopsy, CSF); blood cultures are rarely positive.
  - Aspergillus hyphae are hyaline, septate, acute-angle branched, even diameter (3-6 µm in width).
  - Features distinguish Aspergillus from mucormycosis but not from other opportunistic molds (e.g., Fusarium, Lomentospora, Paecilomyces).
- **Respiratory Samples**:
  - Aspergillus cultures in high-risk patients (via BAL) can support probable IA diagnosis.
  - Positive cultures in low-risk patients should be interpreted with caution.
- **Radiographic Findings**:
  - Plain chest radiographs are insensitive and nonspecific.
  - “Halo” sign on CT is an early marker for initiating antifungal therapy.
  - Radiologic progression should be interpreted cautiously; CT findings validated in high-risk groups but not well defined in other patients.
- **Non-culture-based Methods**:
  - Antibody detection is of limited utility.
  - Galactomannan (GM) enzyme immunoassay is valuable for diagnosing probable IA; sensitivity and specificity vary based on factors like prior antifungal therapy.
  - False positives reported in some children and patients on certain antibiotics.
  - GM detection in BAL fluid demonstrates greater sensitivity than serum testing.
  - Serial GM assessments may offer prognostic value.
  - (1→3)-β-d-glucan is another potential marker.
- **Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)**:
  - Shows promise as an early diagnostic marker for IA; commercially available and standardized.
  - Specificity of BAL PCR greater than GM testing.
- **Susceptibility Testing**:
  - Standardized for Aspergillus, but clinical response correlation not well established.
  - Antifungal resistance (e.g., to itraconazole) linked to cyp51A gene mutations.
  - Resistance associated with agricultural triazole fungicides; TR34/L98H mutation causes multi-azole resistance.
  - Other resistance mechanisms (e.g., TR46/Y121F/T289A) reported.
  - Resistance variability: some mutations cause resistance to specific triazoles or panazole resistance.
  - Testing for species like Aspergillus terreus and cryptic Aspergillus spp. may be warranted.
# Epidemiologi
- **Affected Population**:
  - Immunocompromised individuals: Patients with AIDS, hematologic malignancies, prolonged neutropenia, long-term corticosteroids, and transplant recipients on anti-rejection medications.
- **Incidence in Transplants**:
  - Hematopoietic stem-cell transplant (HSCT) incidence: 0.5% after autologous transplant, up to 3.9% after unrelated donor transplant.
- **Critically Ill Patients**:
  - Seen in intensive care patients with underlying pulmonary conditions like chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) or asthma.
- **Global Cases**:
  - Approximately 250,000 cases of invasive aspergillosis occur globally each year.
- **Recent Affected Groups**:
  - Severe influenza and severe COVID-19 patients.
- **Rising Incidence**:
  - 44% increase in hospitalized patients with invasive aspergillosis between 2004 and 2013.
  - Increased incidence linked to greater accessibility and utilization of transplantation.
- **Chronic Aspergillosis Risk**:
  - Patients with underlying lung diseases (e.g., COPD, tuberculosis, asthma, lung cancer, sarcoidosis).
- **ABPA**:
  - Almost exclusively found in asthma and cystic fibrosis patients.
- **Occupational Risk**:
  - Workers in construction, farming, and wastewater treatment may have increased risk due to chronic exposure.
  - Smoking marijuana contaminated with the fungus poses a risk.
- **Nosocomial Infections**:
  - Reported from hospital showers and healthcare facilities undergoing construction.
# Pencegahan
### Prevention Measures
- **Challenges**: Prevention of IA in high-risk patients is difficult.
- **Nosocomial Outbreaks**: Linked to construction, contaminated ventilation systems, and possibly contaminated water.
- **Hospital Setting**: High-efficiency particulate air filters, frequent air exchanges, and positive pressure ventilation recommended for high-risk patients (e.g., HSCT).
- **Infection Control**: Construction barriers to limit aerosol exposure; routine maintenance and cleaning of showers and water systems.
- **Community-acquired Infection**: Common due to the increasing number of immunosuppressed patients receiving care outside the hospital setting.
### Antifungal Prophylaxis
- **Efficacy**: Limited due to the toxicity of amphotericin B and the limited activity of other oral agents against Aspergillus.
- **Agents Evaluated**: 
  - Low-dose amphotericin B
  - Low doses of lipid formulations of amphotericin B
  - Nasal and aerosolized forms of amphotericin B
- **Itraconazole**: Suggested benefit but poor tolerance in high-risk patients limits its use; newer formulation may be useful.
### Posaconazole Prophylaxis
- **HSCT with GVHD**: Posaconazole significantly reduced breakthrough fungal infections, including IA.
- **Acute Myelogenous Leukemia/Myelodysplastic Syndrome**: Reduced breakthrough fungal infections and significantly prolonged survival.
- **Adverse Drug Events**: More serious with posaconazole therapy; risk-benefit analysis recommended.
- **Guidelines**: Recommend posaconazole prophylaxis in high-risk patients (acute myelogenous leukemia or GVHD).
### Voriconazole Prophylaxis
- **High-risk Patients**: Anti-mold active prophylaxis should be considered.
- **Lower-risk Patients**: Careful monitoring and preemptive therapy may be appropriate.
