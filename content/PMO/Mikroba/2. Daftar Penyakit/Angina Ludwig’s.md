# Definisi Singkat
Ludwig's angina is a condition characterized by rapidly spreading infection of the sublingual space initially and later the submaxillary space and is often dentogenic in origin. Unlike other neck space infection where the abscess formation is the rule, here in Ludwig's angina, it has characteristic cellulitis without lymphatic involvement causing massive swelling of the tongue and floor of the mouth. Unless early surgical intervention is done the disease can be fatal due to respiratory obstruction.

# SS
- **Difficulty in Swallowing (Odynophagia)**:
  - Varying degrees of trismus (difficulty in opening the mouth).
- **Swelling and Induration**:
  - **Localized to Sublingual Space**:
    - Structures in the floor of the mouth are swollen.
    - Tongue seems to be pushed up and back.
  - **Spread to Submaxillary Space**:
    - Submental and submandibular regions become swollen, tender, and impart a woody-hard feel.
  - **Submental and Submandibular Swelling**:
    - Presents with increased fullness in the submental area and a loss of mandibular angle definition.
  - **Swelling of the Floor of the Mouth**:
    - Elevation of the tongue, causing it to be pushed upwards and backwards, threatening the airway.
- **Clinical Features**:
  - **Patient Appearance**:
    - Patient looks toxic, with high fever and malaise.
    - Disease is rapidly progressive and presents with painful neck swelling below the mandible.
  - **Respiratory Involvement**:
    - Signs include tripod positioning, drooling, and dysphagia.
  - **Other Symptoms**:
    - Mouth pain, hoarse voice, tongue swelling, stiff neck, chills, and weakness.
- History of recent tooth extraction or trauma.
- Most commonly from second or third lower molar infections.
- **Signs of Sepsis**:
  - Fever, rapid pulse, and toxic appearance.
- **Cellulitis and Edema**:
  - Rapidly increasing cellulitis with induration and erythema below the mandible, obliterating the mandibular line.
  - **Baruny Edema**:
    - Edema of the floor of the mouth and tongue, pushing the tongue posteriorly.
  - **Laryngeal Edema**:
    - Forces the patient to sit up and lean forwards, possibly causing dyspnea and stridor.
- **Other Signs**:
  - Absence of lymphadenitis.
  - Drooling of saliva.
  - Trismus indicating more advanced disease.
  - Tenderness of the involved teeth.
  - Induration of the submental neck and edema in the upper part of the neck.
# Etiologi
>Hazarik
- More common in the age 20 to 50 years
- Dental caries especially that of the lower 2nd and 3rd molars are the main source of the infection. Infection from the apex of the tooth can directly involve the submaxillary space.
- Infection following trauma of the tongue, floor of the mouth.
- Lingual tonsillitis
- Infection following dental extraction
- Post-radiotherapy osteoradionecrosis of the mandible.
>Dhingra
1. dental infections. They account for 80% of the cases. Roots of premolars often lie above the attachment of mylohyoid and cause sublingual space infection while roots of the molar teeth extend up to or below the mylohyoid line and primarily cause submaxillary space infection.
2. submandibular sialadenitis, injuries of oralmucosa and Fractures oF the mandible account for other cases.
## Bacteriology
Mixed infections involving both aerobes and anaerobes are common in Ludwig's angina. The disease is typically polymicrobial and includes oral flora such as Alpha-haemolytic Streptococci, Staphylococci, Peptostreptococcus, Fusobacterium, Bacteroides, and Actinomyces. Less commonly, Haemophilus influenzae, Escherichia coli, and Pseudomonas are seen. Infection with Streptococcus anginosus can progress the disease more rapidly than other bacteria. Additionally, more than half of patients with Ludwig's angina and diabetes present with Klebsiella pneumoniae. Patients with diabetes, those on hemodialysis, and those recently hospitalized (within a year) are at increased risk of methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) infection.
# Diagnosis
 - It is based mainly on classical clinical presentation
- Dental X-rays to assess dental status
- CT scan to know the extent of disease, extension to other neck spaces and the patency of the airway.
## DD
- Submental space infection
- Acute submandibular sialadenitis
- Acute cervical lymphadenitis
- Infected plunging ranula
- Infection of a necrotic tumor mass
- Tumors/granulomas of submandibular gland. 
# Tatalaksana
>Hazarika
- Intravenous antibiotics, fluids and analgesics
- Serratiopeptidase can be given along with anti­ inflammatory analgesics.
- Tracheostomy if stridor is present or in case of threatened airway obstruction.
- Surgical drainage is done via horizontal incision 2 finger bread th below the mandibular margin from one angle of the mandible to the other over the area of induration. Pus is often not seen as seen in other abscess rather edematous fluid collection may be drained. The incision provides decompression as this allows fluid from edematous tissue to drain. Drainage tube or antibiotic soaked ribbon gauge is placed and the incision is not closed. Daily dressing is done and the wound is allowed to heal by secondary intention 
- Treatment of underlying cause like dental sepsis, diabetes, if any.
>Dhingar
1. Systemic antibiotics.
2. Incision and drainage of abscess. 
	1. Intraoral—if infection is still localized to sublingual space.
	2. External—if infection involves submaxillary space. A transverse incision extending from one angle of mandible to the other is made with vertical open- ing of midline musculature of tongue with a blunt haemostat. Very often it is serous fluid rather than frank pus that is encountered.
3. Tracheostomy, if airway is endangered.
# Komplikasi
1. Spread of infection to parapharyngeal and retropharyngeal spaces and thence to the mediastinum.
2. Airway obstruction due to laryngeal oedema, or swelling and pushing back of the tongue.
3. Septicaemia.
4. Aspiration pneumonia
5. Airway obstruction requiring tracheostomy which may be difficult
6. Aspiration pneumonia
7. Lung abscess formation
8. Fluid and electrolyte imbalance
9. Tongue necrosis
10. Spread to other spaces like parapharyngeal space, retropharyngeal space, mediastinitis, etc.
